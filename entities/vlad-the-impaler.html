Vlad III Drăculea — Vlad the Impaler (Kazıklı Voyvoda)

Vlad III, known as Vlad Drăculea (Vlad the Dragon’s Son) and universally called Vlad the Impaler (Kazıklı Voyvoda, قازیقلی وایووده, “the Staked Voivode”), was the voyvoda (وایووده, prince) of Wallachia whose conflict with Mehmed II in 1462 produced one of the defining military confrontations of the Conqueror’s reign. Both Tursun Beg and Kritobulos devote extended passages to his rebellion, the Ottoman campaign against him, and his spectacular — though ultimately doomed — defiance of the sultan.

Background and Rise to Power

Vlad was the son of Vlad II Dracul, a voyvoda who had been invested by the Holy Roman Emperor Sigismund. The young Vlad and his younger brother Radu were sent as hostages to the Ottoman court after their father accepted Ottoman suzerainty — a common practice for securing the loyalty of vassal princes. Kritobulos records that when John Hunyadi marched against their father and killed him, the two brothers fled to Sultan Murad II, who “received them, and, while they were still children, had them raised with great honor in the imperial palace, and at his death left them to his son the Emperor.” Mehmed II continued this arrangement, raising the brothers “beside himself with great humanity, honor, and imperial provision.” When the situation in Wallachia shifted, Mehmed intervened and installed Vlad “with a great army and at great expense, delivering to him the entire principality of the Getae, and binding him by agreement and oath.”

Character and Methods of Rule

Tursun Beg introduces Vlad with unmistakable hostility: “The Impaler Voivode was the governor of the Wallachian province, a blood-drinking monstrous infidel.” He compares Vlad to Hajjaj ibn Yusuf, the notoriously brutal Umayyad governor of Iraq, writing: “His tyranny and cruelty were at an extremely wicked level.” The scale of Vlad’s impaling was legendary even in its own time. Tursun Beg describes the voivode’s seat at Ağaçhisarı: “The area six miles across from it, he enclosed with a double-armed fence and, saying ‘I will make a garden,’ covered it with thick brush. Between these two fences, he filled it with impaled people — from Hungarian infidels, from the infidels of his own province, and from the infidels of the Moldavian province. Moreover, the location of his own castle is also a wooded and fenced place. On every branch of every tree there are countless corpses strung up.” So absolute was this law of terror that “whoever took down one of the hanged would immediately be put up in its place.”

Despite this savagery from the Ottoman perspective, Tursun Beg acknowledges that Vlad had been militarily effective: “Despite being so evil-doing, he had defeated the Hungarian folk, broken them severely, and also made other peers and equals bleed, achieving superiority.” This success bred pride, and Vlad began to consider rebellion.

The Breach with the Devlet (دولت, the Ottoman State)

The immediate catalyst for conflict was Vlad’s refusal to pay the annual haraç (خراج, tribute). While Mehmed II was on campaign against Trebizond in 1461, Vlad judged the opportunity ripe. Tursun Beg writes: “Upon returning from the Trabzon campaign, the accumulated tribute from the infidel for that year was demanded. His answer was: ‘This year I have been very busy with my surroundings and have paid considerable tribute to Hungary. Therefore I have no means to give the tribute to the Ottomans.’ So he neither came nor paid his tribute.”

But the defiance went far beyond words. Kritobulos records that Vlad “first, secretly crossing the Danube with no small force, he raided the Emperor’s neighboring province, namely the region of Nicopolis and Vidin, and after collecting vast booty and committing no small slaughter, recrossed and returned to his own country.” Mehmed, still seeking a diplomatic resolution, sent envoys to Vlad “to remind him of the treaty, to confer peacefully with him, and to inquire into the reasons for his defection.” The response was a direct challenge: Vlad “seized them and had them impaled, also threatening the Emperor with many curses.”

The Ottoman Campaign of 1462

Mehmed crossed the Danube with a vast army — Kritobulos records that he “overran nearly the entire country of the Getae, ravaging, devastating, overturning and scattering everything as he encountered it like a storm, seizing the fortresses, plundering the villages, and carrying off countless booty.” The gaziler (غازی لر, holy warriors) advanced under brutal conditions: Tursun records that “the heat was at such a level that one could roast kebab on the steel armor worn by the ghazis.”

Vlad had deployed strategic defenses, placing his beylerbeyi (بكلربكى, governor-general) with seven thousand select troops to protect his country from the direction of Moldavia. The lack of water drove the Ottoman army forward directly onto these positions. What followed was a decisive Ottoman victory: Mahmud Pasha arranged the regiments in perfect order, and when the Wallachians emerged from the forest they saw the formations and “the fire of terror fell into his heart.” The gaziler pursued, “slicing them like slicing cucumbers with the sword.” Of seven thousand Wallachians, only seven hundred remained. For approximately thirty days the Ottoman army surged through Wallachia, taking “much spoil, property, and many captives.”

The Night Attack

As the army withdrew to rest, Vlad — now cornered and desperate — launched his most famous tactical stroke: a night raid on the Ottoman camp. Tursun Beg describes him “like a moth circling around the camp.” Kritobulos provides the psychological portrait of a man with nothing left to lose: “Drakul, now utterly despairing of his cause and having no further hope of ruling, resolved upon death, that he might perish himself while doing some harm to his enemies.”

Tursun Beg’s account is the more dramatic: “One night, he fell upon the imperial army like a drop falling into the sea. First he struck the Anadolu askeri (Anatolian troops). He received a slap befitting his state and was scattered. Those who could flee fell upon the wrath of the Gate-people and the Janissaries. Here too they found no victory and fell, wounded and broken.” The decisive factor was confusion in the darkness: “But in the darkness of night, mistaking the place where the army crowd was for a path of escape, they marched there. This confusion became their destruction, for it brought them and dropped them among the Rumelian troops. In every tent, candles and lamps were burning. Here and there, heroic guard sentries waiting motionless shouted war cries. The infidels, stunned, became like water flowing in a rocky stream. They lost one another and were scattered.”

Kritobulos adds that Mehmed had anticipated the attack and ordered his soldiers to withdraw to trap Vlad. The wounded Vlad escaped with a few survivors.

Flight to Hungary, Imprisonment, and Death

Both sources agree on the aftermath. Tursun Beg records that Vlad’s forces were divided into three groups: “One group’s heads were separated from their bodies… one group fell captive… the third group were those who escaped from the field. They too went out with their commander and fled to Hungary.” Kritobulos writes that the Hungarians “seized him and cast him into prison.” Tursun Beg adds a note of grim irony: “He had previously done much harm to Hungary. He went of his own foot and entered his own trap.” Vlad was eventually released and briefly restored to the Wallachian throne in 1476 but was killed in battle later that year.

Radu the Fair Installed

With Vlad eliminated, Mehmed installed his brother Radu (called “Radu the Fair” or “Radu cel Frumos”) as the Ottoman vassal voyvoda of Wallachia. Tursun Beg writes: “His Majesty the Padishah gave the administration of this country to the previous Voivode Vlad’s brother, Radu Voivode, whose fortune was accepted and who spent many years of his life in the service of the state. Because of the excellence of his service, he was also granted a banner, a sword, and a belt” — the traditional Ottoman insignia of vassalage.

Legacy

Vlad the Impaler’s rebellion of 1462 was the most serious challenge to Ottoman authority in the Danubian principalities during Mehmed II’s reign. His use of terror as a weapon made him a figure of lasting infamy in Ottoman historiography. In Ottoman chronicles he is remembered as Kazıklı Voyvoda, the Staked Voivode, his name forever linked to the punishment he inflicted on others. Tursun Beg’s judgment is characteristically blunt: the Impaler Voivode was a warning of what befell those who broke faith with the sultan. Kritobulos, writing in a more classical mode, treats Vlad as a cautionary example of ingratitude and reckless ambition — a prince raised by imperial favor who repaid that favor with treachery, and who paid for it with his throne and his freedom.
