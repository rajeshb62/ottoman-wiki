Barbarossa Hayreddin Pasha — Khayr al-Din Barbarossa (c. 1478–1546)

Barbarossa Hayreddin Pasha (c. 1478–1546), known in the West as Barbarossa (“Redbeard”) and in Turkish as Hayreddin Barbaros or Barbaros Hayreddin Paşa, was the greatest Ottoman admiral of the classical age. Born on the island of Lesbos (Midilli), he began his career as a Barbary corsair in the western Mediterranean before becoming the conqueror of Algiers and, ultimately, the kapudan-i deryâ (grand admiral) of the Ottoman navy under Sultan Suleiman I. Halil Inalcik’s account places Barbarossa at the center of the Ottoman naval revolution that transformed the empire into a Mediterranean superpower, culminating in his decisive victory over the Holy League at the Battle of Preveza in 1538.

Origins and the Barbary Corsair Years

Barbarossa was born on the island of Midilli (Lesbos), then under Ottoman rule, around 1478. His father, Yakup Ağa, was a former Ottoman sipahi of Albanian or Greek origin who had become a potter. Together with his elder brother Oruç Reis, Hayreddin took up privateering in the Mediterranean, operating from bases in the western Aegean and later the North African coast.

The Barbarossa brothers established themselves as the leading corsair captains of the Barbary Coast, raiding Christian shipping and coastal settlements. In 1516, Oruç and Hayreddin captured Algiers from the Spanish, expelling the Spanish garrison that had occupied the offshore Peñón fort. Oruç declared himself Sultan of Algiers and sought Ottoman protection, but was killed in 1518 during a Spanish expedition against Tlemcen. Hayreddin succeeded his brother as the leader of Algiers and as the foremost Muslim naval commander in the western Mediterranean.

Inalcik describes Hayreddin as the “famous Turkish corsair and conqueror of Algiers,” a figure whose reputation was already legendary before he entered Ottoman service. Recognizing the growing threat from Charles V and the Spanish-Habsburg naval buildup in the Mediterranean, Hayreddin formally submitted to Ottoman suzerainty in 1519, placing Algiers under the sultan’s protection. This act integrated the Barbary corsair fleet into the Ottoman imperial system — a step that would transform the balance of naval power in the Mediterranean.

Appointment as Kapudan-i Deryâ

The strategic context that brought Barbarossa to supreme command was the Habsburg-Ottoman rivalry of the 1530s. In 1532, while Suleiman was campaigning against the Habsburgs in Hungary, the Genoese admiral Andrea Doria — serving Charles V — captured Coron in the Morea from the Ottomans. Inalcik records that Suleiman, “realizing that he now had to open a second front in the Mediterranean, placed all Ottoman naval forces under the command of the famous Turkish corsair and conqueror of Algiers, Hayreddin Barbarossa, appointing him kapudan-i deryâ — grand admiral.”

This appointment in 1533 was a transformative moment for the Ottoman navy. Barbarossa brought with him the expertise, the crews, and the aggressive tactical doctrine of the Barbary corsairs — men who had spent their lives in galley warfare against the Spanish, the Knights of St John, and Italian maritime states. Inalcik notes that 1533 also saw the creation of the beylerbeyilik (province) of Algiers, conferred upon Barbarossa “in an attempt to unify all naval forces against Charles V.” As kapudan-i deryâ, Barbarossa united under his administration Algiers, which he himself had conquered, and thirteen sanjaks on the shores and islands of the Mediterranean, creating a unified naval command stretching across the Ottoman Mediterranean littoral.

Organization of the Ottoman Navy

Barbarossa’s achievement as grand admiral went beyond battlefield victories. He reorganized the Ottoman navy into a permanent, centrally-coordinated force capable of sustained campaigns far from home ports. Before his tenure, the Ottoman fleet had been largely a coastal defense force, assembled for specific campaigns and then dispersed. Under Barbarossa, the Imperial Arsenal (Tersane-i Amire) in the Golden Horn became a permanent shipbuilding and logistical hub, and the Mediterranean fleet was structured as a standing force.

Inalcik records that from the second half of the sixteenth century — a direct institutional legacy of Barbarossa’s reorganization — the kapudân-i deryâ presided over a separate council which dealt with naval matters and lawsuits arising in the fleet, and made nominations, appointments and dismissals. This gave the admiralty an institutional independence it had not previously possessed. The grand vizier retained the right to visit and inspect the Arsenal, but operational naval command was firmly in the hands of the kapudan pasha.

The thirteen Mediterranean sanjaks under Barbarossa’s administration — ranging from the coasts of the Morea and the Aegean islands to the North African littoral — supplied ships, crews, timber, and naval stores to the imperial fleet. This administrative unification was the foundation upon which Ottoman naval supremacy in the eastern and central Mediterranean was built.

The Franco-Ottoman Alliance and Mediterranean Campaigns

Barbarossa’s appointment coincided with the deepening of the Franco-Ottoman alliance. Since 1531, Inalcik records, the French had been “trying to persuade the sultan to attack Italy,” and now they sought a formal alliance. In 1536 this alliance was concluded, with Suleiman ready to grant the French freedom of trade within the empire. The alliance was secret in its political and military details, kept secret by both parties, and provided Francis I with a powerful counterweight to Charles V.

In 1537, Suleiman brought his army to Valona in Albania and besieged Venetian ports in Albania and the island of Corfu, where a French fleet assisted the Ottomans. In the Mediterranean theater, Charles V had captured Tunis from the Ottomans in 1535, but this setback was about to be reversed.

The Battle of Preveza (1538)

The climactic naval engagement of Barbarossa’s career came on 28 September 1538 at Preveza, off the coast of Epirus in western Greece. Pope Paul III had organized a Holy League — the Sacra Lega — uniting the naval forces of Spain, Venice, Genoa, the Papal States, and the Knights of Malta under the overall command of Andrea Doria. The League assembled a fleet of some 300 vessels, outnumbering the Ottoman fleet commanded by Barbarossa.

Inalcik’s account is characteristically concise and definitive: “Barbarossa defeated a crusader fleet under the command of Andrea Doria at Preveza, leaving him undisputed master of the Mediterranean.”

The victory at Preveza cemented Ottoman naval dominance in the Mediterranean for the next three decades. Barbarossa’s tactical brilliance — exploiting his galleys’ maneuverability, using the lee of the coast to negate the League’s numerical advantage, and forcing Doria into an indecisive engagement that turned into a rout — became legendary. After Preveza, no Christian fleet dared challenge Ottoman naval superiority in the eastern Mediterranean until Lepanto in 1571.

The Conquest of Tunis and the Nice Campaign

Despite Charles V’s capture of Tunis in 1535, Barbarossa had previously conquered Tunis in August 1533, the same year he became grand admiral. The city remained contested terrain between the Ottomans and the Spanish-Habsburgs throughout the 1530s.

In 1543, when Suleiman again marched into Hungary, he “sent a fleet of 110 galleys, under the command of Barbarossa, to assist Francis.” The Franco-Ottoman fleet besieged Nice, then under the control of Charles V’s ally the Duke of Savoy, and the Ottoman fleet wintered in the French port of Toulon — a deeply controversial arrangement that allowed Christian Europe to see Ottoman sailors and Muslim worship on French soil. In return, a small French artillery unit joined the Ottoman army in Hungary. Inalcik notes, however, that “this cooperation, however, was not particularly effective.”

Death and Legacy

Barbarossa Hayreddin Pasha died in 1546 in Istanbul, where his tomb stands to this day in the Beşiktaş district, near the shore from which his fleet sailed. He was succeeded by his lieutenant and protege, Turgut Reis (Dragut), who continued the tradition of Barbary corsair-admirals in Ottoman service.

Inalcik’s narrative makes clear that Barbarossa’s legacy was twofold. First, his victory at Preveza established a generation of Ottoman naval supremacy that made possible the empire’s projection of power across the Mediterranean, from the Adriatic to the coast of Spain. Second, his institutional reforms — the unification of the Mediterranean naval command under a single admiral, the creation of the Algiers beylerbeyilik, and the establishment of the kapudan pasha’s independent naval council — created an administrative structure that outlasted him.

The decline of Ottoman naval power after the 1590s, which Inalcik analyzes in detail — the failure to adopt the new ocean-going sailing ships that replaced galleys, the inability to face combined Christian fleets, the loss of control over the North African corsair provinces — only underscores by contrast the magnitude of Barbarossa’s achievement. Under his command, the Ottoman navy reached a peak of effectiveness that was never again attained. In the broader arc of Inalcik’s account, Barbarossa stands as the figure who transformed the Ottoman Empire from a land power with a supplementary navy into a true Mediterranean naval power, capable of contesting and defeating the combined fleets of Christendom.
