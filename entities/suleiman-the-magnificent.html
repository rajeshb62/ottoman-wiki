Suleiman I — Kanuni Sultan Süleyman (the Magnificent / the Legislator)

Suleiman I (1494–1566), known to his own people as Kanuni (قانونى, “the Legislator”) and to Europeans as “the Magnificent,” was the tenth and longest-reigning Ottoman sultan, governing from 1520 until his death in 1566. His rule marked the apogee of Ottoman power, territorial extent, and institutional maturity. The Harvard historian Albert Howe Lybyer, whose 1913 study forms the core of this account, describes Suleiman’s empire as one whose power “nothing surpassed the manifestations of power that swept forth from Constantinople” amid the vast energies of the sixteenth century. His reign is the central case study for understanding the Ottoman Ruling Institution at its most developed and effective stage.

Accession and Early Reign

Suleiman succeeded his father, Selim I (Yavuz, “the Grim”), in 1520. He inherited an empire that had already expanded dramatically under Selim’s conquests of Egypt, Syria, and the Holy Cities of Mecca and Medina. He inherited as well the caliphal authority that Selim had consolidated, though the title itself was not commonly used by Suleiman in public documents and seems to have been unknown to Western writers of the sixteenth century.

His reign opened with two great triumphs that had eluded even Mehmed the Conqueror: the fortresses of Belgrade fell in 1521 and Rhodes in 1522. These victories announced a ruler as formidable as his most celebrated predecessors. The internal machinery of government he found in excellent order — the Ruling Institution, having been gathered closely about the sultans and constantly amended by them, needed no comprehensive codification. More remote matters, however — the feudal system, the bearing of taxation and land tenure on the subject population, criminal law, and the market regulations of the cities — had fallen into considerable disorder.

The Sultan as Head of State

Suleiman’s authority rested, Lybyer writes, “actually and immediately upon the military might which he controlled.” Psychologically, it was supported by the ancient Turkish tradition of absolute obedience to the ruler who led and fed his people, and by the undying allegiance of wide populations to the Caesar of New Rome, whose seat and splendor Suleiman had succeeded to. Theoretically and constitutionally, his power was that of the ancient caliphs of Islam.

In his capacity as caliph, Suleiman was head of the Islamic state, defender, executor, and interpreter of the Sacred Law, and defender of the faith. He was under obligation to punish heretics and unsubmissive infidels, to protect true believers, and to extend the area of his divinely-appointed rule. To him, after Allah and the Prophet, was due the absolute obedience of all good Moslems within his dominions. All the great institutions of the empire are to be thought of, Lybyer emphasizes, not as built upward from a basis in the popular will, but as extended downward from the divinely-appointed sovereign at the top.

“Suleiman’s authority rested actually and immediately upon the military might which he controlled.”

Suleiman was a devout Moslem whose piety increased in old age. He took seriously his duty of enforcing the provisions of the Sacred Law, not hesitating at unpopular measures like the prohibition of wine-drinking, or at those demanding self-sacrifice, such as the disapproval of musical instruments and silver plate. He had a fiery active period of youth when the liberal policy of the Ruling Institution was stronger in his mind, and a quieter old age when Moslem influence became predominant — it is not unlikely that a consciousness of his position as caliph grew upon him with advancing years.

Legislation and the Kanun-Nameh

Suleiman earned the name Kanuni not by producing a single comprehensive code, but by issuing a large number of Kanuns (written decrees of the sultan) that addressed the accumulated confusions of a rapidly grown empire. The law that demanded obedience within the Ottoman Empire was fourfold: the Sheri (Sacred Law of Islam), the Kanuns (written decrees of the sultans), the Adet (established custom), and the Urf (sovereign will of the reigning sultan). The Sheri was above the sultan and unchangeable by him; the Kanuns and the Adet were subordinate to the Urf; the Urf, when expressed and written, became Kanun and annulled all contradictory Kanuns and Adet.

The Multeka ol-Ebhar

The Sacred Law itself needed renovation. Mehmed II had commissioned a code from Khosrew Pasha in 1470, but by Suleiman’s day the Ottoman Empire had acquired Damascus, Bagdad, and Cairo — three seats of the later caliphate — and held the protectorate of the Holy Cities. A new code better adapted to the more widely Moslem character the empire had assumed was demanded. Suleiman charged Sheik Ibrahim Halebi (of Aleppo) with the task; the result, prepared before 1549, was the Multeka ol-ebhar, the “Confluence of the Seas,” which remained the foundation of Ottoman law until the reforms of the nineteenth century.

His Principal Kanuns

Suleiman’s own legislation dealt especially with timars (fiefs), rayahs (subject Christians), ceremonies, and criminal and market regulations. The most complete body of Kanuns he published was the Kanun-nameh Misr, a constitution for Egypt issued in 1532, probably inspired by his grand vizier Ibrahim following his visit to Egypt in 1524. The collection of the great Mufti Ebu Su’ud, which is called the Kanun-nameh of Suleiman, contains chiefly his ordinances in regard to land tenure and taxes of the subject Christians, together with laws to regulate the feudal system, and a few relating to judges and legal processes.

Limitations

Lybyer judges Suleiman “great as a legislator only by comparison with his predecessors.” His legislation was doubly hindered: by the conservatism of his people and his religion, which made radical departures practically impossible; and by the weakness inherent in despotic legislation, where the distance of the lawgiver from the subjects affected made true adaptation and complete enforcement impossible. Most of his laws professed an attempt to restore a former better state of affairs. Suleiman “set nothing in final order; and the ground had to be gone over again within fifty years after his death, in the reign of Achmet I.” The suspiciously great wealth of high officials like Ibrahim and Rustem, and the fact that fiefs and finances were in worse disorder than ever after no great time had elapsed, gives evidence that his laws were not faithfully enforced.

Nevertheless, he used severe measures to put down extortion, and strove by his market and police regulations to maintain justice, fairness, and order. If he did not enact measures directly to increase the welfare of the common people, his attempts to regulate the tax and tenancy systems tended to lighten their condition.

The Grand Vizierate

Under Suleiman, the office of grand vizier reached the climax of a noteworthy development. Whereas this official had formerly been the senior member of the sultan’s board of advisers — primus inter pares — he now became a personage far above his fellow-viziers. His position came to differ from theirs not merely in degree but in kind, a difference typified by the fact that in reporting to Suleiman after the meetings of the Divan, none spoke but the grand vizier. This development resulted, Lybyer argues, from Suleiman’s willingness to entrust much power to a chosen instrument who would thus relieve him of the immense cares of empire.

There were ordinarily four viziers in Suleiman’s reign, that being a sacred number with both Turks and Moslems. Seven grand viziers served him over forty-six years: Mohammed Piri Pasha (whom he found in office at his accession, serving six years in all) and Mohammed Sokolli (whom he left in office at his death, serving fifteen years). Thus in forty-six years there were only nine grand viziers in total. Three deserve to be called great: Ibrahim for his splendor, breadth of mind, and continuance in favor (thirteen years); Rustem for his financial shrewdness (fifteen years in two periods); and Mohammed Sokolli for his statesmanship (fifteen years without a break). All except Mohammed Piri Pasha were Christian renegades who had risen as slaves to the highest honor of the empire.

Ibrahim first held his master’s confidence for many years; Suleiman violated the rules of advancement in the government service to promote him. Later, Rustem came to full power, supported by Suleiman’s wife Roxelana (Hürrem Sultan) and his favorite daughter Mihrmah; for him Suleiman broke the rule of giving no high place to relatives of the imperial family. In Suleiman’s last years he left well-nigh everything to Ali and to Mohammed Sokolli.

The Divan

Suleiman did not preside at the Divan, the imperial council, and he has been greatly blamed for discontinuing the custom. Lybyer notes that “it is not impossible to sympathize with him” — spending several hours in deliberation on four days of each week during a lifetime was a prospect from which any man would shrink. Nevertheless, it was a serious rift in both great institutions of the empire at the most dangerous place, and its effect was decidedly to hasten their disintegration.

The Divan met four times each week throughout the year in time of peace: Saturday, Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday. Sessions lasted seven or eight hours. The grand vizier sat Turkish fashion in the middle of a long sofa extending round three sides of the hall. On his right sat the other viziers, with the Nishanji beyond them; on his left the two Kaziaskers, and beyond them the Defterdars. The Beylerbeys of Anatolia and Greece, the Kapudan Pasha, the Agha of the Janissaries, and the chief interpreter also had places. During the session, a simple meal of bread, meat, rice, fruit, and water was served to all in attendance.

Suleiman kept the Divan under control by means of a grated window in the wall of the room where it met. Not knowing when he might be listening there, his councillors had always to speak as if he were present with them. After the day’s work, those officials who had the right of audience went to the hall of audience to meet the sultan. The grand vizier alone reported on the decisions of the day, which the sultan usually approved as made, sometimes mitigating a decision or himself dictating a reply to an ambassador.

“The Divan … was a training-school of judges, administrators, and statesmen.”

Lybyer judges the Divan “a splendid ceremony” that transacted a great amount of administrative and judicial business. It was by no means a legislative chamber, but rather a combination of a president’s cabinet and a supreme court, unique in its character. It was “a training-school of judges, administrators, and statesmen, since men ordinarily rose from place to place among its offices as they gained experience.”

Finance and Taxation

Suleiman’s revenues have been variously estimated. Junis Bey, chief interpreter of the Ottoman court, and Alvise Gritti, business partner of the grand vizier Ibrahim, gave a total regular revenue of about four million ducats for the years between 1530 and 1537: 1,300,000 ducats from kharaj (tribute) from Anatolia and Greece; 1,600,000 from Egypt; 700,000 from Syria; 150,000 from Mesopotamia; and 250,000 from farms, islands, and customs of Constantinople and Pera.

Later estimates indicate growth. By the close of his reign, after large territories in Europe and Asia had been incorporated and after Rustem had made new arrangements, the total amount was probably seven or eight million ducats. Lybyer calculates the bullion value of six million ducats as less than fourteen million dollars, with purchasing power perhaps five times that of his era — equivalent to less than seventy million dollars for so great an empire, a remarkably small sum by modern standards.

This revenue, however, by no means covered all public expenses. It excluded the revenues devoted to the Moslem Institution, those specifically assigned by feudal grant to local government officers, and the income gathered by permanent fief-holders for their own support — the last probably amounted to about twice as much again. Allowing for everything, the equivalent of perhaps two hundred million dollars supported all central and local government. “Had there been no extortion,” Lybyer writes, “the people of the empire would not have been burdened heavily. Even with it … they probably did not suffer greatly in Suleiman’s time.”

The treasury department numbered more than eight hundred personnel organized into twenty-five bureaus or kalems, as instituted by Mehmed the Conqueror. These included bureaus for book-keeping, accounts of tithes and taxes, cavalry control, the capitation tax (jizyeh) on adult male subject Christians, accounts for the Holy Cities of Mecca and Medina, tributes paid in kind, grain stores for the border fortresses, and the chancery of the treasury department.

Court and Palace

Suleiman was known as the Legislator and the Conqueror, but beyond both these titles as the Magnificent. Lybyer writes that he “shone as head of the government and the army, but still more as head of the court.” His enormous establishment and his fondness for display and ceremony developed a similar spirit in all his kullar. Each officer of position became inordinately ambitious to have a large household, many horses, much portable wealth, and superb equipment on state occasions and in time of war.

This splendor ultimately proved ruinous. Suleiman’s own example embarrassed his finances to such an extent that he was willing that Rustem should require payment for office from newly-appointed great officials. Most of his kullar, in order to keep up display, were led to undignified and extortionate procedures. The pressure upon the subject population tended to become worse and worse. Lands began to grow less productive and to pass out of cultivation.

Yet in Suleiman’s time this weakness hardly appeared. Even after his best two sons had come to cruel deaths, and twenty thousand of his troops had died in vain at Malta, he went forth to his last campaign with a train which surpassed in pomp and splendor all that he had led before.

The Janissaries

The Janissaries in Suleiman’s time numbered between twelve and fourteen thousand. They were organized into one hundred and sixty-five ortas (regiments). Many were distributed in garrisons; probably not more than half resided in the capital. The Agha of the Janissaries was not himself a former Janissary but had come through the colleges of pages. He served as a sort of minister of war for them, directing enrolment, pay distribution, promotions, location, supplies, and clothing.

Suleiman succeeded, on the whole, in keeping the Janissaries in hand. They never revolted against him, and they supported him against his son Bayezid. He was able to lead them farther east than could his father Selim. Yet the Janissaries’ power was never far from the surface of politics. Busbecq, the Habsburg ambassador, relates how even the grand vizier Rustem, and Suleiman himself, felt toward these men when they were all together and their blood was hot. On one occasion Rustem sent word to Busbecq asking him to remove every cause of offense “which might occasion a quarrel with those atrocious scoundrels,” asking whether Busbecq was not aware “that it was war time, when they were masters, so that not even Solyman himself had control over them, and was actually himself afraid of receiving violence at their hands?”

“The Janissaries in Suleiman’s time numbered between twelve and fourteen thousand.”

Despite these tensions, the regular cavalry (the Spahis of the Porte) appear never to have caused Suleiman any special trouble. The Janissaries were constantly drained of their ablest men by promotion to higher posts, and could be repressed by punishment — now and then an especially active promoter of trouble was executed.

Military Campaigns

Suleiman’s reign saw the Ottoman army at its most formidable. The Ruling Institution, organized as an army, was victorious in battle throughout his reign. Belgrade (1521) and Rhodes (1522) — fortresses that had defied Mehmed the Conqueror — fell before his troops. At Mohács in 1526, the Hungarian kingdom was shattered. The siege of Vienna in 1529 failed, but in the thirty-five succeeding years he made large conquests, strengthened his power, and saw his prestige grow steadily.

His campaigns extended into Persia and Mesopotamia, and he died during the siege of Szigetvár in 1566, crushing the Hungarian frontier fortress in his last campaign. Lybyer records that in his final year he went forth “with a train which surpassed in pomp and splendor all that he had led before.”

The weakness of the Ottoman army as an institution was its essential indivisibility: only one great war could be waged at a time, although there were great enemies in two directions. An overwhelming defeat of the principal army would have been irreparably disastrous. But the army was to suffice for a long period, and its worst foes were to be internal rivalries and departures from its constitutive principles.

Family and Influence

Suleiman’s personal relationships had profound political consequences. His favorite wife, Roxelana (Hürrem Sultan), and his daughter Mihrmah both exerted significant influence. Lybyer notes that Roxelana “undoubtedly played some part” in the deaths of Suleiman’s son Mustapha and later of Roxelana’s own son Bayezid — victims of inexorable circumstances surrounding the succession. “In so far as she contributed to the fatal outcome, she hastened the fall of the empire.” Mihrmah, if tradition is correct, urged her father on to the great expedition against Malta — the expedition that broke his mailed fist upon a rocky isle and whose failure gave confidence to the Christian fleet that won at Lepanto.

In his late years Suleiman came greatly under the influence of the Ulema, who had readier access to him than did any other outside force. The influence of Roxelana and Mihrmah foreshadowed the power exerted in later reigns by far inferior and far worse women.

The Ruling Institution and Legacy

Lybyer’s central argument is that Suleiman’s government was, at its core, a vast slave-family — the Ruling Institution — which recruited its members almost entirely as Christian children, converted them to Islam, educated and trained them with the greatest care, and placed them in every position of military, administrative, and governmental authority. The men who guided Suleiman’s empire to its height were “sons of peasants and herdsmen, of downtrodden and miserable subjects, of unlettered and half-civilized men and women.” It is not easy to decide, Lybyer writes, “which is more to be admired, the ability by which such young men rose, or the confidence with which they were chosen and expected to rise.”

“Suleiman laid hold of many problems which had arisen, and through the agency of his ablest servants strove to set his house in order.”

Suleiman laid hold of many problems that had arisen and, through the agency of his ablest servants, strove to set his house in order. That he did not succeed in accomplishing more permanent results was due, Lybyer judges, to the fact that the task was too great for any man — the institution was too artificial to endure indefinitely.

After Suleiman the Magnificent, the Legislator, was to come Selim the Sot. The increase under Suleiman of the relative power of the grand vizier was a dangerous and eventually a disastrous development: it became easy for later sultans to withdraw into the harem, while the grand vizierate was left at the mercy of harem intrigue. The splendor and luxury of Suleiman’s example, while magnificent, was in the end to be ruinous.

Nevertheless, in the time of Suleiman the Ottoman government “seems to have attained, during the sixteenth century, the highest degree of perfection of which its constitution was capable” (Robertson, quoted by Lybyer). The empire took by siege Belgrade and Rhodes; it failed at Vienna and Malta; but for generations afterward its worst foes were not foreign armies but internal rivalries and departures from the principles that had built it.
