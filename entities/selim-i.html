Selim I — Yavuz Sultan Selim (Selim the Grim)

Selim I (1470–1520), known in Ottoman tradition as Yavuz (“the Grim” or “the Stern”), was the ninth sultan of the Ottoman dynasty, reigning from 1512 to 1520. The son of Bayezid II and the father of Suleiman the Magnificent, Selim transformed the Ottoman state in eight years from a frontier principality rooted in Anatolia and the Balkans into a world empire spanning the Middle East. His conquest of Syria, Egypt, and the Holy Cities of Mecca and Medina, together with his annexation of eastern Anatolia and his destruction of the Safavid army at Chaldiran, made the Ottoman Empire the dominant power of the Islamic world and brought the old heartland of the Abbasid caliphate under Ottoman rule. Halil Inalcik treats his reign as the decisive break between the “definitive establishment” of the Ottoman Empire (1453–1526) and its emergence as a world power — the period when the empire ceased to be a frontier gazi state and became, instead, an Islamic caliphate.

Early Life and Governorship

Selim was one of eight sons of Bayezid II, born to one of the sultan’s concubines. As Ottoman custom required, he was sent as a prince to a provincial governorship, serving in Trebizond on the eastern Black Sea coast. This posting placed him directly on the frontier with the emerging Safavid state of Shah Ismail, whose agents stirred up the Kizilbas (Red Head) heresy among the Turcoman tribes of Anatolia. From his position in Trebizond, Selim observed the Kizilbas threat firsthand and developed the implacable hostility toward Safavid Persia that would define his reign. He demanded strong action against Shah Ismail while his father, Bayezid II, pursued a cautious, conciliatory policy.

Rebellion against Bayezid II

In the last years of Bayezid II’s reign, the succession struggle among the Ottoman princes tore the empire apart. Bayezid’s mild administration had encouraged disaffected elements in Anatolia — Turcoman nomads, dispossessed sipahis, and heterodox dervish orders — to rebel against Ottoman authority. The Safavid agents of Shah Ismail fanned these fires, and in 1511 the Kizilbas rose in full revolt in the highlands of western Anatolia, marching on Bursa under a Safavid agent. It was clear that the aged and sick sultan could no longer control the situation.

Each of the princes sought to win the Janissaries to his cause with promises of increased salaries. Although the sultan and the grand vizier preferred Prince Ahmed, it was Selim who won the support of the Janissaries — men who admired his warlike character, which reminded them of Mehmed the Conqueror. In 1511 Selim took arms against his brothers, and the Janissaries sided decisively with him over Ahmed. On 24 April 1512 Selim forced his father to abdicate. Bayezid II died shortly afterward, possibly poisoned, on the road to his retirement in Dimetoka.

Elimination of Rivals

Selim I, one by one, eliminated his brothers who were rivals for the throne. He also executed his nephews and any other potential claimants, applying the Law of Fratricide codified by Mehmed the Conqueror with a thoroughness that earned him his grim reputation. Inalcik notes that these purges followed the established dynastic principle: to preserve the unity of the empire, the victorious prince removed all threats to his rule.

The Kizilbas Purges and the Campaign against Shah Ismail

Before confronting Shah Ismail directly, Selim conducted a sweeping purge of Safavid partisans within his own domains. He imprisoned and executed some forty thousand Kizilbas supporters in Anatolia who were suspected of allegiance to the Safavid shah. This was a religious as well as a political measure: Selim denounced Ismail as a Shiite heretic, presenting the coming war not merely as a struggle for territory but as a jihad against those who had corrupted Islam.

Shah Ismail, for his part, replied as Uzun Hasan had done before him — reminding Selim of the fate that had befallen Bayezid I at the hands of Timur, and threatening to invade Anatolia. The two armies met on the plain of Chaldiran (Caldiran), east of Lake Van, on 23 August 1514.

The Battle of Chaldiran

The battle was a decisive Ottoman victory. The Ottomans possessed overwhelming superiority in firearms — cannon and muskets — which Selim deployed to devastating effect against the Safavid cavalry. The Safavids, like the Mamluks, had been slow to adopt gunpowder weapons, and their traditional mounted archers could not stand against the Ottoman artillery and Janissary musket-fire. Shah Ismail himself was wounded and narrowly escaped capture, his camp and his harem falling into Ottoman hands.

Chaldiran was one of the most consequential battles of the early modern Middle East. It broke the Safavid threat to Ottoman Anatolia, established Ottoman military dominance in the region, and fixed the eastern border of the Ottoman Empire for centuries. The victory temporarily subdued the Kizilbas threat and allowed Selim to annex the mountainous region from Erzurum to Diyarbekir. In 1516–17, the local dynasties and tribal chieftains in this area recognized Ottoman suzerainty. Anatolia was now secure against invasion from the east, and the routes to Azerbaijan, the Caucasus, and Baghdad were open to the Ottomans.

The Conquest of the Dulkadir Principality

In the year following Chaldiran, Selim invaded the buffer principality of Dulkadir (Dulkadiroglu) in southeastern Anatolia, a vassal state of the Mamluk sultanate. This act made a conflict with the Mamluks inevitable. The Mamluk rulers, a military caste of Turkish and Circassian slaves who had ruled Egypt and Syria for two and a half centuries, had long contested Ottoman influence over the Anatolian frontier principalities. The rivalry had simmered since Mehmed the Conqueror’s annexation of Karaman in 1468, and Bayezid II had fought inconclusive campaigns against the Mamluks between 1485 and 1491. Selim’s annexation of Dulkadir broke the buffer and brought the two powers to war.

The Conquest of Syria and Egypt (1516–1517)

By 1516 the entire Arab world, alarmed by Portuguese attacks in the Red Sea and Indian Ocean, had begun to fix its hopes on the Ottoman sultan as the great gazi ruler who could defend Islam. In that year, the Sherif of Mecca — a descendant of the Prophet Muhammad — proposed to send a delegation to Selim. The Mamluk sultan, al-Ghawri, prevented it; but the Arab lands were increasingly prepared to accept Ottoman rule. When Selim marched against the Mamluks, he announced to the Arabs that he came to free them from the Mamluk yoke and to protect the Islamic world.

Selim marched first against Aleppo. The city’s governor and people deserted to the Ottomans, and on 24 August 1516 — exactly two years after Chaldiran — Selim destroyed al-Ghawri’s army at Marj Dabiq, north of Aleppo. The Mamluk sultan died on the battlefield. In the great mosque of Aleppo, in the presence of the Abbasid caliph al-Mutawakkil, Selim received the title “Servant of Mecca and Medina.” The Ottoman army overcame the remaining Mamluk resistance and took Damascus and Jerusalem.

In Egypt, Tuman Bay proclaimed himself sultan and refused to submit. Selim crossed the Sinai Desert with his army, announcing that he would grant amnesty to the people and peasantry of Egypt — that his campaign was directed only against the Mamluk military caste. On 22 January 1517, at the Battle of Reydaniyya outside Cairo, Selim defeated Tuman Bay. The Mamluk sultan attempted to continue resistance by guerrilla methods but was captured and executed. Cairo fell, and the Mamluk sultanate — which had ruled Egypt since 1250 — was extinguished.

The Holy Cities and the Transformation of the Caliphate

On 17 July 1517, the Sherif of Mecca sent Selim the keys of the Holy Cities — Mecca and Medina — and announced his submission. Syria, Egypt, and the Hejaz now recognized Ottoman sovereignty, and Selim also laid claim to parts of the Yemen. Appointing Hayra Bay, the former Mamluk governor of Aleppo, as governor of Egypt, Selim returned to Istanbul.

Inalcik treats the addition of the Arab lands — and especially of Mecca and Medina — as the beginning of a new era. Selim sent to his palace in Istanbul the holy relics of the Prophet, considered the symbols of the caliphate. However, Inalcik explicitly rejects the traditional story that the Abbasid caliph al-Mutawakkil formally surrendered the office of caliph to Selim. By classical Sunni doctrine, the caliph had to descend from the Quraysh, the Prophet’s own tribe, and the classical concept of a single caliph for the whole Islamic community had had no force since the thirteenth century. Every Muslim ruler called himself caliph.

Nevertheless, Selim’s status changed radically. By annexing the old heartland of the caliphate, he became more than simply a gazi sultan on the frontiers of the Islamic world: he became at the same time protector of Mecca and Medina and guardian of the pilgrimage routes — a responsibility that carried more real authority than the title of caliph. The empire was no longer a frontier state but an Islamic caliphate, and the Ottoman sultans now considered themselves protectors not only of the frontiers but of the entire Muslim world. The Sherif of Mecca and the Muslim rulers of Sumatra and India would soon address the Ottoman sultan as “Protector of Islam,” and the khans of Turkestan would appeal to Istanbul to block Russian expansion toward the Volga.

Administration and Legacy

One result of Selim’s conquests was the elevation of religious law (the seriat) to a position of prime importance in the administration of the state. The new consciousness of the Ottoman rulers as protectors of all Islam reinforced the influence of the ulema and the conservative religious establishment. Another, equally important result was economic: the Ottomans now controlled the world’s richest centers of the transit trade. Ottoman state income doubled. The reserve treasury in the palace overflowed. With these resources, Selim’s son and successor, Suleiman I (1520–66), was able to support his plans for worldwide conquests.

Selim died in 1520 at the age of fifty, struck down by cancer (the “sultan’s disease” — a carbuncle or skin infection that proved fatal). He had reigned only eight years, but his conquests doubled the size of the Ottoman Empire and transformed its character permanently. The Venetian ambassador reported that Selim had been a man of iron will, cruel and decisive, who inspired terror in his own court as much as in his enemies. Ottoman chroniclers, while acknowledging his harshness, celebrated him as Yavuz — the Stern, the Grim — a sultan who crushed enemies of the faith and raised the empire to heights his predecessors could not have imagined.

Inalcik’s Assessment

Inalcik places Selim at the hinge of Ottoman history. Bayezid II’s reign of consolidation created the economic and military conditions — particularly the modernization of the army and the adoption of firearms — that made Selim’s conquests possible. Selim, in turn, broke the old pattern of the frontier principality: he made the Ottoman state into the dominant power of the Islamic world, the protector of the Holy Cities, and the heir of the caliphal tradition. His son Suleiman would inherit an empire whose income had doubled, whose treasury overflowed, and whose strategic position — controlling the trade routes between Europe, Asia, and Africa — made it a true world power. “This period of development,” Inalcik writes of the years leading up to Selim’s reign, “created the conditions necessary for the great conquests of Selim I and Suleiman I.”
