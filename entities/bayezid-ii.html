Bayezid II

Bayezid II (1447–1512), known in Ottoman tradition as Sofu or Veli Bayezid (“the Pious” or “the Saint”), was the eighth sultan of the Ottoman dynasty, reigning from 1481 to 1512. The son of Mehmed the Conqueror, he succeeded his father after Mehmed’s sudden death in 1481, defeating his brother Cem in a civil war. His twenty-one-year reign has often been dismissed as “feeble and inglorious,” but Fisher argues it was a period of necessary consolidation — the empire absorbing Mehmed’s conquests while managing a complex web of diplomacy with Venice, the Mamluks, Safavid Persia, the Papacy, and the kingdoms of Europe.

Youth and Character

Bayezid served as governor of Amasya from 1455 to 1481 thirty years in one provincial post. At Amasya he developed into a scholarly, introspective prince who filled his days with music, poetry, philosophy, theology, astronomy, and cosmography. Unlike his warlike father, he was given to books and talk rather than arms. Ottoman historians called him Sofu or Veli — a religious devotee. He wore simple clothes, ate moderately, abstained from wine, and collected finely cut diamonds and hand-wrought metal objects. He was, however, a fine horseman and excellent marksman with the bow. He took part in the 1473 campaign against Uzun Hasan, leading thirty thousand Rumelian troops on the right wing at the Battle of the Euphrates, but he was never a magnetic military leader. After the battle, his troops were found in disorder by Gedik Ahmed Pasha, who publicly reproached him — beginning a feud that would end with Gedik Ahmed’s execution after Bayezid became sultan. He had eight sons: Abdullah, Shehin, Alem, Ahmed, Mehmed, Mahmud, Korkud, and Selim (the future Selim I).

The Succession Crisis

Mehmed II died unexpectedly in his camp at Maltepe in early May 1481, while preparing a campaign. The grand vizier Karamani Mehmed Pasha, hoping to bring Mehmed’s younger son Cem to the throne, concealed the sultan’s death, closed the city to the Janissaries, and sent slaves to notify Cem in Konya. The plot failed: the messengers were caught by the beylerbey of Anatolia, and the Janissaries learned the truth, crossed to Istanbul on any available transport, and lynched the grand vizier, parading through the streets shouting “Long live Bayezid!” The pashas in Istanbul — Ishak Pasha, Daud Pasha, the ağa of the Janissaries, and the beylerbeys of Rumelia and Anatolia — were all relatives of Bayezid by marriage. They raised Bayezid’s son Korkud as a regent and placed the weight of the Janissaries, the solaks (the sultan’s left-handed guards), and the palace pages behind Bayezid. Bayezid learned of his father’s death and rode from Amasya to Istanbul in eight days. At Ismid he halted, sent a trusted slave to confirm with his own eyes that Mehmed was truly dead, then proceeded to Üsküdar, where the pashas greeted him with galleys and barges. The Janissaries surrounded his barge demanding a donative of a thousand akçes per sipahi and three hundred per Janissary plus a daily pay raise. Bayezid agreed, landed, and rode through a cordon of soldiers who also demanded he appoint only Janissary officers, court sipahis, and palace pages as vizirs. He assented and spurred his horse to the palace. Seventeen days had elapsed between Mehmed’s death and Bayezid’s arrival in Istanbul.

The Civil War with Cem

Cem raised twenty thousand men at Konya, including feudal sipahis from Anatolia, asabs (irregular infantry), dervishes, tribesmen from Karamania and the Warsak and Turgud tribes, and the sipahis and timarjis assembled for Mehmed’s cancelled campaign. He marched to Bursa, where the Janissary garrison initially resisted but eventually recognized him, and Bursa opened its gates. Cem had money coined and the Friday sermon read in his name — the two prerogatives of a sultan. Astrologers had spread the rumor that Cem was destined to rule, and many Anatolians preferred him, believing him to be just. The citizens of Bursa, fearing for their property, sent a deputation to Bayezid led by Seljuk Hatun (a daughter of Sultan Mehmed I) and several mollas, proposing the empire be divided: Bayezid keeping the European half and Cem ruling Anatolia. Bayezid replied, “Kings have no relatives,” and prepared for battle. At the plain of Yenişehir on 20 June 1481, Bayezid exhorted his troops, renewed promises of money, and led them personally. He had also corrupted Cem’s chamberlain Yakub with a promise of a hundred thousand akçes and the position of beylerbey of Anatolia. The battle lasted until noon; Cem was decisively defeated. As the chronicler Neşri wrote, “The Karaman crows scattered like young from the nest when they saw the eagles of Bayezid.”

Cem fled through Eskişehir to Konya, then set out for Egypt with his family. Sultan Kaitbay of Egypt received him as a son. Bayezid pursued leisurely, reaching Konya four days after Cem’s departure, and sent Gedik Ahmed Pasha in pursuit as far as Ereğli, the Ottoman-Egyptian border. Bayezid invested his son Abdullah with the governorship of Karaman and returned to Istanbul.

The Gedik Ahmed Crisis

Bayezid had Gedik Ahmed Pasha arrested and imprisoned — partly from personal feud (Gedik Ahmed had publicly humiliated Bayezid at the Battle of the Euphrates) and partly on suspicion that he favored Cem. The Janissaries, sipahis, and silihdars (sword-bearers), who adored Gedik Ahmed as the ablest general in the empire, stormed the palace, threatening Bayezid. They demanded his liberation and the dismissal of his accusers. Bayezid, fearing the troops might turn to Cem, capitulated. Not only did he release Gedik Ahmed, but he made him vizir with special authority. Bayezid then sent Gedik Ahmed to subdue a revolt in Karamania led by Kasim Bey of the Karamanoglu family, which he did.

Cem in Europe and the Diplomatic Crisis

The most persistent foreign relations problem of Bayezid’s reign was the presence of his brother Cem in Christian hands. After leaving Egypt, Cem travelled to Rhodes, where the Hospitaller Grand Master Pierre d’Aubusson saw his value. D’Aubusson imprisoned Cem and negotiated an agreement with Bayezid: the Hospitallers would keep Cem securely in return for an annual payment of 45,000 ducats. Over the next decade, Cem was moved from Rhodes to France and then to the Papal court in Rome, where Pope Innocent VIII and later Alexander VI used him as a lever against Bayezid, threatening to release him at the head of a crusade. Bayezid’s agents paid the Pope, the Hospitallers, and the King of Naples to keep Cem imprisoned and out of active use. The crisis involved diplomacy with France (Charles VIII, who saw Cem as a weapon against the Papacy), Naples (King Ferrante, who housed Cem), the Mamluk sultan Qaitbay (who had offered Cem refuge), and the Venetian Republic. Cem died in Naples in 1495, possibly poisoned, while in the custody of Pope Alexander VI. His death freed Bayezid from the largest diplomatic burden of his reign.

Foreign Policy and Wars

Bayezid’s foreign policy was one of strategic consolidation. He made peace with Venice, which ended the long Ottoman-Venetian war inherited from Mehmed II, though hostilities flared again in 1499–1503 over Venetian holdings in the Morea. Relations with Hungary remained tense along the Danube frontier. The rise of Shah Ismail’s Safavid Empire in Persia posed a new ideological threat: Safavid propaganda spread among Anatolian Turkmen tribes, leading to the Şahkulu rebellion of 1511, a major religious and political uprising that nearly toppled Bayezid. The Ottoman-Mamluk rivalry simmered over control of Anatolian buffer states and the holy cities. Bayezid’s conquest of Kili and Akkirman (1484) secured the mouths of the Danube and Dniester, completing Ottoman control of the Black Sea coast.

Deposition and Death

In his final years, the succession struggle between Bayezid’s sons — particularly Selim (the future Selim I) and Ahmed — tore the empire apart. The Janissaries supported Selim, who was more warlike and resembled Mehmed the Conqueror. In 1512, Selim forced his father to abdicate. Bayezid died shortly afterward, possibly poisoned, on the road to his retirement in Demotika/Dimetoka.

Legacy

Fisher’s revisionist argument presents Bayezid not as a “slothful sultan” but as a ruler who understood that an empire strained by decades of relentless expansion needed a period of internal consolidation. His reign preserved the Ottoman state through a complex international environment, managing the Cem crisis through diplomacy rather than war, making strategic peace with Venice, and keeping the Safavid and Mamluk threats at bay. The foundations he laid made the even more dramatic conquests of his son Selim I — who would conquer Syria, Egypt, and the Holy Cities — possible.
