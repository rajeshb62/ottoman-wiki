Lisbon — لزبون (Lizbon) / Lisboa

Lisbon (Portuguese: Lisboa; Ottoman Turkish: لزبون, Lizbon; Arabic: لشبونة, Lishbuna) was the capital of the Kingdom of Portugal and the metropolitan center of the Portuguese maritime empire. From Lisbon, Vasco da Gama departed in 1497 to open the Cape Route to India, and to Lisbon returned the spice-laden fleets that bypassed the traditional Ottoman-controlled routes through the Red Sea and Persian Gulf. For the Ottoman Empire, Lisbon was the source of the Portuguese naval threat that the Ottomans confronted in the Indian Ocean, the Red Sea, and the Persian Gulf throughout the sixteenth century.

Geography and Strategic Position

Lisbon lies at approximately 38.7° N, 9.1° W, on the northern bank of the Tagus River (Rio Tejo) estuary, at the point where the river widens into a vast natural harbor — the Mar da Palha (Sea of Straw) — before emptying into the Atlantic Ocean approximately 15 km downstream. The Tagus estuary, one of the finest natural harbors in Europe, provided deep, sheltered anchorage for the largest fleets. The city is built on a series of hills on the northern shore, with the harbor on the south and west. Its position at the southwestern edge of Europe, projecting into the Atlantic, made Lisbon the natural departure point for Atlantic exploration and the terminus for ships returning from Asia around the Cape of Good Hope.

Before the Portuguese Age of Discovery, Lisbon was a marginal Mediterranean-Atlantic trading port. After da Gama’s voyage, it became the entrepot of the European spice trade and the seat of the Casa da India (India House), which administered the Portuguese monopoly on the Cape Route.

Lisbon and the Disruption of Ottoman Trade

The opening of the Cape Route by Vasco da Gama (1497-1499), who landed at Calicut in 1498, fundamentally altered the structure of Eurasian trade. Before da Gama, spices reached Europe through the Red Sea and Persian Gulf routes controlled by the Mamluks (and later the Ottomans), with Venetian merchants acting as the primary European purchasers in Alexandria, Beirut, and Aleppo. The Portuguese Cape Route bypassed these intermediaries entirely, delivering spices directly to Lisbon for distribution throughout Europe.

Inalcik assesses the impact: “the annual purchase of twelve thousand quintals [of spices by the Venetians in Alexandria] between 1560 and 1564 was the same amount as before Vasco de Gama’s discovery of the sea route to India. As a result the Lisbon market underwent periodic crises” (Inalcik, p. 5428-5432). This passage reveals that the Ottoman spice trade was far more resilient than traditional historiography has suggested. The Lisbon market, despite being the official monopoly supplier of spices to Europe, could not undercut the traditional Red Sea routes. In 1564, a Portuguese spy in Egypt reported that thirty thousand quintals of spices had arrived in Alexandria — a volume that directly challenged Lisbon’s monopoly.

The Portuguese in Lisbon reacted to this competition by attempting to enforce a naval blockade of the Red Sea and Persian Gulf. The Portuguese crown prohibited the sale of ships, naval stores, and weapons to Muslim powers, and the cartaz system was designed to intercept and tax any Indian Ocean shipping not licensed by Portuguese authorities.

Relations with the Ottoman Empire

Portugal and the Ottoman Empire were the two great rival powers of the sixteenth-century Indian Ocean. The confrontation unfolded across multiple theaters:

1509: The Portuguese destroyed a Mamluk fleet at Chaul. The Mamluk sultan turned to the Ottoman sultan for assistance, and the Ottomans “immediately sent material and craftsman to Suez to build ships” (Inalcik, p. 5405-5407).

1516-1517: The Ottoman conquest of Syria, Egypt, and the Hejaz brought the Ottomans into direct confrontation with Portuguese power in the Red Sea. Selim I ordered the construction of a fleet at Suez “to drive the Portuguese out of the Indian Ocean” (Inalcik, p. 5410-5412).

1517-1525: The Ottoman admiral Selman Reis repulsed Portuguese attacks on Jidda and advanced on Yemen and Aden (Inalcik, p. 5412-5413).

1538: A major Ottoman fleet of thirty ships sailed for Diu in northern India but failed to dislodge the Portuguese, partly because the local Gujarati sultan refused to cooperate (Inalcik, p. 5416-5420).

1552: The Ottoman expedition against Hormuz failed to oust the Portuguese from the Persian Gulf chokepoint (Inalcik, p. 1752-1754).

1578-1585: Taking advantage of the Portuguese defeat at Alcazar (Alcacer Quibir) in Morocco, where King Sebastian of Portugal was killed, an Ottoman fleet sailed from Suez and captured Portuguese factories on the East African coast. The Prince of Mombasa recognized Ottoman suzerainty in 1585 (Inalcik, p. 2011-2014).

1580: The Spanish Habsburgs annexed Portugal (the Iberian Union, 1580-1640), making the Portuguese Empire a target of the Anglo-Ottoman alliance. The Ottoman sultan “showed an interest in efforts to revive the kingdom of Portugal” (Inalcik, p. 1956-1957), potentially as an independent state freed from Spanish control.

1603: A Persian diplomatic mission to Lisbon, led by the shah’s envoy, “carried with it two hundred bales of silk and tried to prove that silk would be cheaper when shipped by sea” (Inalcik, p. 2051-2053). This was part of Shah Abbas I’s effort to bypass Ottoman transit routes by selling Persian silk directly to Europe via the Indian Ocean.

Lisbon as a Market

The Casa da India in Lisbon organized the annual spice fleet. Each year, a fleet of Portuguese ships — the Armadas da India — departed Lisbon in March or April, rounded the Cape of Good Hope during the southern winter, and arrived in Goa or Cochin in September. The return fleet, laden with pepper, cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg, and other Asian goods, departed India in December or January, catching the winter Monsoon across the Indian Ocean, rounded the Cape, and arrived in Lisbon between June and September. The Lisbon market then distributed spices throughout Europe, primarily through Antwerp (until the Dutch Revolt) and later through Amsterdam.

Despite Portuguese efforts, the Ottoman spice trade survived. Inalcik notes that “European merchants bought some of the spices at Damascus, exporting them through Beirut, while a large part of the cargo was forwarded to Bursa and Istanbul” (Inalcik, p. 5439-5442). The customs regulations of Bursa for 1545 show European merchants buying spices there, and by 1582 customs receipts on spices in Bursa had reached 7,250 gold ducats (Inalcik, p. 5442-5444).

Key Connections

  • Goa: Portuguese capital in India; the administrative center of the Estado da India subordinate to Lisbon
  • Seville/Cadiz: Spanish ports; Portugal’s Iberian rival before 1580 and partner in the Iberian Union
  • Antwerp: Primary distribution center for the Lisbon spice trade in the early sixteenth century
  • Suez: Ottoman naval base for Indian Ocean operations; the Lisbon-Suez axis defined the Ottoman-Portuguese naval confrontation
  • Aden: Ottoman-controlled Red Sea port; the Lisbon fleet’s spices competed with those routed through Aden and Jidda
  • London: English capital; after 1580, London merchants received Ottoman capitulations and competed with Lisbon for the Levant trade
  • Hormuz: Portuguese vassal in the Persian Gulf, whose cartaz system was administered in coordination with Lisbon
  • Cairo/Alexandria: Ottoman Egyptian ports; the resilience of the Red Sea spice route kept these markets competitive with Lisbon
