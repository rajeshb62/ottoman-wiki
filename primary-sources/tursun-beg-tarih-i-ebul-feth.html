Tursun Beg — Tarih-i Ebul Feth (History of the Father of Conquest)

Tursun Beg (Turnisa Bey) wrote the Tarih-i Ebul Feth (History of the Father of Conquest) between approximately 1497 and 1500, during the reign of Bayezid II. The work covers the Ottoman state from Mehmed II’s first accession to the throne in 1442 through to the 1480s, encompassing a forty-seven-year span. Tursun Beg was a timar-holder and state official who rose to the position of chief finance minister (defterdar). He was present at the conquest of Istanbul in 1453 and accompanied the sultan into Hagia Sophia. He later undertook the registration of houses and shops in the newly conquered city under the delegation of Sultan Mehmed himself. The modern Turkish edition was prepared for publication by Ahmet Tezbaşar, based on the Ottoman Historical Society Journal edition, with three surviving manuscript copies in the Topkapı Palace Revan Library and the Ayasofya Library.

A notable feature of the work is its uneven coverage — events during the period when Tursun Beg personally accompanied campaigns are narrated in great detail, while later events after Mahmud Pasha’s dismissal from the grand vizierate are summarized or omitted entirely. The important affair of Sultan Cem is not mentioned at all. The book contains extensive Arabic and Persian verse passages which the author included for artistic reinforcement of his prose narrative.

Contents

Editor’s Foreword (A Few Words)

Ahmet Tezbaşar’s preface describes the scope of the work, the manuscript tradition, and the editorial choices made in preparing the modern Turkish edition. The three surviving manuscripts are all written in naskh script. The editor explains the simplification process: the original 15th-century Ottoman language is heavily dominated by Arabic and Persian vocabulary, so a freer approach was taken, with certain passages unrelated to historical events being omitted. A glossary was added for historical and military terms. The editor also notes the difficulty of reading place names, country names, and personal names pertaining to Anatolia and Rumelia.

About the Author

Tursun Beg’s actual name was Turnisa Bey, corrupted by common usage to Tursun. Despite holding high state office as defterdar, there is almost no record of him in history or biography books — his name appears only once in Sehi Bey’s tezkire. What is known comes from autobiographical remarks scattered through his own work. He was a timar-holder from a young age, likely the son of a military commander or zeamet-holder. He was proficient in Arabic and Persian, had studied the sciences and arts, and was personally known to Sultan Mehmed. After the conquest he gave up his timar to serve under his uncle Hacı Ali Bey (the governor of Bursa), and was entrusted by the sultan with registering the population and properties of the newly conquered Istanbul. Later in his career he rose to the position of defterdar under Mahmud Pasha’s patronage.

Traditional Opening

The history begins with the classical Ottoman historiographical framework: praise of God (hamd), praise of the Prophet (na’t), and praise of Sultan Bayezid II. A philosophical discourse on the necessity of sultanate draws on Nasir al-Din Tusi’s Akhlaq-i Nasiri and the four cardinal virtues. Stories are included about Alexander the Great and Ali ibn Abi Talib, along with sayings of Mahmud Pasha. This section traces the Ottoman sultanate through Murad II, Mehmed II’s first accession and the subsequent transfer back to Murad II, leading to Mehmed’s second and definitive accession.

The Second Accession and the Karaman Campaign

Mehmed’s reaccession to the throne after the death of Murad II. The Janissary discipline incident, where the new sultan asserts his authority over the soldiery. The Karaman campaign against the Karamanid beylik, one of Mehmed’s early military actions in Anatolia.

The Building of Boğazkesen (Rumeli Hisarı)

The strategic reasoning behind constructing the fortress on the European shore of the Bosphorus — the narrowest point of the strait. Tursun Beg provides a detailed description of the fortress and its construction process, the division of labour among the pashas for building sections of the walls, and the resulting confrontation with the Byzantine emperor Constantine XI.

The Conquest of Constantinople (1453)

The longest and most detailed section of the work. The accidental skirmish that triggered open hostilities between the Ottomans and Byzantines. The massive siege operations: the land army under Mehmed’s personal command, the naval forces, the great bombard cannons. The extraordinary feat of transporting Ottoman ships overland past the Byzantine chain boom across the Golden Horn. The final assault on 29 May 1453, the breaching of the walls, the death of Emperor Constantine XI (referred to as the Tekfur), and the sack of the city. Mehmed’s entry into the city and his visit to Hagia Sophia.

The Rebuilding of Istanbul

The immediate aftermath of the conquest: Mehmed’s program of repopulating the devastated city, the restoration of water systems, the construction of mosques (including the first imperial mosque complex), the building of the new palace (Saray-ı Cedid), the Eyüb Sultan complex, and the Kilitbahir fortress on the Dardanelles.

The Conquest of Aynoz and Taşoz

The capture of Aynoz (Ainos/Enez) on the Thracian coast and the island of Taşoz (Thasos), expanding Ottoman control over the northern Aegean.

The Belgrade Campaign (1456)

The reasons for the campaign against the Hungarian fortress of Belgrade. The siege operations, the intervention of John Hunyadi, the fierce battle, and the Ottoman withdrawal. Tursun Beg notes the death of Hunyadi shortly after the campaign.

The Circumcision Festival

A detailed account of the grand circumcision festival (Sünnet Düğünü) held in Edirne for the princes Bayezid and Mustafa — an important state ceremony demonstrating Ottoman wealth, power, and dynastic continuity.

The First Morea Campaign (1458)

Mehmed’s first campaign into the Peloponnese (Mora). The conquest of Corinth (Kurisus) and Patras (Ballubedreh), bringing the peninsula under Ottoman suzerainty.

Mahmud Pasha’s Laz Expedition and the Conquest of Semendre and Amasra

Grand Vizier Mahmud Pasha’s military expedition to the Laz country in northeastern Anatolia. The sieges of Resova, Mul, Smederevo (Semendre), Güğercinlik, and Mačva. The conquest of Amasra on the Black Sea coast.

The Conquest of the İsfendiyar Province and Sinop (1461)

The submission of İsmail Bey of the İsfendiyarid dynasty. The surrender of Sinop, one of the key Black Sea ports, and the incorporation of the İsfendiyar province into the Ottoman domain. İsmail Bey was granted the governorship of Scopia (Skopje) in exchange.

The Conquest of Koyluhisar and Trebizond (1461)

The campaign that culminated in the surrender of Trebizond (Trabzon), capital of the Empire of Trebizond under Emperor David Komnenos. The conquest of Koyluhisar fortress along the way. The pacification of Uzun Hasan’s Aq Qoyunlu, who had been allied with the Komnenoi.

The Matter of the Impaler Voivode (Vlad Dracula, 1462)

The campaign against Vlad III Dracula (Kazıklı Voyvoda), the voivode of Wallachia. Vlad’s brutal methods of impaling Ottoman prisoners. The Ottoman expedition, Vlad’s famous night attack on the Ottoman camp, his defeat and flight into Hungary. Radu the Fair (Vlad’s brother) installed as the Ottoman vassal voivode of Wallachia.

The Conquest of Midilli (Lesbos/Mytilene, 1462)

The naval campaign against the Genoese-held island of Lesbos. The siege of Mytilene, the betrayal of Nicorezzo Gattilusio, and the incorporation of the island into the Ottoman realm.

The Conquest of Bosnia (1463)

The campaign against the Kingdom of Bosnia. The capture of King Stephen Tomašević. The siege of Jajce (Yayçe) and the conquest of associated fortresses. Cave warfare tactics employed during the Bosnian campaign.

The Death of Karamanoglu İbrahim Bey and the Karaman Succession

The death of İbrahim Bey of the Karamanids and the subsequent struggle between his sons İshak Bey and Pir Ahmed Bey. Pir Ahmed Bey sought Ottoman support, leading to further Ottoman intervention in Karaman.

The Infidel Alliance and the Second Bosnia Campaign

The Venetian-Hungarian-Albanian coalition against the Ottomans. The defence of the Morea. The second Bosnia campaign and the recapture of Jajce. Mahmud Pasha’s relief of Midilli against Venetian attack.

The Siege of İzvornik (Zvornik)

Winter campaigning and the Hungarian siege of Zvornik on the Drina River. The use of Martolos messengers for communication through enemy territory. The Hungarian withdrawal.

The Albanian Campaigns and the Construction of Elbasan (1466–1467)

The first raid into Albania (Arnavutluk), the revolt of Albanian forces under Skanderbeg (İskender Bey), and the construction of Elbasan (İlbasan) Castle as a permanent Ottoman stronghold in the region. The second campaign into Albania, featuring mountain warfare and the attempt to capture Croia (Krujë).

The Sultan’s Karaman Campaign and the Expulsion of Pir Ahmed

Mehmed’s personal campaign into Karaman against Pir Ahmed, who had rebelled against Ottoman authority. Pir Ahmed’s expulsion from Karaman and the full incorporation of the beylik into the Ottoman state.

The Conquest of Ağriboz (Negroponte, 1470)

The conquest of the Venetian-held island of Negroponte (Eğriboz/Chalkis), a major Venetian fortress in the Aegean. The siege operations, the naval blockade, and the fall of the island to the Ottomans.

The Conquest of Alâiye (Alanya)

The incorporation of the coastal fortress and port of Alâiye (Alanya) on the southern Anatolian coast into the Ottoman domain.

The Battle of Otlukbeli and the Defeat of Uzun Hasan (1473)

The decisive Ottoman victory over Uzun Hasan’s Aq Qoyunlu at the Battle of Otlukbeli. The campaign narrative covers the mobilization of the Ottoman army, the march through eastern Anatolia, the battle itself with its use of artillery and Janissary infantry against the Aq Qoyunlu cavalry, and the pursuit of the defeated forces. This victory secured Ottoman dominance over eastern Anatolia.

The Conquest of Kefe (Caffa, 1475)

The naval expedition against the Genoese colony of Caffa (Kefe/Feodosia) in the Crimea. The surrender of the city and the establishment of Ottoman suzerainty over the Crimean Khanate.

The Campaign Against Kara Boğdan (Moldavia, 1476)

The campaign against Stephen the Great of Moldavia (Kara Boğdan). The Battle of Valea Albă (Războieni), where Stephen inflicted losses on the Ottomans, followed by the Ottoman counter-campaign and Stephen’s eventual submission as a vassal.

The Conquest of İskenderiye and Akça Hisar

Further military operations against Venetian and Hungarian holdings: the conquest of İskenderiye (Scutari/Shkodër) and Akça Hisar, consolidating Ottoman control in Albania and Bosnia.

Gedik Ahmed Pasha’s Campaign and the Rhodes Expedition

Gedik Ahmed Pasha’s naval campaign against Polya (Puglia/southern Italy), raiding the Italian coast. Mesih Pasha’s unsuccessful expedition against the Hospitaller-held island of Rhodes.

The Sultan’s Plan to Deliver Egypt and Syria

Mehmed’s intention to campaign against the Mamluk Sultanate in Egypt and Syria, a project cut short by his death. This section indicates the scope of Mehmed’s ambitions in his final years.

The Death of Sultan Mehmed Han (1481)

A detailed account of the sultan’s final illness and death. Tursun Beg notes the year as 1481 (or possibly 1479 in some recensions). The reaction of the state officials and the army, the succession crisis, and the beginning of the interregnum.

The Days of Interregnum (Fetret Günleri)

The struggle for succession between the princes Bayezid and Cem following Mehmed’s death. The Janissary uprising, the murder of Grand Vizier Mehmed Pasha (the Conqueror’s last vizier), and the factional conflicts at court.

Sultan Bayezid Han’s Accession to the Ottoman Throne

Bayezid II’s consolidation of power after defeating his brother Cem’s challenge. The re-establishment of central authority and the new sultan’s early policies.

The Conquest of Kili and Akkirman (1484)

Under Bayezid II, the conquest of the Black Sea fortresses of Kili (Chilia) and Akkirman (Cetatea Albă/Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi), securing Ottoman control over the mouths of the Danube and Dniester rivers.

Karagöz Bey’s Expedition and Ali Pasha’s Campaign

The later military expeditions under Bayezid II’s reign: Karagöz Bey’s campaign into the Arab lands and Ali Pasha’s march against Karaboğdan (Moldavia), continuing Ottoman expansion into the early 1490s.

Glossary (Lugatçe)

A comprehensive Ottoman-era glossary appended by the modern editor, covering administrative, military, and technical terminology used throughout the work — from A through Z, with Ottoman terms and their Turkish and English equivalents.
