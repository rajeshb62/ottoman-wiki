John Lloyd Stephens — Incidents of Travel in Egypt, Arabia Petraea, and the Holy Land

John Lloyd Stephens (1805–1852) was an American lawyer, diplomat, traveler, and the pioneer of Maya archaeology. His Incidents of Travel in Egypt, Arabia Petraea, and the Holy Land (Harper & Brothers, 1844) recounts his journey through Ottoman Egypt, the Sinai Peninsula, and Ottoman Syria (including Palestine) in 1835–1836. The work is one of the most popular 19th-century American travelogues of the Middle East and provides extensive first-hand observation of Ottoman provincial administration, the hajj route, Bedouin society, the condition of Christian and Jewish communities, and the monuments of Pharaonic and Biblical antiquity under the rule of Mehmed Ali Pasha. The text is drawn from the Carnegie Library copy at the Pennsylvania State College.

Contents of the First Volume

Preface

Stephens explains his motives for travel: a desire to see the lands of the Bible and the monuments of ancient civilization, coupled with the advantages of his diplomatic appointment as United States special agent to the Sublime Porte.

Chapter I — Alexandria

Arrival at Alexandria. Description of Pompey’s Pillar, the catacombs, the Warwick Vase, and the Pasha’s canal. First impressions of Ottoman Egypt under the governorship of Mehmed Ali Pasha. The boats of the Nile.

Chapter II — From Alexandria to Cairo

The journey upriver. Stephens contrasts his experiences with the conventional travelers’ tales he had read. An unintended bath in the Nile. The iron rule of Mehmed Ali Pasha. Entrance into Cairo and a chat with a pasha.

Chapter III — Cairo: The Slave Market and the Pyramids

The slave-market at Cairo — a detailed description of the human trade under Ottoman rule. The tomb of the Pasha. The Pyramid of Cheops, oppressive attentions of the Bedouin Arabs, and the Sphinx.

Chapter IV — Journey up the Nile

Travel up the Nile. An Arab burial observed. Pilgrims en route to Mecca. Trials of patience with local officials. A hurricane on the Nile. Experience of a Turkish bath.

Chapter V — Sport and Adventure on the Nile

Sporting on the Nile. A recluse encountered. An Egyptian village scene. Siout (Asyut). A wolf-race among the tombs. Adventure with a provincial governor.

Chapter VI — Slavery and Crocodiles

Slavery in Egypt — Stephens’ observations on the institution as practiced under Ottoman rule. How to catch a crocodile. An elaborate practical joke. Imaginary perils of travel. Arabs not so bad as they might be.

Chapter VII — Dendera and Thebes

The Temple of Dendera. Practice versus theory in Egyptian archaeology. Regulating the sun. The French at Thebes. The Curse of Pharaoh. An Egyptian tournament. Preparations for dinner. An English traveling lady.

Chapter VIII — The Cataracts and Nubia

The Rock of the Chain. Ravages of the plague. Deserted quarries. A youthful navigator. Ancient inscriptions. An awkward discovery at a temple.

Chapter IX — Ascent of the Cataracts

Navigation past the cataracts. A nautical patriarch. Political improvement under Mehmed Ali. A Nubian damsel’s wardrobe. A test of friendship. East and West contrasted. Moonlight on the Nile.

Chapter X — Thebes: Temples and Tombs

Thebes and its great ruins. The Obelisk of Luxor (now in Paris). An avenue of sphinxes. Karnak. The Mummy-pits. The Tombs of the Kings. The Memnonium.

Chapter XI — The Arabs and the Pasha

The relationship between the Bedouin Arabs and Mehmed Ali Pasha’s government. March into the desert. Arab Christians encountered. A cold reception. Arab punctuality. A night in a convent. An Arab Christian priest. Speculative theology.

Chapter XII — Memphis and the Pyramids

A traveling artist and antiquary. An Egyptian sugar-house. Grecian architecture. A melancholy greeting. Tyranny of the Pasha — further observations on Mehmed Ali’s rule. Amateurs of physic. Memphis. Adventure with a wild boar. Perils of a pyramid. The catacombs of birds. Amor patriae.

Chapter XIII — Into the Desert

A good word for the Arabs. A prophecy fulfilled. Ruins of a lost city. A sheik of the Bedouins. Interviews and negotiations. A Hadj, or Pilgrimage to Mecca. Mohammedan heaven for wives. A French sheik. The bastinado. Departure for the desert.

Chapter XIV — Suez and the Red Sea

The caravan. Arab political economy. A projected railroad. The sirocco. Suez. A traveled Englishman. The Red Sea. Embarkation of pilgrims for Mecca. A misadventure. Scriptural localities. The bitter fountain (Marah).

Chapter XV — Sinai

The aspect of the mountains. Arab graves. The Pasha and the Bedouins. The value of water in the desert. Perplexing inscriptions. Habits of the Arabs. Ethics of the desert. Breach of the marriage vow. Arrival at the Convent of St. Catharine. An excess of welcome. Greece and America compared. Amor patriae.

Chapter XVI — Ascent of Mount Sinai

Ascent of Sinai. A miracle described. The Grotto of Elias. A monkish legend. The pinnacle of Sinai. Anchorites. Mohammed and his camel. An argument with monks. Legend of St. Catharine. The Rock of the Tables. The stone struck by Moses. Description of the Convent of St. Catharine. Habits and character of its inmates.

Chapter XVII — The Convent of St. Catharine

Diet of the monks. Advantages of abstinence. Scruples overcome. A mysterious brother. The convent burial-place. Strange charnel-house.

Contents of the Second Volume (Excerpted)

The second volume continues the journey through Ottoman Syria and Palestine covering Jerusalem, Bethlehem, the Dead Sea, the River Jordan, Nazareth, the Sea of Galilee, Damascus, and Baalbek, with detailed observations of Ottoman administration in the Levant, the condition of Christian and Jewish communities under Ottoman rule, the competing European consular jurisdictions, and the pilgrimage routes in the Holy Land.
