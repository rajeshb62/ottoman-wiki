Ottoman Markets and Agriculture

بخش اول: نظام بازار — The Ottoman Market System

The Çarşı (چارشى) and Bedesten (بدستان) System

The physical organization of the Ottoman market followed a pattern that had been established in the Islamic Near East for centuries. At the heart of every major Ottoman city stood the bedesten (بدستان) — a monumental stone building with iron doors, domed roof, and thick walls designed to resist both fire and pillage. Orhan Gazi constructed the first Ottoman bedesten in Bursa in 1340, establishing a trading centre that remains the commercial heart of the city to this day. Mehmed the Conqueror built the great bedesten of Istanbul after 1453, with 118 shops and storerooms inside the central structure and 984 shops in the surrounding markets, forming the nucleus of what is today known as the Covered Market (Kapalıçarşı — قپالى چارشى).

The bedesten served not merely as a marketplace but as a secure repository for valuable goods, money, and jewellery. Its doorkeepers, night-watchmen and brokers operated under direct government control. Around the central bedesten, streets of shops branched out in every direction, each street or quarter occupied exclusively by members of a single craft or by merchants selling the same type of goods. These market streets — the çarşı (چارشى) — were either roofed with stone vaults, as in Istanbul, or remained as lanes of open shops shaded by trees.

Evliya Çelebi, the great seventeenth-century traveller, divided Ottoman cities into two categories: those with a bedesten and those without. The bedesten and its surrounding markets were the economic engine of the Ottoman city, where local and long-distance merchants congregated, valuable goods accumulated, and the commercial life of the city pulsed. All large Ottoman towns in the Balkans — Tatar Pazarjik, Plovdiv, Sarajevo, Sofia, Skopje, Monastir, Serres, Salonica — grew around bedestens.

The Narh (نرخ) System: State Price Controls

The Ottoman state exercised tight control over market prices through the narh (نرخ) system. Prices were not determined by supply and demand but by administrative decree, established through a formal procedure. Prominent members of the community and the ehl-i hibre (اهل خبره — expert inspectors from the guilds) would meet in the presence of the local kadi (قاضى). They established the amount and quality of raw materials, added labour costs, and fixed a market price that allowed a profit of approximately 10 per cent — or 20 per cent for highly skilled labour. Sometimes the sultan himself inspected the registers of Istanbul market prices.

The purpose of the narh system was threefold: to prevent profiteering and exploitation of consumers; to ensure stable prices for essential goods, particularly foodstuffs for Istanbul; and to maintain social and political stability by guaranteeing that the urban population — especially the Janissaries and other kapıkulu troops — could afford basic necessities.

Food prices were fixed at the point of production, not just at the point of sale. Only state-authorized merchants could buy produce, and strict measures were taken to prevent smuggling. Provisions arrived at specified weigh-houses, whence they were distributed to representatives of the trade guilds. Large merchants and ship-owners who were caught smuggling or profiteering had their goods or ships confiscated.

The Muhtesib (محتصب) and İhtisab (احتساب) — Market Inspection

The enforcement of market regulations was entrusted to the muhtesib (محتصب), an officer derived from the old Islamic institution of hisba (حسبه) or ihtisab (احتساب). The Koran commanded that for the good of the Islamic community, the authority should actively support generally accepted social standards and prohibitions. In practice, hisba regulations mainly concerned trade and aimed at preventing fraud and profiteering, fixing fair prices in the markets, and controlling weights and the quality of merchandise.

The muhtesib constantly patrolled the markets to ensure enforcement of these laws. He brought violators before the local kadi, who could order flogging or fines. He stamped certain materials — timber, tile, cloth — according to their standard and prohibited the sale of unstamped materials. The Ottoman state reviewed the ihtisab laws to which the guilds were subject on the accession of each new sultan, indicating the importance attached to this institution.

Merchandise for the city market had to enter through particular gates, was carried through certain streets to specified markets or caravanserais, and was sold under the supervision of officials on duty. Valuable goods had absolutely to be sold through special brokers under government control. After goods had been weighed in the presence of guild representatives and taxed according to quantity, permission was given for their sale.

بخش دوم: نظام زراعت — The Agricultural System

Land Tenure: Miri Arazi, Mülk, and Vakıf

The Ottoman land system in the classical period rested on the principle that all agricultural land belonged to the state. This was miri arazi (ميرى اراضى — crown land), and it constituted approximately 87 per cent of all agricultural land in 1528. The state’s absolute control over land was essential to the functioning of the timar system (تيمار), and the Ottoman government, following the example of earlier Muslim states, announced that all rural agricultural land was miri — the property of the state.

The exceptions were mülk (ملك — freehold property) and vakıf (وقف — pious endowment) lands. Mülk lands were private property, usually granted by the sultan to distinguished individuals. Vakıf lands were endowed in perpetuity for charitable purposes — to support mosques, medreses, hospitals, imarets (soup kitchens), caravanserais, and other public institutions. The deed of endowment (vakfiye — وقفيه) was drawn up before a kadi, entered in his register, and confirmed by the sultan. Once endowed, only God had proprietary rights over a vakıf, so even if governments or states changed, the continuance of the public service was theoretically guaranteed.

These exceptional lands were contested throughout Ottoman history. When the state was weak, the area of land held as mülk or vakıf expanded; when a strong central authority emerged, private property rights and vakıfs were abolished and state control re-established. Bayezid I and especially Mehmed the Conqueror were famous for this type of reform. When Mehmed reviewed all land holdings in about 1470, he established the principle that all vakıfs that had not received the sultan’s sanction, or whose buildings or purpose no longer existed, should revert to the state. In this way over twenty thousand villages and farms became miri lands.

The Çift-Hane (چفت خانه) System: The Peasant Family Farm

The basic unit of agricultural production was the çift-hane (چفت خانه) — the peasant family farm. A çift (چفت) was a unit of agricultural land varying according to place from 60 to 150 dönüms (a dönüm being roughly one-quarter of an acre), a size deemed sufficient to support a single peasant family. The çift was indivisible: on the peasant’s death, his sons worked the holding jointly, but the unit itself could not be broken up.

The peasant who worked a çift had the status of a hereditary tenant. He enjoyed usufructuary rights — the right to work the land and pass it to his sons — but he could not sell it, grant it as a gift, or transfer it without permission. The land belonged ultimately to the state, and through the state to the sipahi (سپاهى — timar-holding cavalryman) who had the right to collect fixed revenues from it. This was what Inalcik called “fragmented possession”: the state, the sipahi, and the peasant all had simultaneous rights over the land.

The peasant paid two main taxes to the sipahi: the tithe (öşür — عشر, one-tenth of the crop, usually collected in kind) and the çift resmi (چفت رسمى), a fixed annual cash tax of twenty-two akçes for a full çift. Christians paid this tax under the name ispence (اسپنجه). The çift resmi was originally in lieu of labour services — the hay, fodder, wood, and services that the peasant had owed the Byzantine fief-holder. The Ottomans commuted these services for fixed cash payments wherever possible.

Taxation of Agriculture: Öşr, Haraç, and Cizye

Agricultural taxation rested on three principal pillars:

Öşr (عشر — tithe): The tithe on agricultural produce was the basic land tax, levied at one-tenth of the crop on Muslim peasants, though rates varied according to district and local custom as recorded in the kanunnames (قانون نامه) of each province. The tithe was usually collected in kind — grain, grapes, livestock — and stored in the sipahi’s barn.

Haraç (خراج): A land tax levied on non-Muslim peasants, technically a different category under Islamic law. The distinction between öşr and haraç was rooted in the şeriat but in practice the Ottoman fiscal system often blended them.

Cizye (جزيه): The poll tax on non-Muslim subjects, collected not by the sipahi but directly by the central Treasury. In the classical period the cizye was a fixed annual payment per adult male, but during the financial crises of the late sixteenth century it was increased four- or fivefold.

In addition to these, the peasant faced a variety of incidental taxes: marriage taxes, fines for minor crimes (half going to the sipahi and half to the sanjak beyi), and the çift bozan akçesi (چفت بوزان آقچهسى — farm-breaker’s tax), a compensation payment owed by a peasant who abandoned his land to enter a craft in a town.

The timar system was characterized by detailed fiscal registration. Immediately after the conquest of a region, and subsequently every twenty or thirty years, a commissioner called the il yazıcısı (ايل يازيجىسى — clerk of the district) was sent to determine the sources of revenue. He recorded the name of every head of family in each village and the approximate amount of land he owned, listing the total sum to be realized from the tithe, the çift resmi, and other taxes. These registers guided the kadi in case of dispute, and the timar was an indivisible and unalterable unit.

The Role of Agriculture in the Ottoman Economy

Agriculture was the foundation of the Ottoman economy. The overwhelming majority of the population — the reaya (رعايا — tax-paying subjects) — were peasants working the land. State revenues depended principally on agricultural taxes: the tithe on grain, the çift resmi, and the cizye. In 1528, state revenues totalled approximately 477 million akçes, of which a substantial portion came from agricultural taxation. About half the state’s income from the provinces was distributed as timars to the sipahi cavalry; the rest went to the central Treasury or was reserved as has (خاص) for the sultan, princes, and high officials.

The agricultural surplus fed the cities, especially Istanbul. The capital’s ovens alone consumed 250 tons of wheat daily in the mid-seventeenth century. Wheat, rice, sugar and spices came from Egypt; livestock, cereals, edible fats, and hides from the Black Sea region; cereals and hides from Thessaly and Macedonia; wine and Mediterranean products from the Morea and the Aegean islands. The Dobrudja became the granary of Istanbul, with hundreds of villages established and state grain-storehouses constructed at the ports.

This provisioning system was not left to the market. The need to feed Istanbul, the Palace, and the army was a major factor determining the rigidly monopolistic character of the Ottoman economy, with strict governmental controls on the trade of necessities. The state regulated everything from the distant producer to the retailer in Istanbul, linking the empire’s various areas of production to a single centre and creating an integrated — if centrally directed — economy.

بخش سوم: بحران و انحطاط — Crisis and Decline

The Price Revolution (نرخ طوفانى) and Its Impact on Agriculture

In the mid-sixteenth century, vast quantities of Mexican and Peruvian silver flooded the European market, causing the great Price Revolution. By the 1580s, European silver coins — especially Spanish reals — inundated the Ottoman market. A contemporary reported that “one of the main items of trade going to Turkey are Spanish reals sent by the chestful.” Within a short time, Ottoman prices doubled.

The impact on agriculture and rural society was devastating. Fixed-income groups — timar-holding sipahis, kapıkulus, and those who derived their incomes from vakıfs — were suddenly impoverished. The akçe was debased repeatedly in panic measures that only worsened the situation. In 1534 the central Treasury’s annual income had reached five million gold ducats, but by 1591 it was worth only half its original value, since most taxes were levied in akçe.

The debasement of the currency and the doubling of prices made it impossible for sipahis to maintain themselves on their timar incomes. The çift resmi, fixed in akçe since the time of Mehmed the Conqueror, lost most of its real value. Sipahis abandoned their timars rather than go on long campaigns that had become too costly. The state, desperate for cash to pay the Janissaries and the newly hired sekban musketeers, brought timar lands under direct Treasury control and farmed out their revenues to tax-farmers.

Depreciation brought in its train counterfeit coin, speculation, soaring interest rates, usury, and profiteering. The financial crisis was compounded by the long war with Austria that began in 1593, which drained the Treasury. The government increased taxes — the cizye was raised four- or fivefold — and converted the extraordinary levies called avarız (عوارض) into regular annual cash taxes. Money taxes became the principal source of state revenue, a development that revolutionized the tax system but placed an enormous burden on the peasantry.

The Celali (جلالى) Rebellions and Rural Devastation

The financial and military crises of the late sixteenth century produced the Celali rebellions (Celali İsyanları — جلالى ايصرانلرى), a period of anarchy and devastation that transformed Anatolia between 1595 and 1610.

To pay the expanding Janissary corps and the new sekban and sarıca infantry equipped with firearms, the state authorized provincial governors (beylerbeyis) to raise local taxes for the enlistment of sekban troops. This encouraged minor commanders to operate independently and plunder the people on their own account. When the state could not pay these troops after a campaign, or discharged them, bands of armed and unemployed men roamed the countryside, collecting provisions and money by force. The situation in Anatolia resembled France after the Hundred Years War, with companies of discharged soldiers pillaging the land.

The government proclaimed them Celalis — rebels against the state. Sipahis whose timars had been confiscated, landless peasants, and nomads seeking plunder joined the rebel forces. Under the leadership of Kara Yazıcı (قاره يازيجى), the rebel army numbered some twenty thousand men by 1598. After Kara Yazıcı’s defeat in 1602, the brigands dispersed throughout Anatolia. Between 1595 and 1610, these armed bands wrought havoc across the Anatolian countryside.

The consequences for agriculture were catastrophic:

  • Flight and migration: The richer Anatolians fled to the Balkans, the Crimea, Iran, and the Arab lands. Peasants abandoned their villages en masse.
  • Abandoned land: Fields lay fallow, and the area under cultivation shrank dramatically.
  • Famine: Hunger and famine followed the disruption of agricultural production.
  • Revenue collapse: The Treasury lost its rural sources of revenue as tax-paying peasants disappeared.
  • Permanent transformation: Janissaries were stationed in the provincial towns and cities, where they joined the ulema, guild masters, and merchants as a new influential class. They acquired large tracts of state lands by extortionate tax-farming, reducing the villagers on these lands to the status of sharecroppers.

The Celali period came at a time of great financial crisis and dragged the empire into a decline from which it never fully recovered. In 1607 the English ambassador wrote from Istanbul: “As far as I can see, the Turkish Empire was in great decline, almost ruined.”

The Decline of the Timar System and Its Impact on Agricultural Production

The timar system (تيمار) had been the backbone of both Ottoman military organization and agricultural administration. In the classical period, the timar-holding sipahi lived in the village of his timar, enforced the state’s land laws, maintained order, and collected the prescribed taxes in accordance with the kanunname. The peasant knew exactly what taxes were due, the sipahi had a secure fixed income, and the state had a cavalry army that cost it nothing in cash. This system began to disintegrate in the late sixteenth century.

Several factors drove the decline:

Military obsolescence: The timar-holding cavalry, armed with bow, sword, shield, and lance, was a mediaeval army whose day passed when it encountered German infantry equipped with firearms. The sipahis considered firearms unbecoming to their chivalric sense; the state therefore expanded the Janissary corps (from 16,000 in Suleyman’s time to 37,000 by 1609) while the number of sipahis collapsed — from at least 87,000 in Suleyman’s reign to 45,000 by 1609, and only about 8,000 by 1630 according to Koçi Bey.

Financial crisis: The Price Revolution wiped out the real value of fixed cash incomes from timars. Sipahis found they could no longer afford to campaign; they abandoned their timars or sold their rights.

Corruption and usurpation: Many timars were acquired by courtiers and converted into private property or vakıfs. Others were granted to the reaya in return for bribes. As the system of fragmented possession broke down, the state’s control over land weakened.

Tax-farming (iltizam — التزام): To raise cash, the state brought timar lands under direct Treasury control and farmed out their revenues to tax-farmers (mültezim — ملتزم). Unlike the sipahi, who had a long-term interest in the productivity of his timar, the tax-farmer was interested only in maximum short-term extraction. This shift devastated the peasantry.

Flight from the land: As taxes increased, security collapsed during the Celali period, and the economic position of agriculture deteriorated, peasants fled the land in ever greater numbers, seeking refuge in cities, joining bandit bands, or migrating out of Anatolia entirely.

The result was a profound transformation of Ottoman rural society. The small, independent peasant family farm — the çift-hane — gave way to large estates (çiftliks — چفتلك) owned by absentee landlords and worked by sharecroppers. The disciplined, state-regulated timar system was replaced by a chaotic and oppressive regime of tax-farming and local strongmen. The productivity of Ottoman agriculture declined, the state’s tax base shrank, and the empire entered a long period of economic and demographic stagnation.

Summary

Inalcik’s analysis of Ottoman markets and agriculture reveals a system that was highly organized, tightly controlled by the state, and remarkably stable during the classical period (1300–1580). The market system — with its bedesten-centred bazaars, its narh price controls, its muhtesib inspectors, and its guild-based distribution — ensured the provisioning of cities and the maintenance of social order. The agricultural system — with its state ownership of land (miri arazi), its çift-hane peasant farms, its detailed fiscal registration, and its timar-based extraction of revenue — provided the economic foundation for the empire’s military and political power.

The crisis of the late sixteenth century — driven by the Price Revolution, military change, currency debasement, and the Celali rebellions — shattered this system. The narh controls could not contain inflation, the timars collapsed under fiscal pressure, the peasants fled the land, and the agricultural economy was transformed from a state-regulated system of small family farms into a regime of tax-farming, latifundia, and rural exploitation. The Ottoman Empire never fully recovered the economic dynamism of its classical age.
