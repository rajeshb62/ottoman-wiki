Ottoman Sufi Orders (Dervish Orders / طريقت)

The Two Worlds of Ottoman Religious Life

Inalcik distinguishes two broad currents of religious life in the Ottoman Empire. Among the educated urban elite and the Palace, high Persian culture and the theosophical mysticism of Ibn al-Arabi (ابن عربى) prevailed. On the frontiers and among the Turcoman tribes, a popular Turkish culture of gazis and dervishes dominated, shaped by shamanist beliefs, tribal custom, and a form of Islam profoundly modified by Central Asian tradition.

The dervishes on the frontier, usually named baba, abdal or ahi, had been coming to Anatolia since the eleventh century with the waves of migrating Turcomans. They were the social and spiritual centres of the Turcoman tribes, like the old Turco-Mongol shamans. Arabic sources describe with amazement the hundred or so dervishes who came to Syria with Barak Baba in 1307: they hung bells and knucklebones around their necks, shaved their beards and let their moustaches grow, carried wooden swords or crooked cudgels, and danced to drums and pipes with vigorous movements.

Major Established Orders (Tarîkat)

Inalcik divides the religious orders of the Ottoman Empire into two main groups. The first group consisted of the established orders, with lodges supported by the income from vakifs which sultans or great men had founded, with a clearly defined organization and fixed rites and ceremonies. The most famous were the Nakşbendîs, the Mevlevîs, the Halvetîs, and their various branches. They usually settled in the cities and drew their novices from the upper ranks of society.

Nakşbendî (نقشبندى)

The Nakşbendî order was founded by Muhammad Nakshband (1317-89). It was among the most widespread of the established orders, maintaining close ties with the ulema and the Ottoman ruling class. The Nakşbendîs emphasized strict adherence to the Sharia alongside mystical practice, which made them generally acceptable to the orthodox ulema. They settled primarily in the cities and drew their membership from the upper ranks of society. The order’s influence grew steadily throughout the classical period, as its emphasis on sobriety, discipline, and conformity to Islamic law gave it a privileged position vis-a-vis the state.

Mevlevî (مولوى) — The Whirling Dervishes

The patron saint of the Mevlevî order was Maulana Jalal al-Din Rumi (1207-73), one of the world’s greatest mystical writers. He grew up in Konya, the Seljuk capital, where Ibn al-Arabi’s thought prevailed. He first achieved fame as a scholar and preacher, but at a certain stage in his life he became a saint, devoting his whole existence to mystic love. His lyrical verse tells how in mystical ecstasy he saw all differences of race, religion and creed disappear.

Rumi recognized music and dance — sema (سماع) — as the most effective means to attain mystic ecstasy. He was not a man to concern himself with rites and ceremonies, but in time a tarikat was founded in his name, taking its final form in the fifteenth century. The Mevlevîs became known as the “whirling dervishes” from their whirling dances in special dress.

When the order was founded its first centre was the “Seat of the Pir” in Konya, a central lodge built around Rumi’s tomb. Rumi’s successors sent representatives to other towns, approaching local governors and the ruling class to secure patronage and vakifs for the lodges. From the fifteenth century the Mevlevîs established themselves in many Ottoman cities as a tarikat appealing to the elite. All the Ottoman sultans, in particular Murad II, Bayezid II, Selim I and Murad III, took a close interest in the Mevlevîs. Murad II founded a large Mevlevî lodge in Edirne.

Mevlevî tekkes in the great cities acted as cultural centres and art academies. Since the ulema forbade the study of Persian in the medreses, the tekkes became centres for the study of Persian language and literature. Establishments called Dar al-Masnavi were founded with this as their sole function. The most famous commentaries on Rumi’s Masnavi were written in Turkey, usually in the light of Ibn al-Arabi’s philosophical system.

Mevlevî-ism established itself among Ottoman intellectuals, and particularly among the bureaucratic class who were imbued with Persian literary and cultural traditions. It was a major factor in creating classical Ottoman literature, which originally drew its main inspiration from Persian.

Halvetî (خلوتى)

The Halvetî order (from halvet, meaning spiritual retreat or seclusion) was among the most widespread of the established orders in the Ottoman Empire. It found particular favour among the Ottoman elite and ruling class. The order spread through a network of tekkes across the empire, and its branches proliferated throughout the classical period. The Halvetîs, like the Nakşbendîs and Mevlevîs, maintained lodges supported by vakif endowments founded by sultans and statesmen.

Kadirî (قادرى)

The Kadirî order, tracing its foundation to the great sufi Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani (d. 1166), was another of the established orders present in Ottoman lands. Like the other major orders, it maintained tekkes in the cities and drew members from across Ottoman society, though it is discussed less extensively in Inalcik’s treatment than the Nakşbendî, Mevlevî, and Halvetî orders, which he identifies as the three most famous.

The Bektaşî (بكتاشى) Order and Its Role

The Bektaşî order was to become the most important popular tarikat, gradually absorbing various other groups of dervishes — the abdal, kalenderî and haydarî — who had been preaching to the people since the fourteenth century.

The founder and patron saint was Hacci Bektas, a disciple of Baba Ishak who had led the rebellion of 1241. He was active in the second half of the thirteenth century in Seljuk Anatolia, on the Ankara-Kayseri trade route, an area with dense Turcoman population. In the Ottoman Empire, Bektasi-ism first became important when it spread among the Turcoman tribes and then when it became the tarikat of the Janissary corps.

Bektasi dervishes appeared on the Ottoman frontiers in Rumelia from the middle of the fourteenth century, adopting as their own Sari Saltuk, who had become the patron saint of the Ottoman gazis in Rumelia. By the fifteenth century, Bektasi-ism had established itself in the Janissary corps. Devsirme children sent to live in Turkish villages to learn Turkish and Islam tended towards popular forms of religion rather than sunni Islam. From the end of the sixteenth century Hacci Bektas was officially recognized as the patron saint of the Janissaries, and a Bektasi father permanently resided with the corps. The Bektaşî order and the Janissary corps became so inseparable that when a new dede was chosen as head of the order, he came to the Janissary barracks in Istanbul to be crowned by the aga of the Janissaries.

Bektasi-ism was particularly strong among Turcoman nomads and in the villages they had established. In the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries many of these nomads settled and founded villages. Bektasi-ism was a major factor in spreading Islam among the native Christian populations of Rumelia. The eclectic nature of this popular tarikat made Islam easily acceptable: it looked tolerantly on all religions, attached importance to the esoteric rather than externals, did not compel observance of Islamic rites such as ritual prayer and fasting, permitted wine-drinking, and allowed women to go unveiled and mix socially with men.

Bektasi-ism was a sect with beliefs composed of various elements drawn from a multitude of sources — from shamanism to the religious beliefs of the Balkan peoples. Shamanist influence is particularly clear in the ecstatic dances, and the ancient Turkish tradition of the ritual meal and remnants of shamanist stone and tree cults continued in Bektaşî rites. Women retained the position of freedom and equality with men which they had enjoyed in pre-Islamic Turkish society. Scholars have noted the influence of Christianity as well: the concept of the Trinity is reflected in the Bektaşî belief that God, Muhammad and Ali are one; bread, wine and cheese were offered to the novice in the ritual admitting him to the order; disciples confessed their sins to the seyh. From the sixteenth century celibacy was incumbent on dervishes living in tekkes.

The arrangement of the order was hierarchical, with the pir or dede at the head, below him the halifes or fathers, then the seyhs, and then the murids or muhibs. In the mid-seventeenth century Evliya Celebi wrote that there were seven hundred Bektaşî tekkes in the Ottoman Empire (likely an exaggeration), and at the beginning of the nineteenth century it was recorded that one-fifth of the population of Istanbul were Bektaşîs, with fourteen tekkes.

Heterodox and Secret Orders

The second group of religious orders were the secret orders, known usually as the Melâmîs or Melâmetîs. They sought not fame but blame and censure, avoided all ostentation and external organization, and their forms of worship were secret and esoteric. They established no links with the state and were opposed to authority. They lived off the fruits of their own labour, accepting no bequest or alms from the state.

Bayramî Order

The Bayramî order was a religious-social movement born of the upheaval after the Battle of Ankara (1402). Its founder was Hacci Bayram (d. 1430), a peasant from near Ankara and a Melâmî dervish. He wanted his disciples to subsist on the fruits of their labour; he and his followers tilled the fields together and disposed of the produce together. Murad II, sympathetic towards the dervishes, pardoned him and granted his disciples tax exemptions.

The Bayramî movement later split into two. One group accepted sunni Islam and state protection; its follower Aksemseddin became seyh to Mehmed the Conqueror and played an important part in the conquest of Istanbul. The other group remained faithful to the traditions of the Melâmîs, remaining a secret sect with links to the guilds in the towns.

Melâmîs and Hamzavîs

The Melâmîs formed a close-knit group around a kutb (pole) — a spiritual leader believed to be the centre of the universe. They organized secret meetings, tried the accused in their own courts, and had no wish to establish links with the state. They required their members to work at a trade and earn an honest living, condemning idleness.

In the sixteenth century, when the Melâmîs began to show Safavid leanings, the government pursued them ruthlessly. In 1529 the kutb Ismail Masuki was captured and with his twelve disciples executed on the Atmeydani in Istanbul. In 1561 a fetva of the seyhulislam Ebussuud condemned to death another Melami, Hamza Bali of Bosnia. Thereafter the Melâmîs were often known as Hamzavîs. Despite persecution, Melami-ism spread in the great cities and eventually even to the ruling classes.

Hurûfîs

The Hurûfî sect was founded by Fadlullah (d. 1394) in Iran. Fadlullah declared himself the manifestation of God and the Mahdi awaited by Muslims, Christians and Jews. He interpreted the Koran according to an ultra-esoteric system based on cabalistic interpretation of its letters, maintaining that “the world is eternal and creation is a continual process.” He did not believe in the hereafter.

Hurûfî-ism spread rapidly in Ottoman towns from the early fifteenth century. In 1444 the Hurûfîs formed a numerous sect in Edirne, and a Persian missionary had influence in the Palace. The sunni ulema reacted sharply; the missionary was burned and many followers had their tongues cut out. Violent persecution continued into the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, and a strong Hurûfî influence is apparent in Bektaşî thought.

The Kizilbaş (قزيلباش)

From the fifteenth century, extreme shiite doctrines from the Safavid order of Ardabil spread among the Turcomans, who became known as Kizilbaş — “red head” — from the red headdress they wore. The Kizilbaş movement was as much social and political as religious, expressing strong Turcoman opposition to Ottoman centralization. In 1511 the Kizilbaş under Sah Kulu raised a terrible rebellion in south-west Anatolia, and Selim I’s merciless repression and his victory at Chaldiran (1514) only temporarily halted the movement.

Kizilbaş beliefs were closely related to Bektaşî-ism, but the Turcomans were fanatical shiites, mixing tribal customs and shamanist beliefs. They recognized as heads of the tarikat the Celebis, claimed descendants of Hacci Bektas. The long struggle with the Kizilbaş strengthened the position of narrow sunni Islam in the Ottoman Empire.

The Tekke and Zaviye System

Dervishes founded innumerable zaviyes (زاويه — hospices) in newly conquered territories, which later became the nuclei of new Turkish villages. Turkish colonization rapidly followed conquests in Thrace as dervishes established these foundations. The government also established zaviyes near mountain passes, fords and bridges to maintain security on the roads, posting derbendcis (guardians of passes) at these locations.

The established orders had tekkes (تكيه — lodges) supported by the income from vakifs. Each order had its own standard and headdress, its own form of recitation and ceremony, and recognized a famous sufi, saint or companion of the Prophet as its patron.

Dervish Orders and the Guilds (Ahilik)

The early Ottoman ahi brotherhoods represented the intersection of guild organization and sufi spirituality. On Osman’s death, the meeting to choose his successor was held in the zaviye of Edebali’s nephew, Hasan the Ahi. Orhan and his son Suleyman later created hundreds of vakifs for these ahis and other dervishes in newly conquered areas, confirming the ahis’ important part in the establishment of the Ottoman state.

The futuwwa (فتوت) ethics which underlay guild organization shared common ground with sufi principles: absolute obedience, modesty, disdain for wealth, regarding greed and competition as the ugliest moral defects. The Melâmîs maintained particular links with the guilds, requiring their members to work at a trade.

Dervish Orders and Colonization

Dervishes played a critical role in the colonization of newly conquered territories. The Ottoman government encouraged Turks from Anatolia, sometimes by forcible deportation, to settle newly conquered lands, and dervishes founded innumerable zaviyes which became the nuclei of new Turkish villages. The Bektasi order was particularly active in spreading Islam among Balkan Christians; its tolerant and eclectic character made Islam easily acceptable to many Balkan peasants.

The Dobrudja region, where Sari Saltuk had been settled in 1261 with about forty Turcoman clans, remained throughout the Ottoman period a centre where Turcoman tribes, gazis and heretical dervishes thrived.

Conflicts with the Ulema

The relationship between the dervish orders and the orthodox ulema was often tense. The ulema regarded certain heterodox beliefs as heretical: the philosophy of monism (the unity of being — vahdet-i vucud), the esoteric interpretation of the Koran, latitudinarian attitudes toward the Sharia, and shiite tendencies all provoked condemnation. Seyh Bedreddin (d. 1416) was classed among the great scholars of Islamic law but was condemned as an extreme batinite who completely ignored the seriat.

The ulema also resented the influence of Ibn al-Arabi, whose works had many Ottoman commentators, though the seyhulislam Kemal Pasazade issued a fetva approving all his works and Selim I built a mausoleum over his tomb. In the sixteenth century, the observatory of Takiyuddin Mehmed was destroyed on the petition of the seyhulislam, who regarded astronomy as irreligious — a sign of the growing power of religious orthodoxy.

From time to time the government conducted investigations of heterodox groups and searched dervish lodges, forbidding wandering dervishes “who raised standards, played on the pipe and drum, and acted contrary to religion in all things” from travelling. The Kizilbaş faced systematic repression, and the Melâmîs and Hurûfîs were ruthlessly persecuted.

The Intellectual Mystical Tradition

Beyond popular dervish religion, mysticism was a main element in the thought of the Ottoman intellectual elite. This tradition goes back to the Seljuk period, when sultans welcomed scholars and mystics fleeing the Mongol invasion. Konya, Kayseri, Aksaray and Sivas became brilliant centres of mystical thought. The illuminism of al-Suhrawardi, the philosophy of Nasir al-Din of Tus, and above all the system of Ibn al-Arabi (d. 1240) — one of the greatest mystical theorists in the Islamic world — were prevalent in intellectual circles.

Ibn al-Arabi’s thought became a dominant influence in Turkish thought. His works had many Ottoman commentators: Davud of Kayseri, Kutbeddin of Iznik and Yazicizade Mehmed of Gallipoli in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, and Bali of Sofya (d. 1533) and Abdullah of Bosnia (d. 1660) in the sixteenth and seventeenth. Mehmed al-Fanari, founder of the Ottoman medrese tradition, clearly betrayed Ibn al-Arabi’s influence.
