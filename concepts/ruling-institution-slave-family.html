The Ruling Institution as Slave-Family (Kapıkulu)

Definition and Central Thesis

Lybyer’s central thesis is that the Ottoman state was built upon two great institutions: the Ruling Institution (the sultan’s personal slave-administration) and the Moslem Institution (the ulema and Sacred Law). Of these, the Ruling Institution was the engine of war, government, and expansion. It comprised the sultan and his family, the officers of his household, the executive officers of government, the standing army (both cavalry and infantry), and a large body of young men being educated for future service. The most vital and characteristic features of this institution were, first, that its personnel consisted — with few exceptions — of men born of Christian parents or the sons of such; and second, that almost every member of the Institution came into it as the sultan’s slave, and remained the sultan’s slave throughout life no matter to what height of wealth, power, and greatness he might attain.

“Perhaps no more daring experiment has been tried on a large scale upon the face of the earth than that embodied in the Ottoman Ruling Institution. Its nearest ideal analogue is found in the Republic of Plato, its nearest actual parallel in the Mameluke system of Egypt.” — Lybyer, ch. II

The Ruling Institution deliberately took slaves and made them ministers of state; it took boys from the sheep-run and the plow-tail and made them courtiers and the husbands of princesses; it took young men whose ancestors had borne the Christian name for centuries, and made them rulers in the greatest of Mohammedan states. It never asked its novices “Who was your father?” or “What do you know?” or even “Can you speak our tongue?”; but it studied their faces and their frames and said, “You shall be a soldier, and if you show yourself worthy, a general,” or, “You shall be a scholar and a gentleman, and if the ability lies in you, a governor and a prime minister.”

The Sultan as Despot

The sultan was the head and center of the institution in every aspect. He gave it its unity, its vigor, and its propelling force. Although his despotic power was limited in many directions (by the Sacred Law, by custom, by the Moslem Institution), it knew no limits with regard to the members and mechanism of this institution. The person, the fortune, the property, and the life of every member lay in his hand. The very word despot (from Greek δεσπότης) means by derivation the master of slaves, and it was only over his kullar (قول, slaves) that the sultan’s power was despotic in the fullest sense.

The absolute character of the sultan’s authority was an element of great strength, but it contained also the possibility of great danger. The sultan held the position of Deity toward his slaves, and needed the omniscience and benevolence of Deity to exercise his power wisely and justly. The only position in the entire scheme filled by the uncertain lot of heredity was the sultanate itself.

The Slave-Family: Kapıkulu

Every one who belonged to the Ruling Institution in any capacity — from gardener to grand vizier, save only the members of the royal family — bore the title of kul (قول), or slave, of the sultan. Nor was this title a mere form: with few exceptions, all members entered the system as actual slaves, and there was nowhere along the line of promotion any formal or real process of emancipation. The power of the sultan over the lives, persons, and property of the members of the institution, and his right to their absolute obedience, bore every mark of having been derived from the idea of slavery.

The whole number in the system in Suleiman’s time is estimated at about eighty thousand. Since losses by war were sometimes tremendous, the average annual renewal required was as much as one-tenth, or between seven and eight thousand.

The institution contained several component parts capable of separate existence:

  • The Janissaries (يڭیچرى, yeniçeri, “new troop”) — the permanent infantry, representing the brute force of the system and its most dangerous element.
  • The Spahis of the Porte (قپوقولی سپاهی لری, kapıkulu sipahileri) — the permanent cavalry, more numerous than the Janissaries, better educated, and less dangerous because of greater opportunities.
  • The hierarchy of governing officials — the viziers, beylerbeys, defterdars, and nishanjis.
  • The acemi oğlanlar (عجمی اوغلان, “foreign youth”) — apprentice Janissaries.
  • The colleges of pages — training many of the Spahis of the Porte and most of the officers of government.

Recruitment: The Four Methods

Entrance to the system came by the door of slavery, which was open regularly only to Christian boys from ten to twenty years of age, with preferred ages between fourteen and eighteen. Four methods were employed:

1. Capture (War Captives)

By the Sacred Law, the sultan was entitled to one-fifth of all captives taken in war. His officers chose, as his share, such young men as seemed suitable for a place in the system. Since by special Ottoman regulation the sultan’s fifth belonged to the church, he paid twenty-five aspers to the church for each slave he took. Captives came from the irregular raids in Austria, the corsair expeditions of the Mediterranean, and the quasi-slave-farms of the Caucasus. The raided belt around the empire — stretching across Austria-Hungary, Southern Poland and Russia — furnished a continuous supply.

2. Purchase

The sultan’s officers purchased youths in the public slave-market of Constantinople. These came from the captives that the Tartars of the Crimea took in great numbers, from the Caucasus region, and from Mediterranean corsair expeditions.

3. Gift

The sultan received a large number of boys as gifts, since it was well known that no presents were more acceptable. Those who desired his favor kept a lookout for suitable youths.

4. Tribute: The Devşirme (دوشیرمه)

The devşirme — the tribute of Christian children — is the most famous method, though it furnished perhaps less than half of the annual recruits. It produced, however, the ablest and highest officials of the system; its youth bore the same relation to those obtained by capture as cultivated fruits of the garden do to those gathered in the woods. The annual levy averaged about three thousand in the sixteenth century.

The Devşirme Process

The levying was accomplished by a regular process. Normally every four years, but oftener in case of need, a body of officials — described by contemporaries as more skilled in judging boys than trained horse-dealers in judging colts — was sent out by the government to the Christian provinces. The whole of the Balkan Peninsula, Hungary, the western coast of Asia Minor, and the southern and eastern shores of the Black Sea were included; the strongest and ablest youths came from the mountain regions inhabited by Albanians and the Southern Slavic peoples.

The recruiting officers were commissioned each to bring in a certain number apportioned out of a total determined at the capital. There was no principle of tithing, and no fixed proportion or number of boys was levied from each village or family. A regular procedure was followed: the officers obtained from the Christian priest of the village a list of baptized boys between the ages of twelve and twenty; all were brought before the officers, who selected the best. Parents who had strong and well-favored sons might lose them all, while those who had weaklings would lose none.

Selection Criteria

All recruits were brought before trained officials, carefully registered, and divided into two classes:

Those who best satisfied the criteria of bodily perfection, muscular strength, and intellectual ability (about one in ten of the whole number) were chosen for a superior quality of training, especially on the intellectual side. These regularly became iç oğlanlar (ايج اوغلان, palace pages) and kapıkulu Spahis of the Porte, and the ablest rose to the great offices of the army and government.

The remainder were destined for a different education, mainly physical. They became acemi oğlanlar (apprentice Janissaries) and later Janissaries. But the ablest of these might also rise to positions as Spahis of the Porte and even as generals and officers of state. Merit was recognized everywhere and regularly led to promotion.

Reaction of the Subject Population

The grief at parting was often heart-breaking. The mother whose son was taken by force to an unknown life among enemies of all she held dear would hardly have suffered more at his death. Yet she might hope to see him one day in possession of great wealth and power. Parents who wished to keep their sons would sometimes marry them in tender years (married boys being ineligible); those with means bought exemption from the recruiting officers.

On the contrary, many parents were glad to have their sons chosen, knowing they would escape from grinding poverty, receive a first-rate training suited to their abilities, and enter upon the possibility of a great career. Some parents came to regard the process as a privilege rather than a burden. Turkish parents envied them the opportunity, and sometimes tried to evade the regulations by paying Christians to take their Moslem sons and declare them as Christian children, so that they might be enrolled as the sultan’s slaves.

The Slave Status and Its Implications

Whether captured, purchased, presented, or levied, the young men who entered the system were the sultan’s slaves — his personal property for life. They were never allowed to forget the responsibilities of their condition.

No Inheritance Rights

If a kul was executed (a common fate for those who fell from favor), all his property went to the sultan. When one of the sultan’s slaves died leaving children, the sultan sealed up the property and took the tenth part for himself before distributing the rest to the children — the nine-tenths being given by favor rather than right. If the slave had no sons or daughters, the sultan took the whole estate.

No Marriage Without Permission

The Janissaries were not supposed to marry, although the rule was not strictly enforced. The Spahis of the Porte probably married late, if at all, giving the whole system a monastic aspect. High officials kept harems, but their children were ordinarily few. Frequent wars, the danger of execution, and confiscation of property put a check on family establishment.

Absolute Dependence

They must to the end of their days go where the sultan chose to send them, obey his slightest wish, submit to disgrace as readily as to promotion, and — though in the highest office of state — accept death by his order from the hands of their humblest fellow-slaves. The viziers of Selim I carried their wills in their bosoms; the heads of seven are said to have fallen at his command.

“Being all slaves by condition, and slaves of a single lord, from whom alone they hope for greatness, honor, and riches, and from whom alone on the other hand they fear punishment, chastisement, and death, what wonder that in his presence and in rivalry with each other they will do stupendous things?” — Venetian observer Andrea Gritti, 1503

The Harem and Eunuchs

Suleiman’s harem contained about three hundred women, kept in a separate palace well fortified and guarded. All its inmates except his children were purchased or presented slaves. These women, brought for the most part from the region of the Caucasus, including some of the fairest female captives of many lands, were nearly all daughters of Christians. Khurrem (Roxelana) herself was a Russian.

The imperial harem partook of the characteristics of the schools of pages. There were two odalar for recruits of the harem, where they were taught housework, sewing and embroidery, manners and deportment. Those with a taste for music and dancing learned those accomplishments; those who were studious learned to read and write. All were carefully instructed in the system of Islam. Like the pages, nearly all of them passed out of the palace at the age of twenty-five, being given in marriage to Spahis of the Porte or to other officials. Thus the harem served as a training-school of slave-wives for the sultan himself and for the most highly honored of his kullar.

The royal family itself was part of the slave system. The mothers of the sultan’s children were slaves; the sultan himself was the son of a slave; his daughters were married to men who bore indelibly the title of kul.

Eunuchs

Although the Sacred Law strongly disapproved of the employment of eunuchs, that unfortunate class was thought too useful to be dispensed with entirely. Some were white, brought mainly from the Caucasus region; the great majority were negroes brought from Africa. Tribute children seem rarely to have been made eunuchs. Several important offices of state among the “men of the pen” were held by eunuchs, and now and then one rose to high place in the army or administration.

Other Ottoman Slave-Families

Every great officer of the Ottoman court built up a slave-family after the model of the Ruling Institution. The grand vizier had a very large establishment; the viziers somewhat smaller ones; the governors of provinces had households in proportion to their incomes; each adult prince kept a miniature government.

The grand vizier Ibrahim, Iskender Chelebi the defterdar, and Rustem Pasha each maintained educational systems, standing armies, courts, and governing establishments modeled on the sultan’s. Such imitation might easily become a danger, but a prompt remedy ordinarily existed: every such household was strictly personal, gathered about a living man who was himself a slave of the sultan. Let him show the least movement toward treason, and his head would be removed, his property would come to his master, his household would be incorporated with the central slave-family.

Moslem private citizens also kept slave-families as numerous as they could afford, emulating the missionary and educational character of the greater households, but never daring to attempt imitation of the military features. The whole Ottoman system discouraged great accumulations of wealth so effectively that private citizens could never hope to compete with the power of officials and the sultan.

Relation to the Mamluk System

The Mamluk system of Egypt (1250-1517) is the nearest actual parallel to the Ottoman Ruling Institution. Like the Ottoman system, the Mamluks recruited their ruling class from purchased slaves (primarily from the Caucasus and the steppe lands), trained them in a rigorous military and religious education, and placed them in complete control of the state. However, the Ottoman system differed crucially in that the sultan’s own authority remained supreme above his slaves, whereas the Mamluk system became a self-perpetuating military aristocracy in which even the sultan was chosen from among the slave-officers. The Ottoman system subdued and outlived the Mamluk system, incorporating Egypt after 1517 while adapting the Mamluk governing structure under Ottoman pashas.

Relation to the Crimean Tartars

The Crimean Khanate served as a vital external source of slaves for the Ottoman system. The Tartars conducted regular slave-raiding expeditions into the steppe lands north of the Black Sea — Southern Poland, Russia, and the Caucasus — and the captives were sold in the slave-markets of Caffa (Kefe) and Constantinople. The Crimean khans, though vassals of the Ottoman sultan, maintained their own dynastic government and did not themselves participate in the devşirme system; their contribution to the Ruling Institution was through the market, supplying many of the purchased slaves who entered the sultan’s service, particularly from the Caucasus region.

Character of Ottoman Slavery

Ottoman slavery was a very different institution from that which Anglo-Saxons have practised. In it there could ordinarily be no color-line, and therefore no ineffaceable distinction. Where difference in color existed, it counted for nothing, by old Islamic custom.

Nor did the fact of slavery impart any indelible taint. Islam knew slave and free, but it knew no intermediate class of freedmen. Private citizens had the power of emancipation and often exercised it as a meritorious act. The slave who was set free was immediately in possession of full rights. Slavery had therefore no inherent quality; it was merely an accidental misfortune from which complete recovery was possible. The idea of Aristotle — that some men are born to be slaves — was wholly absent.

The Moslem religion teaches kindness and benevolence to all but armed enemies of the faith. Moslem masters, in constant personal association with persons whose condition led them to strive to please, were apt to become very friendly toward them. Emancipation was common, and not only the sultan but many of his subjects did not hesitate to give their daughters in marriage to worthy slaves. A slave was often beloved above a son — a son possessed a character which was more or less a matter of chance, but a slave had been selected.

Distinct from Classical and Plantation Slavery

Ottoman slavery was fundamentally distinct from classical (Greco-Roman) slavery in several respects:

  • Race and color: Roman slavery often carried ethnic and cultural stigmas that persisted even after manumission; Ottoman slavery had no color-line and no hereditary taint.
  • Assimilation: The Ottoman system was a machine for assimilation and conversion, actively incorporating slaves into the ruling nation; classical slavery tended to maintain a permanent servile class.
  • Occupational range: Ottoman slaves could and did reach the highest offices of state — grand vizier, general, governor; in classical and plantation slavery, such ascent was virtually impossible.
  • No chattel permanence: In plantation slavery (particularly the Atlantic system), the slave condition was lifelong, hereditary, and racially defined. In the Ottoman system, the children of kullar were free Moslems and could not inherit their father’s position in the Ruling Institution. The system was self-consuming: it did not perpetuate a slave caste.

Thus the Ottoman Ruling Institution was, as Lybyer concludes, “a wonderful fabric of slavery” — perhaps the largest, most powerful, and most honorable slave-family in history, better provided for and rewarded, more obedient and more contented, than any other the world has known.

The Missionary Motive

Although almost every member of the governing group in the Ottoman Empire had been born a Christian, it was absolutely necessary for his advancement that he should profess the Moslem faith. The fundamental rule was very simple: every member of the Ruling Institution must have been born a Christian and must have become a Mohammedan.

The missionary purpose stands out clearly in the words attributed to Kara Khalil Chendereli, the traditional founder of the Janissary corps:

“The conquered are slaves of the conquerors, to whom their goods, their women, and their children belong as a lawful possession; in converting the children to Islam by force, and in enrolling them as soldiers in the service of the faith, one is working for their happiness in this world and their eternal salvation.”

Mohammed the Conqueror expressed the idea poetically: “Our empire is the home of Islam; from father to son the lamp of our empire is kept burning with oil from the hearts of the infidels.”

The Requirement of Conversion

Conversion was a principal object of the system, and favor and promotion waited as rewards upon acceptance of the Moslem faith. Yet conversion of the neophytes seems not ordinarily to have been forcible. The Ottomans were too wise to believe that the best results could be accomplished by such means. Their policy was rather to throw every difficulty in the way of remaining a Christian, and to offer every inducement to make the Moslem faith and system seem attractive. The educational scheme involved complete isolation from Christian ideas of every sort and complete saturation in all the ideas of Mohammedanism — religious, moral, social, and political. Even those whose education was mainly physical were isolated from Christians in a strict Moslem environment.

Sincerity of Conversion

Not all who turned Moslem did so without mental reservation. The grand vizier Rustem told Busbecq, after offering him great rewards if he would turn Moslem, that he believed in the salvation of those of other faiths. A deli (scout) in his service confided to a French gentleman that while he pretended to follow Mohammedanism, he was a Christian at heart. The probability is that large numbers of the sultan’s slaves were merely nominal Mohammedans in religious belief, though they necessarily followed the larger part of the Moslem scheme of life.

The Two Great Institutions Compared

Lybyer’s framework requires understanding the Ruling Institution in contrast to the Moslem Institution. The Ruling Institution consisted of men born of Christian parents who became slaves of the sultan; they wielded the sword, the pen, and the scepter, conducting the whole of government except the rendering of justice in matters controlled by the Sacred Law. The Moslem Institution consisted of men born of Moslem parents, born and brought up free; they were the educators, priests, jurisconsults, and judges who embodied and maintained Mohammedan learning, religion, and law.

As Marcantonio Barbaro, Venetian Bailo from 1568 to 1573, first discerned clearly: “The arms and the forces are wholly reposed in the hands of persons all born Christians” while “the administration of the laws is all solely in the hands of those who are born Turks.”
