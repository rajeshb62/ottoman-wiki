Ottoman City Structure (İmâret, Mahalle, and the Vakıf System)

Overview

The Ottoman city, as described by Halil Inalcik, was shaped by three interlocking forces: the imâret system of pious foundations, the vakıf (pl. evkaf) endowment mechanism that financed them, and the mahalle (neighbourhood) as the basic unit of social organization. Following the near-eastern imperial tradition, Ottoman sultans sought to transform their capitals — Bursa (بروسه), Edirne (ادرنه), and Istanbul (استانبول) — into great cities by increasing their population, building monumental religious and commercial complexes, and encouraging their development as trading centres. Istanbul, rebuilt after the 1453 conquest, became the model Ottoman city and, by the first half of the sixteenth century, the largest city in Europe with a population reaching four hundred thousand souls.

The Imâret (عمارت) — The Urban Nucleus

The imâret was the foundational institution of Ottoman urbanism. Inalcik describes it as a “complex of institutions — mosque, medrese, hospital, traveller’s hostel, water installations, roads and bridges — founded with pious or charitable motives, and the institutions which provided revenue for their upkeep, such as an inn, market, caravanserai, bath house, mill, dyehouse, slaughterhouse or soup kitchen.” The religious and charitable buildings (cami, medrese, imaret proper, hospital) were grouped around a mosque, while the commercial establishments (bedesten, han, shops, hamam) stood nearby or in some suitably active location.

These imârets gave Ottoman towns their peculiar character. Until recently, they dominated the skyline of cities in Anatolia and the Balkans. A new city quarter grew up around each imâret: the founder built a mosque, endowed shops and markets to generate revenue, and within a short time people settled nearby and founded new mahalles. As Inalcik writes of Istanbul, “buildings devoted to the public good were erected around the mosque bearing the donor’s name, and within a short time people settled near these imârets and founded new quarters. Istanbul thus took on its characteristic Turkish appearance.”

Physical Components of the Ottoman City

Sur (سور) — The City Walls

The Ottoman city inherited its defensive perimeter from earlier Byzantine, Roman, or pre-Ottoman fortifications. Inalcik notes that the storming of city walls was the decisive military event in conquest — most famously the walls of Constantinople in 1453. After conquest, the Ottomans maintained and repaired existing walls but did not always enclose new neighbourhoods that grew organically outside the old walls. The Eyüp district of Istanbul, for example, grew up outside the land walls around the mosque and tomb of Ebû Eyyûb el-Ensârî, becoming “a large, separate town.”

Mahalle (محله) — The Neighbourhood

The mahalle was the fundamental unit of urban social organization. Inalcik writes: “Away from work … the members of different faiths lived apart in separate quarters of the city, under the headship of their own religious leaders. Ottoman cities always had separate Muslim, Christian and Jewish quarters, the gypsies too having their own district, regardless of their religion.”

Each mahalle was organized around its place of worship. In a Muslim ward, the imam (امام) served as the religious head of the community, while a kethüdâ (كتخدا) acted as its secular representative and spokesman before the government. Priests and rabbis performed the same functions in non-Muslim quarters. The imam’s role was crucial: he recorded births, marriages, and deaths; he represented his mahalle to the kâdî (قاضی); and he witnessed the daily life of the quarter. The mosque of the mahalle — usually a small neighbourhood mosque (mescid, مسجد) rather than a great imperial cami — was the focal point of community life.

This separation of quarters by faith, Inalcik notes, “did not hinder the good relations between Muslims and non-Muslims. Muslim men often married non-Muslim women without the woman having to change her religion. The children, however, had to be Muslim.”

Çarşı (چارشو) — The Market Street

The commercial spine of the Ottoman city was the çarşı — streets lined with shops, each street or section occupied by members of a single craft. Inalcik describes how after the conquest, Mehmed II built 984 shops in the markets surrounding the bedestan. Each group of shops, “branching out and lining both sides of a road, formed a single market, occupied by members of a single craft or by merchants selling the same type of goods. These markets were usually roofed with stone vaults, as in the market at Istanbul, or else they stayed as lanes of open shops, shaded by trees.”

Bedesten (بذستان) — The Covered Market

The bedesten was the commercial heart of every significant Ottoman city. Inalcik describes it as a “monumental building, with its stone domes and iron doors resisting fire and pillage, serving to protect not only valuable commercial goods but also the money and jewellery of the city’s rich.” Its doorkeepers, night-watchmen, and brokers were under government control.

The construction of a bedesten typically played a decisive role in the growth of an Ottoman city. Orhan Gazi had built one in Bursa as early as 1340, which “to this day has remained as the commercial centre of the town.” Trade centres grew up around bedestens in all large Ottoman towns of the Balkans: Tatar Pazarjik, Plovdiv, Sarajevo, Sofia, Skopje, Monastir, Serres, and Salonica. Evliya Çelebi, the great seventeenth-century traveller, divided Ottoman cities into two categories — those with bedestens and those without.

Mehmed II’s bedesten in Istanbul had 118 shops with storerooms inside; the surrounding markets added 984 shops. This became the main business centre of Istanbul, known today as the Kapalıçarşı (Covered Market).

Han (خان) / Kervansaray (كاروانسرای) — The Caravanserai

The han or caravanserai was the lodging and warehouse for travelling merchants. Inalcik describes the great caravanserais as “strongly fortified against brigands.” The grand vizier İbrahim Paşa built one at Tatar-Pazarjik which “according to Evliyâ Çelebi dominated the city like a fortress. It contained two hundred rooms and eighty suites where important men could stay with their families. The rooms surrounded a courtyard shaded by a great tree, and an outer courtyard, with a pond in the centre, could accommodate five or six thousand horses.”

The han provided free lodging and food for three days. Busbecq, the Habsburg ambassador, wrote of one in Niş: “They have several distinct apartments for lodging in them. There is no man to forbid the use of them, either Christian or Jew, rich or poor, they are open equally to all.”

Cami (جامع) and Külliye (كلية) — The Mosque and Mosque Complex

The great imperial mosque (cami) was the symbolic and physical centre of the Ottoman city. Around it was arranged the külliye — the full complex of charitable and educational institutions: medreses (مدارس, theological schools), a sıbyan mektebi (مكتب, primary school), a kütüphane (كتابخانه, library), a darüşşifa (دارالشفا, hospital), tabhane (طبخانه, hostel for travellers), and a imaret (عمارت, public kitchen). The külliye of Mehmed II in Istanbul (1463-1470) included eight medreses, a school for children, a library, a hospital, two hostels for travellers, and a refectory, supported by a market of 318 shops nearby. Six hundred students studied in the medreses and each day the hostels accommodated 160 travellers.

Hamam (حمام) — The Bathhouse

The hamam was a standard component of the imâret complex and an essential feature of every Ottoman neighbourhood. Inalcik notes that Mehmed II built thirteen bathhouses as part of his vakıf endowments in Istanbul. The hamam served both hygienic and social functions — it was a place of cleansing before prayer, a meeting place, and an income-generating property whose revenues supported the charitable institutions of the vakıf. Income from bathhouses frequently funded the maintenance of bridges, hostels, and mosques, as when Murad II used the revenues from “a boza shop, bath-house and shops” to maintain the great Ergene bridge at Uzunköprü.

The Vakıf (وقف) System

Inalcik devotes considerable attention to the vakıf as the financial engine of Ottoman urbanism. In Islamic society, charitable institutions were nearly always established as vakıfs. This ensured their continued existence, since a vakıf devoted in perpetuity the profits from any source to some charitable purpose, “without impairing the capital.” It was a legal fiction that from the time of its endowment only God had proprietary rights over a vakıf, so that “even if governments or states should change the continuance of the public service was guaranteed.”

How a Vakıf Worked

The founder (vâkıf, واقف) drew up a vakfiye (وقفیه) — the deed of endowment — before a kâdî, entered it in his register, and confirmed it with the sultan. The vakfiye determined the endowment’s purpose, conditions, and forms of management, and appointed a mütevellî (متولی) — chief trustee. In large vakıfs a nâzır (ناظر) — superintendent — was also appointed to inspect whether the endowment’s conditions were being fulfilled.

The mütevellî was responsible for collecting and growing the vakıf’s income and using the funds to maintain the foundation’s buildings, pay its employees, and fulfil the conditions of the endowment. Once a year, the principal officers and employees of the vakıf met to audit themselves and could request the mütevellî’s dismissal. The state, through the local kâdî or a specially appointed inspector, also audited the accounts of each vakıf. “The aim of all these precautions,” Inalcik writes, “was to ensure that the institution continued to fulfil its proper function.”

The Temlik System

An important factor in the foundation of urban vakıfs was the institution of temlik (تملیك) — the royal grant of property rights. Statesmen or Palace women could apply to the sultan with a project for a charitable undertaking and receive from him the freehold of a piece of land, in some cases including several villages. They then bequeathed these lands to the charitable enterprise, assuring not only their own salvation in the next world but a continuous livelihood for their descendants, who were appointed mütevellîs. Inalcik notes that “the second and third generation descendants of Ottoman notables frequently enjoyed a comfortable retirement as mütevellîs of vakıfs, forming a kind of a class of absentee landlords.”

Scale of the Vakıf System in Istanbul

According to an official survey of 1546, there were 2,517 vakıfs founded by non-royal persons in Istanbul, with 1,600 new vakıfs added in the following half-century. To meet the expenses of his foundations alone, Mehmed II built markets (260 shops in Galata and 783 in Istanbul, not counting those in and around the bedesten), thirteen bathhouses, a number of dyehouses, bakehouses, warehouses, candleworks, oil-presses, and fifty-four mills. The revenues assigned to Hagia Sophia alone produced a yearly income of thirteen thousand gold ducats.

Inalcik summarizes: “The system of vakıfs created the cultural and commercial complexes in Istanbul.”

Istanbul: The Model Ottoman City

Inalcik presents Istanbul as the paradigmatic Ottoman city, the fullest expression of the imâret system and the model that lesser cities imitated. After the conquest of 1453, when the population had fallen to between thirty and forty thousand, Mehmed II undertook a deliberate programme of repopulation and construction:

  1. Repopulation: He induced refugees to return by restoring their property and guaranteeing freedom of worship. He ordered provincial governors to send four thousand families from Rumelia and Anatolia. He forcibly settled merchants and artisans from conquered cities — Amasra (1459), Trebizond (1461), Karaman (1470s), Caffa (1475) — in different quarters of the city.

  2. Founding of Imârets: In 1459, he assembled the empire’s leading men and required each to create an imâret in any part of the city he wished. The grand vizier Mahmud Paşa and other viziers constructed fine complexes in the centre and around the Golden Horn.

  3. Food Supply: The provisioning of Istanbul became a major factor in creating an integrated imperial economy. The city’s ovens consumed 250 tons of wheat daily by the mid-seventeenth century, and 2,000 food-carrying ships arrived each year. The state regulated everything from producer to retailer to prevent profiteering and inflation.

By the census of 1477, Istanbul had 16,324 families (about 80,000-100,000 people). By the first half of the sixteenth century, the population reached four hundred thousand — larger than any city in Europe. Inalcik notes that “40 per cent of the population was non-Muslim” and that “in the mid-seventeenth century there were in Istanbul 152 Mosques, 126 medreses, a hundred caravanserais and about a thousand mansions which sultans, pashas and lesser persons had founded.”

Key Points from Inalcik

  • The imâret was the nucleus of every Ottoman city: a complex of mosque, medrese, hospital, hostel, and commercial buildings whose revenues came from vakıf endowments.
  • The mahalle was organized by religion and ethnicity; each had its imam (for Muslims) or priest/rabbi (for non-Muslims) and its kethüdâ (secular representative).
  • The bedesten was the defining sign of a major city; Evliya Çelebi divided cities into those with and without bedestens.
  • The vakıf system was autonomous under Islamic law but the Ottoman state controlled and audited all endowments to ensure they fulfilled their purposes.
  • Istanbul was the model: deliberately repopulated by Mehmed II, built through imârets ordered from state officials, and sustained by a centralized provisioning system that integrated the entire empire.
  • The physical structure — sur, mahalle, çarşı, bedesten, han, cami/külliye, hamam — was replicated in Ottoman cities from Sarajevo to Bursa to Damascus.
