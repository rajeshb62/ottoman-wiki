The Janissaries Under Mehmed II

Origins and the Devsirme System

The Janissary corps (yeniçeri, يڭیچرى, “new troop”) was the standing infantry of the Ottoman sultans, raised through the devşirme (دوشیرمه, “collecting”) — the child levy system that took Christian boys from the Balkan and Anatolian subject populations, converted them to Islam, and trained them for state service. The devşirme was not a slave trade in the ordinary sense: the boys were selected for their physical and intellectual promise, raised in Turkish households to learn the language and faith, and then funneled into either the palace bureaucracy or the Janissary barracks. They became, in law and in fact, the personal property of the sultan — his kapıkulu (قپوقولی, “slave of the Porte”) — bound to him by a loyalty that owed nothing to clan, region, or aristocratic lineage.

The institution predated Mehmed. It was established during the reign of Murad I (1362–1389) and formalized under Murad II. But it was Mehmed II who transformed the Janissaries from a palace guard into the nucleus of a professional imperial army.

Mehmed’s Expansion of the Corps After 1451

When Mehmed II ascended the throne for the second time in 1451 — after a brief, turbulent first reign as a boy-sultan followed by his father Murad’s return — he moved immediately to consolidate power. Among his first acts was a thorough review of the state’s military and financial apparatus. Kritoboulos, the Greek historian of Imbros who wrote a panegyric life of Mehmed, describes the scene:

“Then he set about reviewing his entire empire, establishing law in the provinces of the nations under him, and removed some of the governors and installed others in their places, whom he considered superior to the others in military talent and intelligence, valor and integrity of soul.”

Kritoboulos then specifies Mehmed’s particular attention to the Janissaries:

“And then he inspected the military registers and troops, both cavalry and infantry, as well as the armies paid from the imperial treasury, but he devoted particular care to the imperial bodyguard, and also increased the forces, namely the so-called free infantry, and the troop around him, whom in their own language it is customary to call Janissaries, which means literally ‘new troop,’ knowing well that by this his power and the force protecting his life and the entire empire would increase.”

This passage, in Book I, section 26 of Kritoboulos’s history, captures Mehmed’s strategic vision. The Janissaries were not merely soldiers — they were the sultan’s personal instrument, a force answerable to no one but him, financed directly from the imperial treasury rather than through the timar (تیمار, land-grant) system that supported the provincial cavalry (sipahi, سپاهی). By expanding the corps, Mehmed was building a military establishment that would be loyal to the throne, not to the old Turkish aristocracy or the provincial notables.

The Janissary Discipline Incident

Mehmed’s relationship with the Janissaries was not one of simple patronage; it was forged through an iron discipline that he imposed early in his reign. Tursun Beg, the Ottoman bureaucrat and historian who served in Mehmed’s chancery, records a revealing episode that took place as the sultan set out on his first campaign after his second accession.

The Janissary corps lined the road in two armed ranks. When the sultan passed among them, they demanded a gift — a bonus for the campaign. Tursun Beg writes:

“The Janissary corps stood on the road, in two armed ranks. When the sultan came among them, they said: ‘This is our sultan’s first campaign — his servants require a gift.’ Yet the sultan’s gifts had already submerged the world in bounty. Raising his servants’ salaries was a matter that shone in his heart and constantly occupied his mind.”

What seems like a traditional request for a donative was, in Mehmed’s eyes, a provocation — a breach of military order, a test of his authority. Tursun continues:

“But their standing on the road in this improper manner and their impatient demanding of a grant did not sit well with his fine disposition, and he referred the matter to the court scholars (kapu uleması). When he encamped, a council (divan) was held. He commanded that their sergeants (yayabaşı) be brought in and said: ‘This rudeness is due to the deficiency of your wits,’ and having them subdued, gave each a hundred strokes of the rod, dismissed them, and drove them from his gate.”

The message was unmistakable. The men who had lined the road to pressure their young sultan were not punished collectively — their sergeants were publicly beaten and dismissed. Tursun concludes: “With this policy, the love of the sultan became established in the hearts of both those who [desired] justice and order and of others, and until the end of his reign they never set foot beyond the path of discipline.”

This was vintage Mehmed: he understood that the Janissaries’ power to make or break a sultan would grow with their numbers and prestige, and he met that challenge head-on, before they had tested him in war. The discipline held for thirty years.

The Janissaries in the Siege of Constantinople (1453)

When Mehmed’s massive army encamped before the Theodosian Walls in April 1453, the Janissaries were the elite strike force. Tursun Beg describes the camp: “The camp was set up according to Ottoman order, and the Janissary tents formed a circle, like the moon encompassing the new moon.”

During the siege itself, the Janissaries were assigned the sector of the wall at the Lycus Valley, the most critical point of assault. Tursun notes that as the siege progressed: “The gate-people and the Janissary corps had completed their duties, beaten down and demolished the areas assigned to them, and filled in and destroyed the deep moats.”

But it was in the final general assault of May 29, 1453, that the Janissaries played their decisive role. Kritoboulos gives the most detailed account. After the first waves of irregulars (azabs) and Anatolian troops had been repulsed with heavy losses, Mehmed held back his best soldiers:

“Emperor Mehmed, having seen that the troops engaged in the assault had suffered much in the battle and achieved no notable result, while the Romans and Italians fought strongly and triumphed in the combat, being greatly angered at this and not considering the matter tolerable any longer, brings forward all his troops, which he had kept for later: the best armed, the most daring, the most reliable, and by far more capable than the others in experience and strength — heavy infantry, archers, lancers, and the guard corps formed from the other troops and the so-called Janissaries, who constituted the flower of the camp.”

Then Mehmed himself led them forward: “He himself leads them, going at the very front, to the foot of the stone wall, as far as the ditch.” After the Genoese commander Giustiniani Longo was mortally wounded and his men abandoned their posts, the Janissaries and the other elite troops pushed through the palisade, entered the city through the Kerkoporta gate, and streamed into Constantinople.

Once inside, Kritoboulos records: “The Janissaries and other soldiers broke forcibly into the houses, without any order or plan.” The corps that Mehmed had expanded, disciplined, and personally led into battle had delivered him the greatest prize in Christendom.

From Slave-Soldiers to Political Force

Under Mehmed, the Janissaries evolved from a household guard of a few thousand into a standing army that, by the end of his reign, numbered perhaps 10,000–12,000 men. The expansion was deliberate: Mehmed needed a permanent, professional infantry to garrison his new capital, man the frontiers, and serve as a counterweight to the provincial cavalry and the old Turkish nobility.

But this expansion had a latent political logic. The Janissaries were, by design, detached from the networks of kinship and patronage that structured Ottoman society. They had no families in the normal sense — they were forbidden to marry until the later 15th century, and their children were excluded from the corps. They lived in barracks, received regular salaries from the treasury, and owed their advancement solely to the sultan’s favor.

Yet the very qualities that made them loyal also made them dangerous. The Janissaries were a corps with a strong corporate identity. They policed their own ranks, had their own traditions and ceremonies, and developed a keen sense of their collective rights — including the right to petition the sultan for better pay or conditions. The discipline incident at Mehmed’s accession showed that even as a young sultan, Mehmed understood the need to keep this corporate spirit in check. His solution was to mix ferocious punishment with generous reward: he raised their salaries (as Tursun notes, “Raising his servants’ salaries was a matter that shone in his heart”), gave them privileged access to the sultan’s person, and rewarded them lavishly from the plunder of Constantinople and later campaigns.

The Janissary Uprising After Mehmed’s Death

The structure Mehmed built was strong enough to contain the Janissaries during his lifetime, but it could not survive his death. When Mehmed died in 1481 — possibly poisoned, possibly from gout or complications of his incessant campaigning — the Janissaries immediately asserted themselves as kingmakers.

The succession pitted Mehmed’s two sons against each other: Bayezid II, the elder, who served as governor of Amasya, and Cem Sultan, the younger, who held the governorship of Karaman. Mehmed had reportedly favored Cem, but the Janissaries threw their weight behind Bayezid. When the grand vizier, Karamani Mehmed Pasha — a devout and capable administrator who had served Mehmed II loyally — attempted to control the succession and keep order in the capital, the Janissaries turned on him.

Karamani Mehmed Pasha was dragged from the vizieral palace and lynched by the Janissaries in the streets of Constantinople. His body was left in the public square. The uprising was a watershed moment: the corps that Mehmed had created as the sultan’s loyal instrument had, in the absence of a strong hand, become the arbiter of the succession. They demanded — and received — a substantial donative from Bayezid upon his accession, establishing a precedent that would haunt later sultans. The discipline that Tursun Beg had praised — “until the end of his reign they never set foot beyond the path of discipline” — shattered within days of the Conqueror’s death.

Legacy

Mehmed II’s relationship with the Janissaries encapsulates the paradox of the institution he did so much to shape. He expanded their numbers, elevated their status, and relied on them as the cutting edge of his conquests. But the discipline he imposed was personal — it depended on his own authority, his own presence, his own willingness to have sergeants beaten in the dust. After him, the Janissaries became a praetorian guard in the full Roman sense: the power behind the throne, capable of making and unmaking sultans, demanding donatives at every accession, and eventually, by the 17th century, resisting the very military reforms that might have saved the empire.

In the century of Mehmed, however, the Janissaries were still the new troop — young, disciplined, loyal to a sultan who had beaten their officers and led them through the breach. They were, as Kritoboulos wrote, “the flower of the camp,” and with them, Mehmed conquered an empire.
