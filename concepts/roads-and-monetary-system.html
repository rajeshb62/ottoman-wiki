Ottoman Roads and Monetary System

Overview

The Ottoman Empire of the classical age (1300-1600) maintained an extensive road network connecting the imperial capital Istanbul to the furthest reaches of its domains, and administered a bimetallic monetary system centred on the silver akçe. The road system, praised by the historian Konstantin Jireček as the finest since the Roman Empire, was built on Roman foundations and sustained by a sophisticated network of caravanserais, imârets, and zâviyes funded through pious endowments (vakıf). The monetary system, anchored by the akçe — the standard silver coin first minted at Bursa in 1327 — provided a stable medium of exchange through most of the 16th century before being overwhelmed by the flood of American silver and the consequent price revolution of the 1580s.

1. The Empire’s Road Network (شبکه راه‌ها)

Istanbul was the terminus not only of sea lanes but also of the great caravan routes that bound the empire together. Three principal highways connected the Balkans to Istanbul, while Anatolian and Arab routes radiated eastward and southward.

1.1 The Balkan Highways

Three great roads linked the Balkans to the capital:

  • The Via Egnatia — The ancient Roman road from the Adriatic ports of Albania (Durazzo, Valona) through Ohrida (Ohrid), Monastir (Bitola), and Salonica (Thessaloniki) to Istanbul.
  • The Great Military Road — The most important artery of the European provinces, running from Belgrade through Sofia and Plovdiv (Philippopolis) to Edirne (Adrianople) and finally Istanbul. This was the route of imperial campaigns into Hungary and central Europe.
  • The Danubian Road — Running from the lower Danube region through the Tundza valley and Edirne, connecting the northern frontier to the capital.

The Ottomans kept the Roman road between Istanbul and Belgrade in constant repair, improving it with roughly hewn stone and building entirely new sections. Wagons could travel from Belgrade to Istanbul in one month; in 1566, Selîm II covered the same distance in fifteen days on horseback.

1.2 The Anatolian and Eastern Routes

The caravan route from Iran and Anatolia reached Istanbul through Bolu, Izmit (Nicomedia), and Gebze. The road from Ankara joined this artery near Izmit. The pilgrim road from Arabia and Aleppo connected with the highway from Iran at Gebze, crossing the Gulf of Izmit at Dil, beyond Iznik (Nicaea). In the 15th and 16th centuries, the usual route from Istanbul to Bursa went by sea through Mudanya; by the 17th century, the overland route from Gebze via Dil quay to Bursa had become more important.

The routes from Istanbul to Damascus, Erzurum, and Belgrade were lined with wells, fountains, places of prayer, and small guest houses funded by local benefactors as pious endowments, so that travellers had all their needs provided for, usually free of charge.

1.3 The Pilgrimage and Southern Routes

After the conquest of Syria, Egypt, and the Hejaz in 1516-17, the Ottomans became protectors of Mecca and Medina and guardians of the pilgrimage (hajj) routes. The pilgrim road from Damascus to Mecca was one of the most strategically important arteries of the empire, carrying tens of thousands of pilgrims annually. Customs levied on spices brought by the pilgrim caravans at Damascus rose to 110,000 gold ducats in 1562.

The Egyptian route was equally vital: provisions for the imperial palace — rice, wheat, barley, spices, and sugar — came by galleon from Egypt. Sudanese gold reached Istanbul through Egypt, and the imperial Treasury drew the surplus of the Egyptian budget, amounting to 500,000 gold ducats annually, which the central government insisted on receiving as gold.

2. Road Construction and Maintenance (راه‌سازی و نگهداری)

Certain villages along the main routes had the duty of constructing and maintaining roads, receiving exemption from extraordinary taxes (avâriz) in return. The state also appointed derbendcis (guardians of passes / دربندجی) near mountain passes, fords, and bridges. Entire villages were made responsible for the security and upkeep of a particular road or bridge, with tax exemptions as compensation. In the mid-16th century, the state appointed 2,288 village families in Anatolia and 1,906 in the eastern Balkans as derbendcis. If they deserted their posts, they were brought back by force.

2.1 The Menzil System (منزل / Post-Station System)

The Ottomans inherited and refined the Islamic postal-relay system. Stations (menzil) were established at regular intervals along the principal roads, where government couriers (ulak / اولاق) could exchange horses and rest. This system — known collectively as the menzilhane (منزلخانه) system — enabled rapid communication between Istanbul and the provinces. A network of menzil stations linked the capital to the furthest sanjaks and beylerbeyiliks, allowing the central government to maintain tight control over provincial administration. The menzil system operated under strict regulations governing the number of horses, the distances between stations, and the authorisation required to use them.

3. Caravanserais, Imârets, and Zâviyes (کاروانسراها، عمارت‌ها و زاویه‌ها)

To ensure the ease and safety of travel on main roads, the sultans created establishments supported by vakıf endowments and encouraged senior officials to do the same by granting them large estates.

3.1 Caravanserais and Imârets

These were large roadside complexes typically comprising an inn (han / خان), a caravanserai for beasts and their drivers, and often a bridge, a mosque, and a medrese. Murâd II, for example, constructed a great stone bridge 392 metres long with 174 arches over the Ergene River near Edirne in 1443, building at its head a hostel to shelter and feed travellers, a mosque, and a medrese. The expenses were met from the income of a boza shop, bath-house, and shops, supplemented by a caravanserai in Edirne. The population that grew around this complex became the town of Uzunköprü.

Hersek Ahmed Pasha (d. 1517) established a similar foundation on the Istanbul-Damascus road at the first station beyond the Gulf of Izmit, which later became the town of Hersek.

The grand vizier Ibrahîm Pasha built a great caravanserai at Tatar-Pazarjik on the military highway to central Europe. According to Evliyâ Çelebi, it dominated the city like a fortress, containing two hundred rooms and eighty suites for important men and their families. An outer courtyard could accommodate five to six thousand horses. Day and night, servants received all travellers — Muslim and non-Muslim alike. After sundown, each guest received a bowl of soup, a loaf of bread, and a candle; each horse received a bag of oats. After evening prayer the caravanserai’s band played and the gates were closed. Before opening in the morning, the innkeeper asked if anyone had lost anything; if he omitted this step, he was held personally responsible for any losses.

Mehmed the Conqueror’s vakfiye required that all travellers staying at his endowed hostel be well treated and all their needs attended. Hostels offered three days’ free food and lodging, after which the traveller had to leave.

3.2 Scale of Construction

In Bosnia and Herzegovina alone, 232 inns, 18 caravanserais, 32 hostels, 10 bedestans, and 42 bridges were built during the Ottoman period, including the architectural masterpieces of the Mostar bridge (1566), the Sarajevo Kozja bridge (c. 1550), and the Trebinje bridge.

3.3 Zâviyes (زاویه‌ها)

Zâviyes — pious foundations established by a şeyh or dervish — played an essential role in the early Ottoman period, especially along distant and lonely roads and mountain passes. The founder would receive a small parcel of land as freehold property, which he bequeathed to his foundation. The dervishes worked the land, guaranteeing themselves a living and meeting the expenses of the zâviye.

Zâviyes were instrumental in settling Turkish immigrants in newly conquered territories and served simultaneously as shelters for travellers, newly arrived immigrants, and gâzîs. In the mid-16th century, there were 1,100 hospices in Asia Minor alone. By this period, the state abolished those zâviyes no longer situated on roads or performing genuine services for travellers, converting their vakıf land back to state control.

3.4 Security on the Roads

Caravanserais and bedestans were strongly fortified against brigands. The Habsburg ambassador Busbecq (1555) did not take the land route between Buda and Belgrade for fear of bandits called “hayduks.” The situation was better in the 16th century than the 17th, when government troops were often ineffective against large armed bands. In about 1647, the brigand Kara Haydaroğlu raided caravans bound for Istanbul from Iran, Damascus, and Izmir in the mountainous region near Eskişehir, and was overcome only with difficulty.

4. Transport Methods (وسایل حمل و نقل)

In Rumelia and the northern Black Sea region, heavy goods were carried by wagon; in mountainous areas, mules were the normal means of transport. During the Belgrade campaign of 1521, the state hired 30,000 camels from Anatolia and Arabia to transport the army’s heavy baggage, while about 10,000 wagons carried flour and barley from the Danubian regions. Both the state and individual merchants hired camels, horses, and mules from nomadic Turcoman tribes. Persian caravans typically consisted of 300-400 animals, though some had 1,000 or more. In the late 15th century, a horse for the journey from Tabriz to Bursa and back could be rented for about nine gold ducats.

5. The Ottoman Monetary System (نظام پولی عثمانی)

5.1 The Akçe (آقچه) — The Standard Silver Coin

The akçe (آقچه) — a small silver coin weighing approximately 1.15 grams in its classical form — was the chief unit of account in the Ottoman Empire. Orhan, the son of Osman, struck the first Ottoman silver coins in Bursa in 1327 (AH 727). The akçe served as the standard medium for all state transactions — tax payments, salaries, fines, and pensions — and remained the backbone of the monetary system until the late 16th century.

Glossary definition (from İnalcık): “A silver coin, the chief unit of account in the Ottoman Empire.”

The value of the akçe was expressed in relation to gold. For seventy years — from about 1510 to 1580 — the ratio between the silver akçe and gold coinage remained remarkably stable: one gold piece was valued at 54 akçes in 1510 and at only 60 akçes in 1580. This stability was an indicator of the empire’s economic and social equilibrium during the classical age.

5.2 Gold Coinage (سکه زر / Altın)

The Ottoman gold coin was known as the sultani (سلطانی), also called altın (آلتون) or zer-i mahbub (زر محبوب). It was modelled on the Venetian ducat both in weight (approximately 3.5 grams) and fineness. The sultani was first struck in significant quantities under Mehmed II, who established a mint in Istanbul and regulated its circulation. Gold coins were used for large transactions, international trade, and state-to-state payments. The imperial Treasury insisted on receiving the Egyptian surplus (500,000 gold ducats annually) in gold.

The gold/silver ratio provides a window into Ottoman monetary stability. In 1510: 1 gold piece = 54 akçes. In 1580: 1 gold piece = 60 akçes. This near-seventy-year stability was an indication of the empire’s economic health.

5.3 Coinage Administration

Mehmed the Conqueror issued legislative decrees governing the mint (darphane / ضربخانه), the circulation of coin, customs, and monopolies. The mint at Istanbul was the principal mint of the empire, though various provincial mints operated under central control. Counterfeiting was a persistent problem, and the state periodically cracked down on defective and counterfeit coin, particularly during periods of financial strain.

5.4 Currency Debasement and Inflation (تنزیل ارزش پول و تورم)

Debasement of the silver coinage occurred periodically, especially during times of fiscal pressure. Mehmed II, to finance his great undertakings, “repeatedly debased the silver coinage and tightened financial controls.” This was a common expedient: by reducing the silver content of the akçe while maintaining its face value, the state could increase the nominal money supply, but at the cost of inflation and loss of public confidence.

In the 1580s, the debasement of the silver coinage, followed by an increase in counterfeit and defective coin in circulation, aggravated the already severe financial crisis. The state debased and diminished the akçe in panic, but these measures only worsened the situation, leading to speculation, excessive interest rates, usury, and profiteering.

5.5 The Impact of American Silver and the Price Revolution (نقره آمریکا و انقلاب قیمت‌ها)

The most devastating shock to the Ottoman monetary system came from outside. In the mid-16th century, Mexican silver flooded the European market, causing huge price increases across the continent. In the 1580s, this situation was compounded in the Ottoman Empire, where gold was cheaper in relation to silver than in Europe. These relatively low gold prices encouraged the export of European silver to the empire, to such an extent that in 1584 it was reported that “one of the main items of trade going to Turkey are Spanish reals sent by the chestful.”

European silver coins inundated the Ottoman market. Within a short time, prices doubled. The consequences were catastrophic for fixed-income groups:

  • Timar-holding sipâhîs — whose timar incomes were assessed in akçes — found their real incomes halved and abandoned their timars rather than go on long, costly campaigns.
  • Kapıkulus (slaves of the Porte, including Janissaries) — whose salaries were fixed in akçes — were suddenly impoverished.
  • Vakıf beneficiaries and others on fixed stipends suffered similarly.
  • The Janissary corps in the capital mutinied more and more often.
  • Bribery and misappropriations increased among state officials, soldiers, and kadıs.

5.6 The Fiscal Crisis of the Late 16th Century (بحران مالی اواخر سده شانزدهم)

The price revolution compounded a fiscal crisis already brewing. To pay the Janissaries their monthly salary and the new sekban troops their daily wage, the state had to amass ever-increasing amounts of coin in the central Treasury. Some timar lands were brought under direct Treasury control and farmed out for tax revenue, but many passed fraudulently into the hands of palace officials or were converted into vakıfs, losing their revenue to the government.

In 1534, the central Treasury’s annual income had reached five million gold ducats. By 1591, it was worth only half its original value because most taxes were levied in akçes, which had depreciated.

The situation was exacerbated by the outbreak of war with Austria in 1593, motivated partly by a desire to rid the capital of mutinous Janissaries by sending them on campaign. But the war lasted far longer than anticipated, and military and naval expenditure caused a huge and permanent deficit. Taxes were increased four- or five-fold (the cizye, for example); extraordinary levies (avâriz) became regular annual cash taxes. The assessment per tax unit rose from 50 akçes in 1576 to 280 akçes in 1600.

The state sought to offset Treasury expenses by further debasing and diminishing the akçe; but these panic measures only worsened the situation, driving up interest rates, encouraging counterfeiting, and destroying confidence in the currency.

5.7 The Celâlî Rebellions and Economic Collapse

The monetary and fiscal crisis directly contributed to the Celâlî rebellions in Anatolia (1595-1610). Bands of discharged or unpaid soldiers, landless peasants, and nomads — known as levends or gurbet tâifesi — roamed the countryside, pillaging and burning. Sipâhîs whose timars had been confiscated or rendered worthless joined the rebels. The rebellion of Kara Yazıcı in 1598 numbered 20,000 men.

Between 1595 and 1610, Anatolia was devastated. The richer inhabitants migrated to the Balkans, the Crimea, Iran, and the Arab lands. Land was left fallow; hunger and famine followed; the Treasury lost its sources of revenue. In 1607, the English ambassador wrote from Istanbul: “As far as I can see, the Turkish Empire was in great decline, almost ruined.”
