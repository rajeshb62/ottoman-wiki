The Moslem Institution (Ulema) of the Ottoman Empire

Definition and Central Thesis

Lybyer’s second great pillar of the Ottoman state was the Moslem Institution — the religious, legal, and educational hierarchy based upon and inspired by the Mohammedan religion. It stood parallel and complementary to the Ruling Institution. Whereas the Ruling Institution was the engine of conquest, administration, and war — recruited from Christian-born slaves — the Moslem Institution was rooted in the free-born Moslem population and encompassed the ulema (learned men), judges, jurists, teachers, clergy, and the entire apparatus of Islamic law, education, and worship.

“The structure of the Moslem Institution of the Ottoman Empire … was and remains to the present time wholly different from that of any of the Christian ecclesiastical organizations. As a mere church it claimed far less place and influence than they do, but in other aspects it reached out far more widely.” — Lybyer, ch. VII

The Moslem Institution included all those Mohammedans in the Ottoman Empire, outside of the Ruling Institution, who were lifted above the level of the ordinary believer. What united them was a special relationship to the Mohammedan religion — sometimes based on birth or piety, but usually established by intellectual training in the Book and the Law of Islam. The sultan was the head of this institution, just as he was head of the Ruling Institution; the two met at the apex and interacted at every level.

The fundamental difference: members of the Ruling Institution were drawn almost exclusively from Christian families, while members of the Moslem Institution came even more exclusively from Mohammedan families. Between them arose a rivalry of tendency and influence that was to become extremely harmful to the Ottoman state.

Financial Support: The Vakıf (وقف) System

A vast proportion of Ottoman land — perhaps one-third — was set aside as vakf (وقف, religious endowment). Much was endowed by sultans; in such cases the imperial treasury could use none of the revenue. Other parcels were endowed by private individuals, the treasury receiving its customary revenues as before while the surplus was devoted to purposes specified by the donor.

Each tract was assigned to a particular object, with a Muteveli (مُتَوَلّى, administrator) and a Nazir (ناظر, inspector) appointed to manage it. High officials such as the grand vizier, the Mufti, the Kapu Agha (قپو آغاسی), or the Kizlar Agha (قزلر آغاسی) were often put in charge ex officio. In time, these dignitaries organized the properties under their charge by holding private Divans and appointing Mufettishes (مُفَتِّش, special judges) with staffs of subordinates and travelling inspectors.

Three classes of vakıf:

  1. Regular vakıfs of mosques — supported mosques and their associated institutions
  2. Charitable vakıfs — endowments for schools, libraries, hospitals, bridges, fountains, caravanserais, public baths, and dervish convents
  3. Customary vakıfs of mosques — investments of mosque funds, governed by Kanun (قانون) rather than Sheri (شرع)

The chief material unit was the mosque (جامع, cami). Each great mosque was a large house of worship surrounded by smaller institutions — colleges, law schools, hospitals, insane asylums, and soup-kitchens. The income from its vakıfs supported all of these. The revenue of Aya Sofia (آیا صوفیا) alone was estimated at two hundred thousand ducats a year.

Many of the sultan’s kullar (قول, slaves) provided inheritances for their sons by setting apart lands in vakıf, with the desired persons as administrators who would retain a portion of the income. The vakıfs as a whole supported all official members of the Moslem Institution, except that judges derived much of their income from fees and fines.

The Ulema (عُلَما, learned men) possessed the noble privilege of immunity from taxation. Their property was not subject to confiscation and, since they were not kullar, passed by inheritance to their relatives. These privileges gave the learned class the prestige of nobility and great financial advantage.

The Educational System

Like the Ruling Institution, the Moslem Institution contained an educational system that was of its essential structure. From the time of Mohammed II, the great majority of members — all who expected promotion — were required to pass through it, bearing collectively the name Ulema (عُلَما, learned men).

Three grades of schools, supported by vakıfs and attached to mosques:

  1. Mektebs (مكتب, primary schools) — called okumak-yerleri (اوقوماق ییرلری, “reading-places”) in the 16th century — taught Arabic reading and writing and the Koran
  2. Medreses (مدرسه, ordinary colleges) — gave a course of ten studies resembling the Seven Liberal Arts of the West
  3. Higher medreses (law schools) — of university grade, teaching the sciences connected with the Koran and the Sheri (شرع, Sacred Law), including both law and theology

Education was free where primary schools existed; some students were even fed and lodged. Students in medreses were partially supported, and those in law schools received a sufficiency. The system probably gave better opportunity for education to Moslem boys in the 16th century than was afforded to Christian children in any land until a much later date. However, its conservatism — no change in methods or subjects from century to century — was eventually to become harmful.

Medreses were very numerous. The mosque of the Conqueror (Fatih, فاتح) had eight; that of Suleiman (Süleymaniye, سلیمانیه) had five. Suleiman set the gradations in their final form.

Stages of advancement:

  • Softa or Sukhta (سوخته, “inflamed with desire for learning”) — a beginning student
  • Danishmend (دانشمند, “master of arts”) — a graduate; appears in early sources as “Talisman.” Those content to teach primary school or attend to ecclesiastical duties needed study no further
  • Mulâzim (ملازم, candidate) — graduates of the higher law course, examined personally by the Mufti
  • Muderis (مدرس, professor) — those who sought appointment as teachers in medreses, receiving large salaries that increased as they rose

Three classes of Muderis:

  • Muderis of Constantinople (قسطنطنیه)
  • Muderis of Adrianople (ادرنه) and Brusa (بروسه)
  • Muderis of other cities

Constantinople’s roughly four hundred Muderis were in ten grades, distinguished by the subjects they taught. Those who wished to reach the higher judgeships were obliged to pass through all ten grades — so long a process as regularly to bring a man to gray hairs before he reached the top. This led to circumvention: the sons of Ulema were inscribed as Muderis while very young, with substitutes hired to teach in their places. By thirty or forty they could thus attain high position. This practice, combined with the immunities and privileges of the Ulema, led over time to great accumulations of wealth in a few families.

Clergy (Ruhban), Seids (Seyyidler), and Dervishes (Dervişler)

The Clergy

The clergy of the Ottoman nation were of no great education and possessed less influence than the priests of any other religion. They were in five classes:

Seids (Seyyidler / Emirs / Sherifs)

The Seids (سادات, also known as Emirs — امرا — or Sherifs — شرفا) were a class apart. They were not properly members of the Ulema unless they passed through the schools like others. Their distinction rested on a real or assumed genealogy tracing their ancestry back to the Prophet Mohammed. They alone were privileged to wear a green turban (yeşil sarık, یشیل صارق).

They constituted the only hereditary nobility among the Ottomans — their privileges were personal rather than financial: they were not to be struck on penalty of severe punishment, and they had their own justice. Great honor was shown to two notable Seids:

  • Mir Alem (میر علم, the sultan’s standard-bearer) — regularly a Seid, had precedence of all army officers
  • Nakib ol-Eshraf (نقیب الاشراف, chief of the Seids) — ranked second in the Moslem Institution; at Bairam ceremonies had precedence even of the Mufti. Appointed by the sultan for life, he held despotic authority over all Seids, with a staff of officers and clerks in the capital and provinces

Dervishes

Dervishes (درویش) were not members of the Ulema. They represented in Islam the monks, hermits, and begging friars of Christianity. Through them heresies spread, uprisings were concocted, mobs gathered, and holy war was preached. On more than one occasion they endangered the power of the government. Many were honest and God-fearing; others were scarcely more than tramps and wandering thieves. Clergy, Seids, and dervishes represented the merely religious side of the institution; they never grew to possess permanent influence and power in the state.

Jurists and the Mufti (Müfti / Şeyhülislam)

The Muftis (Müftiler)

Among the Ulema who had finished the law course, those who chose to become counsellors and jurists rather than take up the active judicial career constituted a distinct body: the muftis (مفتى), held in high esteem. One was assigned as associate to the judge of every important city — about two hundred in all — while others were counsellors for Beylerbeys (بیگلربیگ) and Sanjak Beys (سنجق بیگی). Appointed for life, they lived in retirement with no initiative of action.

When a judge, Bey, or private citizen confronted a case involving learned knowledge of the Sacred Law, they submitted to a mufti a question in writing (usually a hypothetical case). The mufti, after careful consideration in light of the law books of the school of Abu Hanifa (أبو حنیفة), gave an answer: a fetva (فتوى). These answers were extremely concise and unaccompanied by reasoning, prepared and sealed in solemn form. When a judge or Bey posed a question touching a pending law case, the mufti’s response ordinarily settled the case. Since there was no class of professional lawyers, the muftis were a necessary and very useful body.

The Mufti of Constantinople: Şeyhülislam (شیخ الإسلام)

In ordinary cities the mufti ranked after the judge. But in Constantinople, where the sultan and his officers frequently presented questions touching matters of the highest public importance, the mufti of Constantinople became par excellence the Mufti. Mohammed II assigned him the title of Sheik ul-Islam (شیخ الإسلام, “Ancient of Islam”), which in later times became his ordinary title. The Mufti was not regularly chosen from among his fellows but was usually advanced by the sultan from the active judicial service. He had the right to appoint and promote all other muftis of the empire. Suleiman created the Fetva-khaneh (فتوىخانه) — a special bureau to assist the Mufti in preparing decisions.

Suleiman constituted the Mufti the head of the Ulema. He outranked all officials of government, yielding place only to the grand vizier on ordinary occasions. He was almost the equal of the sultan himself, since he was the expounder and representative of the Sacred Law, which was above the sultan. Bayezid II was accustomed to stand to receive the Mufti and give him a seat above his own.

“The Turk shows his [Mufti] the greatest reverence of any man in his realm, because he represents justice and the image of God.” — Early in Suleiman’s reign

Sixteenth-century Westerners compared the Mufti with the pope. His powers included:

  • Sole right to proclaim that war should be begun and to declare it holy and incumbent on all Moslems
  • Frequent consultation by the sultan on the conformity of proposed Kanuns (قوانین) with the Sacred Law
  • The ultimate responsibility of pronouncing that a sultan had transgressed the Sacred Law and ought to be deposed

The Mufti exercised a function similar to the highest office of the Supreme Court of the United States: the power of defending the Constitution. Urf (عرف, sovereign will) was subordinate to Sheri (شرع, Sacred Law); the sultan, who embodied the former, could not override the Mufti, who represented the latter.

The power of the Mufti illustrated:

  1. Selim I and the forced conversion of Christians (c. 1515): Selim decided to order all Christians to accept Islam on pain of death. The Mufti Jemali (جمالی) intervened decisively, showing the Greek Patriarch that the Sacred Law provided that Christians who had accepted Mohammedan rule and agreed to pay kharâj (خراج) and jizyeh (جزیه) were to be left unmolested. The argument prevailed, and the Christians were not disturbed.

  2. Suleiman and the execution of Mustapha (1553): Suleiman consulted the Mufti Ebu Su’ud (ابو السعود) by means of a hypothetical case — a merchant who left a slave in charge of his household, only to have the slave plot against his wife and children. The Mufti answered that such a slave deserved to be tortured to death. This greatly influenced Suleiman in ordering the execution of his eldest son.

The fetvas of the muftis amounted in practice to a body of legislation intermediate between the Sheri and the Kanuns — partaking of the sacred character of the former while being, like the latter, of a modern and practical nature. However, nothing radical could ever be attempted. In reality, the muftis occupied the most influential position in the Moslem Institution and perhaps in the Ottoman state:

“Usually inferior to judges and officers of government in income and display, and giving no direct impulse to affairs, they nevertheless wielded the greatest continuous power in the state — the quiet, steady, almost changeless, almost irresistible, force of Mohammedanism.”

The muftis’ authority rested on (1) the acceptance by the entire Moslem population of the absolute supremacy of the Sacred Law, and (2) the recognition that they who had acquired learning in the Law by long years of arduous labor were the persons through whom that supremacy was to be maintained. They were the conservative, regulative force in the Ottoman state, contributing largely to the empire’s durability. But their work was done only too well — the idea of the changelessness of the Sacred Law was essentially hostile to progress, and the Ulema held the Ottoman mind nearly stationary while the rest of the world moved on.

The Judicial System

The Kadis (Kazılar / Judges)

The judges belonging to the corps of the Ulema had territorial jurisdictions that covered the whole empire — even extending to the Crimea and North Africa, though these were under vassal governments. The tribunals were competent for all kinds of cases, whether civil or criminal, whether covered by the Sheri, the Kanuns, Adet (عادت, custom), or none of these.

However, they were not competent to try all persons:

  • Kullar (قول, members of the Ruling Institution) had their own justice
  • Seids had their own jurisdiction under the Nakib ol-Eshraf
  • Foreign colonies had separate systems
  • Subject Christians had their own ecclesiastical tribunals for matters among themselves
  • Special courts handled certain vakıf cases, though presided over by the regular judicial body
  • Fief-holders had seigniorial jurisdiction in certain matters

The Kaziaskers (قاضیعسكر, Military Judges)

The Kaziaskers (Kaziasker of Rumelia and Kaziasker of Anatolia) were the two highest judicial officers. Originally their function was to judge the armies in time of war — hence their title “judge of the army” — but their authority had been extended to include the headship of all judges in their respective regions and the appointment of all judges, subject to approval of the pashas. In the Divan, and as “Pillars of the State,” they ranked next to the viziers. They had the first right of audience with the sultan at the close of each Divan, and until the reign of Suleiman they had held all the authority over the Ulema that later came to the Mufti.

Their jurisdictions were divided by Mohammed II: cases concerning Moslems came before the Kaziasker of Rumelia; those concerning non-Moslems before the Kaziasker of Anatolia.

Court Procedure

In every court a single judge sat, with clerks and other subordinates. Cases were presented by the parties themselves, and decisions were usually rendered immediately and in very concise form. The judge cooperated with the Subashi (subası) of the city, who brought summoned persons before the judge and executed sentences — an arrangement similar to ecclesiastical courts of the West, which condemned but left execution to the secular arm.

Hierarchy of appeal:

  1. Lower judges (Kazis, Naibs)
  2. Higher judges (Mollas)
  3. Kaziaskers
  4. Grand vizier
  5. The Divan

Costs and fines were moderate, fixed by Kanun, but constituted a large part of judicial income. Judges were salaried; some had large irregular earnings. The judges also attended to all notarial work of the empire.

The highest courts were:

  • The Kaziaskers’ courts — held at the Divan, at the palace gate, and in their own houses
  • The grand vizier’s court — the highest court of appeal for ordinary civil cases, also a court of first instance where justice was refused to no one
  • The Divan — tried capital cases of great officials, usually in the absence of the accused

The Law Administered

The law which the judges administered was:

  1. The Sacred Law (Sheri/Şeriat, شریعت) — primarily as given in the Koran and the traditions of Mohammed, codified by the doctors of the school of Abu Hanifa, and interpreted in collections of fetvas
  2. The Kanuns (قوانین) of the sultans
  3. Customs (Adet, عادت) and immunities of the regions
  4. Equity — “the good sense and prudence of judges trained in reasoning supplies and decides many things that are not written” (Postel)

The only resemblance to Anglo-Saxon case law was the use of fetvas. Since decisions were rendered very briefly, no extended reports were possible, and the absence of printing (not introduced into Turkey until the 18th century) made general use of judicial precedents practically impossible. Ottoman justice depended more heavily upon the integrity of judges than does Anglo-Saxon justice.

Non-judicial law enforcement: The Muhtesibs (محتصب, lieutenants of police) exercised functions of market and police judges but did not belong to the Ulema. Accompanied by soldiers and attendants, they patrolled streets and inspected markets, inflicting punishment on the spot for infringements.

Quality of Ottoman Justice

Western observers were divided. Some praised the speed and simplicity of Ottoman courts compared with European ones. The grand vizier and Divan were considered the only incorruptible courts. Christian subjects may have received unfair treatment — they were not allowed to testify, and some Moslems considered it nearly a meritorious religious act to turn a case against a Christian by false testimony. Nevertheless, judges were well-paid, highly honored, and carefully inspected by honest men sent out annually by the Kaziaskers. In at least one respect the courts were highly commendable: there was a minimum of “law’s delay.” Cases were always decided promptly, in clear and simple terms.

The Moslem Institution as a Whole

The Moslem Institution represented and maintained the entire system based on the life and work of the Prophet Mohammed. This system claimed to be sufficient for all sides of temporal and eternal life — for individuals and for the state. Its power extended over the whole empire, even beyond the limits of political control.

It was firmly grounded in the allegiance, the fundamental beliefs, and the affections of the entire Moslem-born population. All Moslems of the empire were in a sense members of the institution. In the 16th century, any one of them might hope to see his son mount to a very high place within the organization, since industrious study combined with native ability was all that was demanded. The Moslem Institution was fundamentally democratic.

Basis of membership:

  • By birth: the Seids (descendants of Mohammed)
  • By piety and religious service: the dervishes
  • By learned knowledge of the Sacred Law: all positions of public influence and importance

The three highly-honored classes of teachers (Muderisler), jurists (Müftiler), and judges (Kazılar) were trained in the same superbly-planned educational system, the same text-books, the same ideas. Whether in Constantinople or Cairo, the Crimea or Algiers, Budapest or Bagdad, old, grave, wise, and learned professors, jurists, and judges taught, interpreted, and enforced the same wide-reaching and changeless Sacred Law.

Professors, jurists, and judges alike were, in all that they did and throughout their lives, fundamentally teachers. They taught all the Moslems of the empire, from the young child to the aged sultan. Every important officer of administrative government had a judge and a mufti at his elbow. The sultan himself had always a spiritual adviser — his Hoja (خواجه, teacher) — to whom he showed great deference.

There was an aspect in which the Moslem Institution, based upon the Moslem population of the empire, fitted the government as hand fits glove — and the figure can be pressed further: the hand (the Ruling Institution) is of much the same efficiency with or without the glove, while the glove is useless without the hand; furthermore, the hand may live to wear a succession of gloves.

Relationship Between the Moslem Institution and the Ruling Institution

Lybyer devotes his entire Chapter VIII to comparing the two great institutions.

Likenesses

  • Both were constructed from old, well-seasoned materials with origins lost in prehistoric obscurity
  • Both were founded on ideas, not racial descent — belief, not blood, was the test of membership
  • Both were self-perpetuating through education, with lifelong educational systems adapted to their character (the Ruling Institution trained physically and mentally; the Moslem Institution neglected physical education for greater intellectual training)
  • Both rose to an apex, through the Divan and grand vizier, in the sultan — though the ideas by which they were joined to their head were in striking contrast

Interactions

The two institutions taught each other some evil qualities. Luxury, venality, and unnatural vices — all strongly discountenanced by the Sacred Law — were fostered in the Ruling Institution by the conditions of the system and came to be charged against the Moslem Institution as well. Conversely, the conservatism and resistance to progress of the Moslem Institution came to characterize the Ruling Institution, as Ulema taught the palace pages and princes — eventually giving the Ruling Institution a changelessness that resisted improvement.

Each contributed to the other’s power. The Ruling Institution defended the Moslem Institution by the sword, carried out judicial decisions, and protected its revenue. The Moslem Institution kept the Moslem population obedient and submissive to the sultan’s authority, teaching that the sultan was divinely appointed and always to be obeyed so long as he did not transgress the Sacred Law.

“Accordingly the two institutions, so long as they acted in harmony, were absolutely impregnable in their position among the Moslems of the empire.”

Relative Power

The Moslem Institution’s power grew cumulatively. It gained lands and wealth continually through new vakıfs and never lost any. It held all means and methods of intellectual development. The Ruling Institution, by contrast, lost relatively — sultans constantly gave away state lands as endowments for new mosques and colleges. By the time of Suleiman’s reign, the Moslem Institution had overtaken its rival.

Suleiman’s gifts of great mosques, numerous colleges, and vast endowments — his arrangement of the cursus honorum leading up to the Mufti — and the personal leaning of his later years toward the influence of the Ulema, settled permanently the preponderance of the Moslem Institution.

Yet the Moslem Institution could never destroy its rival. It had become too unworldly to live unsupported by a secular power — “a strong but tender hand, which must always wear a glove.”

“Bound together closely in an alliance which neither enjoyed, but which was necessary for the preservation of both, the Ruling Institution and the Moslem Institution constituted the twofold inner framework of the Ottoman Empire, to which it owed all its might and energy, its grandeur and repute, its continuity and durability.”
