Ottoman Court and Central Government under Suleiman the Magnificent

The Ruling Institution

The government of the Ottoman Empire in the time of Suleiman the Magnificent (سلطان سليمان قانونى) was built upon what Albert Howe Lybyer termed the “Ruling Institution” — a single, integrated slave-family that simultaneously served as the empire’s government, army, court, and nobility. Almost all of its members were recruited as slaves from Christian subjects, converted to Islam, trained through a rigorous educational system, and advanced purely on merit. The institution was a despotism centered on the sultan, but one heavily circumscribed by the Sacred Law (شريعت, Şeriat) of Islam, which functioned as a rigid, unamendable constitution.

The sultan was head of state in multiple capacities: as the supreme military commander wielding actual power through his kullar (قُلَّر, “slaves”); as the caliph (خليفه, Halife), defender and executor of the Sacred Law; as the successor to the Byzantine Caesars, inheriting their imperial splendor; and as the heir of ancient Turkish traditions of absolute obedience to the ruler who led and fed his people. All institutions of the empire — the Ruling Institution, the Moslem Institution, the Christian and Jewish ecclesiastical organizations, and even the foreign colonies — were “extended downward” from the divinely appointed sovereign at the top.

The Imperial Household

The sultan’s household was divided into three principal subdivisions: the outside service (بیرون, Birun), the inside service (اندرون, Enderun), and the harem (حرم, Harem). The outside service was composed of grown men and Ajem-oghlans, the inside service of white eunuchs and pages, and the harem of black eunuchs and women. In time of peace the first two subdivisions were in attendance at the principal palace built by Mehmed II on the acropolis of ancient Byzantium (the site of the modern Topkapı Palace); the harem was permanently located in the Old Palace in the center of the city.

The grounds of the principal palace were extensive: within the first gate lay a large open space used as a parade ground on state occasions; within the second gate stood the palace buildings proper, a beautiful garden, and an exercise ground for the pages. Members of the outside service — except the gardeners — did not ordinarily pass beyond the second gate. In time of war, practically the entire outside service and the principal officers of the inside service accompanied the sultan; no women from the harem were taken on campaign.

The total personnel of the imperial household is estimated at between ten and fifteen thousand individuals, of whom only a few (the Ulema learned men) were not under the complete control of the Ruling Institution.

The Harem (حرم همايون)

The harem in Suleiman’s time was far less prominent than it would become in later centuries, yet it demands attention because of the great influence of two of its ladies: Roxelana (خُرَّم, Khurrem) and Suleiman’s daughter Mihrmah.

The guard and order of the harem palace were committed to forty or more black eunuchs under the Kizlar Aghasi (قزلر آغاسی, “general of the girls”). This Agha was held in great honor and administered many religious endowments (اوقاف, evkaf), particularly those of the Holy Cities of Mecca and Medina.

The greatest lady of the harem, while she lived, was the sultan’s mother, the Valide Sultan (والده سلطان). She received great deference from her son and exercised general authority over all his women. Next in importance was the mother of the sultan’s eldest son, and after her the mothers of other sons — mothers of daughters held far less consideration. Each favored lady had her own suite of apartments, a business staff under a Kiaya (كَهْيَا, “steward”), and personal servants. Sons lived with their mothers during childhood, received careful education in letters and arms, and were sent out at the appropriate age as provincial governors (sancak beyleri). Daughters were married young to high officials.

Suleiman’s mother lived until far into his reign. The mother of his eldest son, Mustapha, held the next place until Roxelana — made a legal wife — supplanted her. Suleiman seems not to have visited his harem very often. His daughter Mihrmah, wife of Grand Vizier Rüstem Pasha, was especially dear to him.

The Inside Service (اندرون)

The inside service of the principal palace was supplied by five chambers (اوده, oda) of pages under the control of white eunuchs, together with doorkeepers. The head of this service was the Kapu Aghasi (قپو آغاسی, “general of the gate”), a white eunuch who managed many religious endowments and had the right to speak to the sultan whenever he wished. The Kapuji-bashi (قپوجی باشی, head doorkeeper), also a white eunuch, commanded a company of twenty or more white eunuchs who guarded the second gate.

The pages have been described from the educational point of view in Lybyer’s account of the palace school. Nearest the sultan’s person were the pages of the Has Oda (خاص اوده, Inner Chamber), of whom there were thirty-nine (the sultan himself being the fortieth). Their chief officer was the Has Oda-bashi. The pages of highest rank were the Silihdar (سله‌دار, who carried the sultan’s weapons outside the palace), the Chokadar (چوقه‌دار, who carried his garments), and the Sharabdar (شرابدار, cup-bearer).

The second group of pages constituted the Khazineh Odassi (خزینه اوده‌سی, treasury chamber), sixty or seventy strong, under a white eunuch — the inside Khazinehdar-bashi. These pages cared for all treasures in the palace, made payments, and kept accounts. A second Khazinehdar-bashi managed the financial affairs of the inside service outside the palace walls.

The Kiler Odassi (كیلر اوده‌سی, pantry chamber), under a white eunuch called the Kilerji-bashi, cared for the sultan’s bread, pastry, and game, and also controlled the kitchen service of the palace. The remaining two chambers — the Large and the Small (or the Old and the New) — were wholly concerned with the education of the pages, under the general direction of the Ikinji-Kapu-oghlan (ایكی نجی قپو اوغلانی, eunuch of the second gate).

The entire personnel of the inside service amounted to six to eight hundred persons. Eunuch officers maintained severe discipline, exact obedience, and perfect order among them all.

The Outside Service (بیرون)

The outside service was far more numerous than the inside. Beginning with those closest to the sultan, its groups were:

Learned Associates — Chiefly from the Ulema (علماء). The sultan’s Hoja (خواجه, teacher) was a religious adviser held in great esteem, often advanced to high judicial office. Next came two Imams (ائمه, preachers) with muezzins, then the Hekim-bashi (حكیم باشی, chief physician) with ten or more associates, the Munejim-bashi (منجم باشی, chief astrologer), and the Jerrah-bashi (جراح باشی, chief surgeon).

Kitchen Service — Under the oversight of the Kilerji-bashi, comprising bakers, scullions, cooks, confectioners, tasters, and musicians, each fifty to one hundred strong. Allied companies of tailors, shoemakers, furriers, and goldsmiths were employed exclusively in the palace. Many menial servants — scullions, wood-cutters, water-carriers — were Ajem-oghlans (عجم اوغلان, “foreign boys” in training).

Body-Guards — Three corps: the Muteferrika (متفرفه, Noble Guard), one to two hundred of the choicest page-school graduates, who followed immediately after the sultan on horseback and defended him in battle; the Solaks (صولاق, Janissary archers), about one hundred and fifty who marched on foot beside him with bows ready; and the Peiks (پَیْك, halberdiers), about one hundred men, taken over from the Byzantine emperors, who ran before the sultan when he rode.

Palace-Guards — The Kapujis (قپوجی, gatekeepers), three to four hundred Ajem-oghlans who watched the outside gates; the Chaushes (چاوش, ushers/marshals), about one hundred, who acted as marshals on Divan days, dressed the ranks at war, served as state messengers, and — being often multilingual renegades — as interpreters and envoys; and the Bostanjis (بوستانجی, gardeners), about four hundred Ajem-oghlans who cared for the gardens and rowed the sultan’s caiques. The Bostanji-bashi seems to have been the only adult man besides the sultan who resided within the inner palace.

Stable Service — Extremely important in a cavalry nation. The sultan kept two hundred horses for his own use, tended by a hundred men, and four thousand for his retinue, tended by two thousand. A thousand or more Bulgarian Voinaks tended herds on the domanial pastures. The Emir-al-Akhor (امير آخور, grand equerry) and the second equerry oversaw saddlers, camel-drivers, and muleteers, and controlled all domanial pastures and forests.

The Rekiab-Aghalari (ركاب آغالری, “generals of the imperial stirrup”) were a special group of officers: the head gardener, head gatekeeper, grand equerry, second equerry, and the Mir-Alem (مير علم, standard-bearer). The Mir-Alem had charge of the imperial standards and the six horsetails (طوغ, tuğ) borne before the sultan, distributed standards and horsetails to beylerbeys and sancakbeys (thus granting them investiture), ranked first among household officers in relation to the government, and commanded the military music.

Additional groups included the tent-pitchers (Mihter-bashi), the Veznedars (وزنه‌دار, weighers of money), and the masters of the hunt — heads of dog-keepers, falconers, vulturers, gerfalconers, and hawkers. Grand Vizier Ibrahim was chief falconer at the time of his promotion.

The Umena (امنا, intendants) exercised clear governmental functions: the Shehr-emini (شهر امينی, intendant of imperial buildings), the Zarabkhaneh-emini (ضرابخانه امينی, intendant of mints and mines), and others. These were exceptions, made for convenience, to the general rule of separating household from governmental officials.

Court Ceremonies

The early Ottoman sultans maintained simplicity of life down to the time of Murad II. Mehmed II, after the conquest of Constantinople, appropriated Byzantine imperial forms and ceremonies. His Kanuns (قانون, laws) dealt extensively with matters of rank and ceremony. By Suleiman’s time the Kanuni Teshrifat (قانون تشريفات, Law of Ceremonies) had become a sizeable collection, standing on a par with the Law of Fiefs, the Law of Egypt, and the Law of Fines and Punishments. It regulated the color, shape, and material of robes and turbans, the order of precedence on every occasion, and the proper observances for each event. It was as much an officer’s duty to wear the correct costume and appear in the right place at the right time as to attend to the business of his position.

All classes of the household, all high officers of government, and all separate bodies of troops were clearly distinguished by costume or head-dress or both.

Ceremonial occasions were numerous:

  • The Divan met four times weekly (Saturday through Tuesday)
  • The sultan rode to Friday mosque in magnificent state
  • Janissaries were paid quarterly with ceremony
  • Ambassadors were received on such occasions to impress them with wealth and power
  • The two great festivals of Bairam (عيد, Id) — one closing Ramadan, the other seventy days later — involved the ceremonial kissing of the sultan’s hand by all officials
  • Extraordinary ceremonies celebrated the birth of children, the circumcision of princes, the marriage of princesses, accessions to the throne, and the sultan’s going forth to war

The greatest of Suleiman’s celebrations was the circumcision of his sons Mustapha, Mehmed, and Selim in 1530 — twenty-one successive days of display, feasting, games, and formal presentation of gifts.

Ogier Ghiselin de Busbecq, ambassador from Emperor Charles V, left vivid descriptions of Suleiman’s court: the sultan seated on a low ottoman covered with costly rugs, surrounded by a sea of turbaned heads in dazzling robes of gold, silver, purple, and silk; the absolute silence and order of thousands of men; the Janissaries standing “so steady and motionless” that Busbecq could not tell whether they were “human beings or statues”; the magnificent cavalry with horses from Cappadocia and Syria, trappings sparkling with gold and jewels, riders in cloth of silver and gold, bows and painted arrows from Babylon, jewelled scimitars, and steel maces.

The Viziers and the Grand Vizierate

Ottoman writers represented their government under the figure of a tent supported by four lofty pillars: the Viziers (وزراء, Vüzera), the Kaziaskers (قاضى عسكر, military judges), the Defterdars (دفتردار, treasurers), and the Nishanjis (نشانجی, chancellors). The pillars did not bear equal weight, however: from Suleiman’s time the grand vizier carried so much greater a burden that he might be compared to a central pillar supporting the entire tent.

The word vizier (وزير) means “burden-bearer” — lifting from the sovereign’s shoulders the burden of state. In Suleiman’s reign there were ordinarily four viziers, that being a sacred number. All bore the title pasha (پاشا). Ordinary viziers had no regular responsibilities beyond their function as councillors; they enjoyed great incomes from regular and irregular sources and kept large establishments modeled on the sultan’s.

Under Suleiman the office of grand vizier (صدر اعظم, Sadr-ı Azam) reached the climax of a notable development. From being first among equals, the grand vizier became a personage far above his fellow-viziers — a difference not merely in degree but in kind, typified by the fact that in reporting to Suleiman after the Divan, no one spoke but the grand vizier.

This development resulted from Suleiman’s willingness to entrust vast power to chosen instruments. Ibrahim (Pargalı İbrahim Paşa) first held his master’s confidence for thirteen years. Later Rüstem (Rüstem Paşa) came to full power, supported by Roxelana and Mihrmah. In Suleiman’s last years he left nearly everything to Ali and to Sokollu Mehmed Paşa. The grand vizier thus came practically to wield the sovereign power of the state. This was a dangerous development: it made it easy for later, weaker sultans to withdraw into the harem, while the grand vizierate itself became prey to harem intrigue.

Ten special prerogatives marked the grand vizier’s dignity: he kept the imperial seal; held his own Divan at his palace; was escorted by the Chaush-bashi and all Chaushes; received weekly state visits from the Kaziaskers and Defterdars; went in solemn Friday procession to mosque; inspected the city and its markets; and was honored at Bairams with official felicitations. Customary ceremonies alone absorbed much of his time, to say nothing of vast administrative and judicial burdens.

Seven grand viziers served Suleiman in forty-six years. Four ended their service at death, two were deposed and executed, three were simply deposed. All but one (Piri Mehmed Paşa) were Christian renegades who had risen as slaves to the highest honor of the empire.

The Defterdars (Treasurers)

The great labor of accounting for the receipts and expenditures of the Ruling Institution was under the care of two principal Defterdars, one for Rumelia (دفتردار روم ايلى) and one for Anatolia (دفتردار آناطولی), aided by two of lower rank — one for Aleppo and the southwest, one for the Danubian countries. The principal defterdars were men of great position, with large incomes and households and the right of audience with the sultan on revenue matters.

Under them were twenty-five bureaus (قلم, kalem), instituted by Mehmed the Conqueror, each with a chief (خواجه‌گان, Khojagan) directing clerks of various grades. Between these and the defterdars were several intermediate officials, of whom the most important were the two Rusnamehjis (روزنامه‌جی, book-keepers). The total personnel of the treasury department numbered more than eight hundred.

The bureaus covered:

  1. The Greater Book-keeping Bureau (central accounting)
  2. The Head Bureau of Accounts (tithes, taxes, munitions, garrisons, intendants)
  3. The Anatolian Accounts Bureau (domanial lands, Aegean garrisons, pensions)
  4. The Cavalry Control Bureau (inner service salaries, Kapujis, stables, Spahis of the Porte) 5-6. Separate bureaus for Spahis and Silihdars
  5. The Holy Cities Accounts Bureau (Mecca/Medina endowments, mosque salaries)
  6. The Capitation Tax Bureau (جزیة, jizyeh — tax on adult male non-Muslims)
  7. The Tributes Bureau (taxes in kind, grain stores, army supplies)
  8. The Treasury Chancery Bureau (diplomas, firmans concerning treasury)
  9. The Lesser Book-keeping Bureau (head Kapujis, stewards, marine)
  10. The Infantry Control Bureau (Janissaries and auxiliary corps)
  11. The Lesser Endowments Bureau (hospitals, soup-kitchens, asylums) 14-15. Greater and Lesser Fortress Bureaus (garrison records for Danube, Albania, Morea)
  12. The Mine Leases Bureau (mines, gypsy tribute, Moldavian/Wallachian tribute, customs)
  13. The Salaries Bureau (fleet captains, Crimean Khan)
  14. The Domanial Leases Bureau (revenues for harem ladies and high officials)
  15. The Head Leases Bureau (Danubian domains, rice fields, salt works, fisheries, forests)
  16. The Holy Cities Leases Bureau (Anatolian side) 21-23. Regional leases bureaus for Constantinople, Brusa, Euboea
  17. Kaffa Leases Bureau
  18. The Dates Bureau (dating all public documents)

Supplementary bureaus covered confiscations, animal taxes, and Christian churches and monasteries.

The system was characterized by decentralization of management: each bureau received specific revenues and paid them out for specific purposes, subject only to inspection and supervision by the first bureau. This was typical of despotic administration — entrusting definite duties to definite groups with sufficient revenues, then leaving them to themselves, intervening only when things went wrong.

Taxation and Suleiman’s Income

A distinction was drawn between legal taxes (authorized by the Sacred Law) and arbitrary taxes (depending on the sultan’s will). The early Islamic system recognized only three taxes: the ‘ushr (عشر, tithe on land), the kharâj (خراج, land tax payable in money, with a share of produce), and the jizyeh (جزية, poll tax on adult male non-Muslim subjects). Additional impositions — customs, tolls, market dues — were tolerated by necessity but discouraged by legists.

The Ottoman system inherited all of these and added Seljuk and Byzantine imposts. Much land was retained as imperial domain; the feudal system (تيمار, timar) was applied to lands conquered from Christians; and the conquests in Syria, Mesopotamia, and Egypt were left under their old regulations with Turkish cadasters.

Until Mehmed II’s time revenues were administered directly; malversations led him to introduce tax-farming (التزام, iltizam). Large regions were sold by the treasury to high kullar, who resold them by sections, sometimes repeatedly. The amount wrung from taxpayers might easily be double what the government received.

The strongly conservative Ottoman tendency made revenues extremely inelastic. Treaties with Western nations were commercially favorable to the latter, limiting customs revenue. As the value of money declined, fixed payments declined too. Stationary taxation was supplemented by booty (the “Prophet’s fifth” went to support the Moslem Institution), tribute from Moldavia, Wallachia, Transylvania, Ragusa, Venice (for Cyprus), and after 1547 from Austria for Hungary, confiscations of executed officials’ property, and the estates of kullar who died without children.

Suleiman’s Revenue: Junis Bey, chief interpreter, estimated the regular income at about four million ducats around 1530-1537 (1,300,000 from Anatolia and Greece, 1,600,000 from Egypt, 700,000 from Syria, 150,000 from Mesopotamia, 250,000 from farms, islands, and customs). Later estimates, including mines, salt works, tributes, and other sources, suggest six million ducats early in the reign, rising to seven or eight million toward its close. The bullion value of six million ducats is less than fourteen million dollars; with purchasing power perhaps five times modern levels, the regular revenue was equivalent to less than seventy million modern dollars — a small sum for so great an empire. But this excludes revenues devoted to the Moslem Institution and those assigned by feudal grant to local government, which probably doubled the total.

Expenses grew particularly for the fleet and the increasingly splendid household. Suleiman felt the need of money and, through Rüstem, exacted payments from officials at their appointment — a fatal precedent that opened the door to systematized corruption.

The Nishanji (Chancellor)

The chancery department (ديوان همايون قلمی) had not reached the same stage of development as the treasury. The Nishanji-bashi (نشانجی باشی), often called simply the Nishanji, was clearly the chief. His duty was to authenticate firmans (فرمان, imperial decrees) sent to the provinces by tracing at the head of each document the sultan’s tuğra (طغرا), or official calligraphic signature.

A description from about 1537 says the Nishanji engrossed the ordinances and commands of the prince, revised writings in consultation with the pashas to ensure clarity (like a keeper of the seals), and received seven thousand ducats of revenue from fiefs, with many slaves and lesser recorders under him.

In the later Ottoman government the Reis Effendi (رئيس افندی, “Chief of the Men of the Pen”) — minister of foreign affairs, secretary of state, and chancellor — displaced the Nishanji at the head of the chancery as the sultan withdrew into the palace and foreign relations grew more complex. Under the Reis Effendi were three bureaus (the Beylik, Tahvil, and Ruus kalems) employing about one hundred and fifty clerks.

The personnel of the chancery and treasury likely changed in Suleiman’s time from Christian-born to Moslem-born scribes. The bureaucratic machinery produced vast quantities of paperwork — laws, ordinances, diplomas, nominations, deeds, grants, pay orders, receipts, reports, addresses, petitions — and the practice of demanding private fees for each act of writing or signing was already present.

The Divan (ديوان همايون)

The Divan (ديوان, imperial council) was the supreme administrative and judicial body of the empire. It held long sessions four times each week in time of peace (Saturday, Sunday, Monday, Tuesday). Its members represented all great components of the Ruling Institution, and through the two Kaziaskers (military judges) it also represented the Moslem Institution. The Divan was thus not strictly a part of the Ruling Institution alone, but the capstone of both institutions — the body that gave final unity, under the sultan, to the organization of the empire.

Former sultans had presided. Suleiman did not — a departure for which he has been blamed. He freed himself from a great burden but created a serious rift in both institutions. He retained control through a grated window in the wall (kafes, قفس); not knowing when he might be listening, councillors always had to speak as if he were present.

The Sessions: Soon after sunrise, officials gathered at the palace, passing the second gate in inverse order of rank. The grand vizier sat in the middle of a long sofa. On his right sat the other viziers, and beyond them the Nishanji. On his left sat the two Kaziaskers, and beyond them the Defterdars. The Beylerbeys of Anatolia and Rumelia sat beyond the viziers. The Agha of the Janissaries had a place; the chief interpreter was often needed; the Reis Effendi sat on the floor at the grand vizier’s left.

Great questions — proposals of ambassadors, the condition of provinces, the desirability of war — were discussed briefly. Lesser matters were decided individually or referred to officials in attendance. Lawsuits were presented by masters of petitions (Teskerejis, تذكره‌جی); parties might appear to plead their own cases. All business was done with despatch. Decisions were briefly formulated and recorded. After the sultan signified approval at the close of a Divan, decisions were irrevocable.

A simple meal was served to all in attendance. Order was absolute; any disturber was immediately bastinadoed.

After the session, officials who had the right of audience went to meet the sultan. The grand vizier alone reported on the day’s decisions; the sultan usually approved as made, sometimes mitigating a decision or dictating an ambassador’s reply.

The Divan was not a legislative chamber but a combination of a cabinet and a supreme court. It was the highest court in the land, not so much a court of appeal as a court of first instance, with cognizance of all civil and criminal cases from any part of the empire. It was a training school for judges, administrators, and statesmen, and its members influenced legislation by providing the information and drafting on which new Kanuns were based.

The Sultan as Legislator: Kanun and Şeriat

The law that demanded obedience in the Ottoman Empire was fourfold: the Sheri (شريعت, Sacred Law of Islam); the Kanuns (قوانين, written decrees of the sultans); the Adet (عادت, established custom); and the Urf (عرف, the sovereign will of the reigning sultan).

The Sheri was above the sultan and unchangeable by him. It was a written constitution for the Ottoman Empire, not subject to amendment but capable of slight modification by judicial and juristic interpretation. The Sheri was based on the Koran (قرآن), supplemented by traditions of Mohammed’s legal decisions (حديث, Hadith), the decisions of the early caliphs, and the interpretations of early judges. The Hanafi school (ابو حنيفه), the accepted teacher of all Turkish peoples, was the foundation.

Suleiman charged Sheik Ibrahim Halebi of Aleppo with preparing a new code, the Multeka ol-ebhar (ملتقى الأبحر, “Confluence of the Seas”), finished before 1549. It remained the foundation of Ottoman law until the nineteenth-century reforms.

The Kanuns were issued in accordance with the principle that “the Imam has the right to make all civil and political regulations demanded by prudence, circumstances, and the public welfare.” They dealt with military, financial, feudal, criminal, and police law, and the law of ceremonies. In the fields of feudal law and ceremonies — non-existent in the early Islamic state — the sultans had a free hand. In all other fields Kanuns were strictly supplementary and administrative, bound not to transgress the positive commands of the Sacred Law.

Kanuns were issued separately to meet special circumstances. A collection of them constituted a Kanun-nameh (قانون نامه, book of laws). Suleiman’s Kanun-nameh proper — chiefly compiled by the great Mufti Ebu Su’ud (ابو السعود افندی) — contained ordinances on land tenure, taxes on subject Christians, and regulations of the feudal system. The only complete body he published was the Kanun-nameh Misr (قانون نامه مصر, constitution for Egypt), issued in 1525.

Suleiman was rightly called Kanuni (قانونى, the Legislator) by comparison with his predecessors, who were men of the sword. He issued a large number of Kanuns dealing especially with timars (fiefs), rayahs (subjects), ceremonies, and criminal and market regulations. His legislation was hindered by the conservatism of Islam, which held that old ways were best, and by the weakness inherent in despotic legislation — the lawgiver’s distance from those affected made true adaptation impossible.

A devout Moslem whose piety increased in old age, Suleiman took his duty of enforcing the Sheri seriously. If he did not enact measures directly to increase the common welfare, his attempts to regulate the tax and tenancy systems lightened the people’s condition. He used severe measures against extortion and strove by market and police regulations to maintain justice, fairness, and order.

Provincial Administration: Beylerbeys and Sancakbeys

Local government and the command of the feudal Spahis were cared for by officials belonging to the sultan’s slave-family, who brought to their posts a number of Spahis of the Porte, pages, Ajem-oghlans, and their own slaves, proportioned to their rank.

The hierarchy of local officials:

  • Subashis (سوباشى, captains): In time of peace, governors of towns, with enough Janissaries and Azabs to police the locality.
  • Alai Beys (آلاى بك, colonels): Ready with a company of two to five hundred troops to move as needed.
  • Sancak Beys (سنجاق بك, district governors): Governed important cities and held superior rule over a number of towns and their surrounding districts.
  • Beylerbeys (بكلربكى, “lord of lords”): Had authority over all the beys of their region. Two were ancient and principal: the Beylerbey of Rumelia (European provinces), who resided in Constantinople in peacetime, and the Beylerbey of Anatolia (western Asia Minor), who spent much time in his dominions.

In Suleiman’s time there were ten beylerbeys in total: the two ancient ones plus those of Karamania, Amasia, Avandole, Syria, Mesopotamia, Egypt, Hungary, and Temesvar. The latter eight, who had fewer feudal troops and greater need of them at home, were summoned to campaigns only when the war was in their region.

Incomes were provided by the assignment of fiefs (تيمار, timar) proportioned in size to each officer’s importance. Each beylerbey had his own mufti, reis effendi, and defterdar with clerks, who advised him, recorded his decisions, administred revenues from estates assigned to his household, and kept accounts of fief-holders in his dominion. Each sancak bey had a similar group on a lesser scale.

In time of war, this local government scheme drew together the feudal Spahis into a perfectly organized territorial army for each region. The army of Rumelia under its beylerbey had the right of the battle-line when fighting in Europe; the army of Anatolia had the right when fighting in Asia. The enrolled feudal troops of Europe numbered about fifty thousand and those of Asia about thirty thousand — figures that should be doubled or tripled to account for additional horsemen brought by each Spahi. About half remained on duty at home to protect the provinces.

This system — which prevented the accumulation of power in local hands — meant that no beylerbey or sancak bey could hope to rebel successfully. All were “strangers and foreigners in the countries they ruled,” holding their positions by the most insecure tenure. The empire was not destined to break up into small independent states so long as its two great institutions were consistently maintained.

The Ruling Institution as a Whole

The Ottoman Ruling Institution was, in its most essential aspect, a government. Its form was a despotism centered in the sultan, but greatly circumscribed by the Sheri — a rigid constitutional law grounded in strong religious belief and intense national conservatism. It maintained public order, defended the empire, and enlarged its possessions by conquest. A large proportion of its energies was devoted to obtaining and distributing the means of its own support, keeping its own machinery in order, and maintaining its authority. The idea of labor for the public welfare was not present.

It kept itself in power by being organized as an army: a standing army of cavalry and infantry with artillery, commissary, and transport services, controlling a much larger feudal and irregular army. Through the feudal army it kept the countryside in subjection; by garrisons it held the towns quiet.

It was also a great court and household, maintaining the pomp and ceremony due to an imperial state. Suleiman was called the Legislator and the Conqueror, but beyond both titles, the Magnificent — he shone as head of the court.

All its members were set off as a nobility by exemption from taxation and special jurisdiction, but nobility was not hereditary — a feature that prevented the entrenchment of families and kept every ambitious member a devoted slave, an indefatigable learner, and a dauntless warrior. “Every man was his own ancestry.”

The most characteristic feature was that its recruits were almost all drawn from children of Christian parents, and that they were expected to become Muslims before advancement. This was both a missionary enterprise and a practical method of obtaining single-hearted servants.

The next most characteristic feature was its educational quality. The Christian slaves were acquired young and trained with the greatest care — body, mind, and spirit. The immediate object was to fit boys for the sultan’s service in war and government, but they were also trained to adorn his ceremonies and to live by the principles of Islam. The results were well-nigh incredible: the men who guided Suleiman’s empire to unexampled glory were sons of peasants and herdsmen, of downtrodden Christian subjects, of unlettered and half-civilized parents. They became educated, cultured, and polished men.

“You are his slave and he is your lord… The Ottoman government… seems to have attained during the sixteenth century the highest degree of perfection of which its constitution was capable.”

— Robertson
