Ottoman Foreign Relations Under Bayezid II (1481-1512)

The foreign relations of the Ottoman Empire during the reign of Sultan Bayezid II (1481-1512) were fundamentally shaped by a single overriding factor: the presence of his younger brother, Prince Cem (Jem), as a hostage and pawn in the hands of western Christendom. For fourteen years, from Cem’s flight to Rhodes in July 1482 until his death at Capua in February 1495, Bayezid was forced to pursue a cautious, often conciliatory foreign policy, fearful that a coalition of European powers or the Mamluk Sultanate might place Cem at the head of an army and threaten the Ottoman throne itself. The death of Cem in 1495 marked a decisive turning point, after which the Porte could adopt a more aggressive stance — leading directly to the war with Venice (1499-1503) and the confrontation with the rising Safavid power of Shah Ismail.

The Cem Sultan Crisis: The Central Diplomatic Problem

The rivalry between Bayezid and his younger brother Cem began as a civil war (1481-1482) but was rapidly transformed into an international diplomatic crisis of the first order. After Cem’s defeat at the Battle of Yenishehir (June 20, 1481), he fled first to Egypt, where Sultan Kaitbay received him “as a son,” and then in the spring of 1482, with Kaitbay’s support (several thousand soldiers, forty thousand ducats, and orders on the governors of Aleppo and Damascus), he returned to Anatolia. His second attempt failed, and on July 26, 1482, Cem boarded a ship for Rhodes at Anamur, placing himself in the custody of the Knights of St. John.

Negotiations with the Knights of St. John (Rhodes)

The Knights of St. John, who had been a persistent thorn in the Ottoman side (Mehmed II had heavily attacked Rhodes in 1480), now held the most valuable diplomatic asset imaginable. D’Aubusson, the Grand Master, initially hoped to win allies and use Cem as an entering wedge to curb Ottoman power. He sent out appeals for a crusade to Pope Sixtus IV, the king of Naples, the king of Hungary, and the Venetian government — all agreed the time was propitious, but none moved a man.

By midsummer 1482, D’Aubusson knew a crusade was hopeless. He obtained a power of attorney from Cem, sent him to France for safekeeping, and opened negotiations with the Porte. The ambassadors Guy de Mont Arnaud and Leonard du Prat were instructed not to agree to pay any tribute or to send an annual ambassador. The resulting treaty established peace on land and sea, freedom of commerce for the duration of Bayezid’s life, the return of fugitive slaves (unless they had changed religion, in which case twenty-two ducats each would be paid), and the castle of St. Peter at Budrum was declared an inviolable asylum for fugitives.

But the real settlement was the secret treaty signed on December 7, 1482, between Bayezid’s envoy and D’Aubusson. For Cem’s confinement, Bayezid agreed to pay the Knights annually in August forty-five thousand Venetian ducats, plus a like sum for the past year. The treaty was approved by Bayezid on January 3, 1483. This was, in effect, a form of tribute paid to the Knights, and many vizirs complained bitterly. As Fisher notes, it was a “poignant illustration of the role Jem was to play in Ottoman foreign relations for the next twelve years.”

The Otranto Decision

The first major foreign policy decision of Bayezid’s reign was the abandonment of the Italian campaign. In August 1480, Gedik Ahmed Pasha had captured Otranto with ten thousand men, likely to secure control of the Straits of Otranto and the Albanian coast. Upon Mehmed II’s death, Bayezid forbade ships at Gallipoli to transport reinforcements. Gedik Ahmed was recalled to fight Cem, and the troops at Otranto — besieged by Alfonso, Duke of Calabria — were abandoned. They surrendered on September 10, 1481. “Never again during Bayezid’s reign did the Turks seriously threaten Italy,” Fisher observes, because Bayezid feared that such an attack might unite Italy against him.

Cem in the Hands of the Papacy and France

Cem was moved from Rhodes to France in 1482, then to Rome in 1489 under Pope Innocent VIII. The Pope invited all the kings of Christendom to join a league against the Turks. At a congress of Christian princes in Rome in March 1490, attended by an ambassador from the Sultan of Egypt, a crusade was outlined in detail: a papal fleet under the direction of the king of France, Spain, or England was to attack the Morea and Negroponte; a first army from the Italian states and the Pope; a second army from Germany, Hungary, Poland, and the Balkans; and a third army from France, Spain, and England — totaling twenty thousand cavalry and one hundred thousand infantry. But upon the death of King Korvin Matyas of Hungary, all plans were discarded.

Bayezid sent his kapujibashi (head gatekeeper), Mustafa, to Innocent VIII with 120,000 ducats (three years’ pension) and holy relics. He offered to pay annually for Cem’s support and agreed not to attack the Christians as long as Cem was confined — though “Christians” was understood to mean only Rhodes, Venice, and the Pope.

In 1494, when Charles VIII of France invaded Italy, Bayezid became intensely alarmed. Pope Alexander VI sent Georgio Buzardo to the Porte with a remarkable proposal: that Bayezid pay the annual pension early and send an ambassador to Venice to warn them to aid the Pope. Buzardo returned with a letter in which Bayezid not only yielded to everything the Pope wished but suggested that Cem “should be relieved of his tiresome journeys upon this earth,” promising three hundred thousand ducats when Cem’s body was delivered to an Ottoman port. This letter, dated September 15, 1494, has been questioned for authenticity but is given by both DaLezze and Sanuto.

Charles VIII forced Pope Alexander VI to consign Cem to him on January 11, 1495, for twenty thousand ducats for six months. Accompanied by Cem, Charles marched south. Bayezid, in consternation, called all pirate captains to Istanbul and hastily repaired and rebuilt the fleet. But Cem fell ill and died at Capua on February 25, 1495, probably of natural causes (pneumonia or erysipelas), though poison was widely suspected due to Bayezid’s letter to Alexander VI.

The death of Cem released Bayezid from the greatest constraint on his foreign policy. “For fourteen years,” Fisher writes, “Bayezid had been hampered in the formulation of an aggressive and positive foreign policy by the thought that his own subjects might turn against him and clamor for the return of Jem, and by the fear that some foreign enemy might obtain Jem, place him at the head of an army, and attack the Ottoman empire successfully.”

Relations with Venice

Venice was the Italian state most interested in Turkey and the Levant, and her citizens were the best informed. Relations went through three distinct phases: the peace of 1481-1499, the war of 1499-1503, and the peace of 1503 that defined the Ottoman-Venetian frontier for decades.

The Peace of 1482

Upon Mehmed II’s death, the ghazis of Serbia and Bosnia under Iskender Pasha began raiding Dalmatia, asserting that the former peace treaty with Venice terminated upon the death of the sultan. The Venetians, however, desired to maintain peace. They sent out four armed galleys to capture and return to the Turks the island of Cefalonia, which a Neapolitan force had just taken. The doge sent Antonio Vituri to present Cefalonia with his compliments to Bayezid and to renew the treaty.

The treaty signed in Istanbul on January 16, 1482, was very favorable to Venice: Venice was no longer required to pay the annual tribute of 10,000 ducats, Venetian customs duties were lowered from five to four percent, Venetians were to be indemnified for Ottoman-inflicted losses, slaves were to be returned, and commerce and the Venetian bailo were to be protected. Venice returned Cefalonia and settled 50,000 ducats in Ottoman claims. Gedik Ahmed Pasha protested that this was a weak treaty, but Bayezid “had Jem in mind and wished to keep Venice in a satisfied mood.”

Late in 1482, Iskender Pasha was sent to Venice to have the doge confirm the treaty and to negotiate about Cem. Bayezid offered the Morea to Venice as a reward or ransom for his brother if Cem fell into Venetian hands. The doge was skeptical, but Venice refused to accept Cem from the Duke of Savoy in July 1483.

Prelude to War (1496-1499)

After Cem’s death, the situation changed dramatically. The janissaries and sipahis grew restless without major campaigns. An outbreak occurred in Istanbul in the summer of 1496. Sensing this shift, Venice sent Alvise Sagudino in May 1496 to advise Bayezid of Venetian peaceful intentions, but Bayezid would not consent to a resident Venetian bailo, regarding such an official as an official spy.

By 1498, Bayezid was building a great fleet. Three thousand shipbuilders and carpenters were gathered in Istanbul; a ship of eighteen hundred tons was under construction. Notorious pirates were being commissioned to captain the ships. Venice, despite repeated warnings from Corfu, Chios, and her own bailos, entered preparations only halfheartedly.

The immediate causes of the war were numerous: frontier incidents along the line from Istria to Rhodes; the alliance Venice made with France (Louis XII); Venice’s protection of George Cernovitch, who had fled from the Cattaro district; the ambush and slaughter of five hundred Turks near Napoli di Romania in the spring of 1499; and the affair of the Sappho galley. But the fundamental causes were deeper: the Ottoman empire was in its prime vigor, ready to expand; the Venetian republic was decadent, vulnerable on land and sea; and Ottoman ghazis and sanjakbeys could not resist plundering Venetian cities.

The various Italian states played their own games. The duke of Milan, Lodovico Sforza, was particularly active in goading Bayezid to attack Venice, sending envoys to report that the Venetians had called France into Italy and that Louis XII planned to cross to Greece after conquering Italy. Sforza admitted in August 1499 that he, Bayezid, and Maximilian were leagued against Venice. Bayezid also signed a treaty with Naples on July 15, 1498 (confirmed July 25), though it was conditional on the return of Cem’s body.

The War with Venice (1499-1503)

The Ottoman fleet left the Straits on June 30, 1499. Estimates of its size range from 150 to 400 ships; a Venetian spy counted 78 regular galleys, 25 long galleys, and two new ships of 1,800 tons each — at that time the largest in the world. Fifteen thousand azabs were recruited as oarsmen. Bayezid himself led an army of 60,000-70,000 men.

The war opened with the rapid occupation of most of the Morea, except the coastal cities of Napoli di Romania, Coron, Modon, and Navarino. The first major naval engagement occurred off Navarino on August 12, 1499. The Turks had 241 ships to Venice’s 123, though the Venetians were stronger in large ships. The Venetian commander, Antonio Grimani, was hesitant and unskilled; he and Andrea Loredano were unfriendly. When Loredano attacked, Grimani held back. The Turkish captain Borrak, seeing his helplessness, fired his ship, and all three vessels went up in flames. The terrified Venetians gave up the contest.

Lepanto surrendered on August 28, 1499, to prevent its sack. The fall of Lepanto shocked the Morea. Along the Dalmatian coast, Iskender Pasha conducted devastating raids, reaching as far as Friuli and Carinthia, crossing the Isonzo River, and sending a cavalry force across the Tagliamento.

In the 1500 campaign, Bayezid launched an even greater fleet — 34 new galleys at Lepanto, 26 at Prevesa, 30 at Valona — again numbering about 250 ships. Modon was stormed on August 10, 1500, the most important key to Venetian trade in the Levant. Coron capitulated on August 15, and Navarino soon after. Only Napoli di Romania and Monemvasia remained Venetian in the Morea.

The combined Spanish and Venetian fleets captured Cefalonia on December 24, 1500. Santa Maura fell to Venice on August 30, 1502. But Venice was exhausted — eleven extra taxes had been passed in 1500 alone, the treasury and arsenal were nearly empty, and Venetian credit was seriously impaired.

The Treaty of 1503

Andrea Gritti, the leading Venetian merchant in Turkey and a warm friend of Grand Vizir Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha, was instrumental in bringing about peace. Zaccaria di Freschi reached Istanbul on December 7, 1502. The Turkish proposals of December 14, 1502, contained thirty-one articles providing for: the restitution of Santa Maura; the delimitation of frontiers at Cattaro, Napoli di Romania, and Monemvasia; an annual payment of 500 ducats from Zante to Turkey; free trade for Venetians; the return of fugitive slaves; and the residence of a Venetian bailo for one year.

After difficult negotiations, the treaty was concluded on July 30, 1503, formally signed and celebrated on August 10, 1503. Gritti secured the extension of the bailo’s residence from one to three years and raised the official valuation of the ducat from fifty to fifty-four aspers. Santa Maura was consigned to Turkey on August 31, 1503.

The war had been disastrous for Venice. Turkey now possessed Durazzo, Lepanto, Modon, and Coron, with near-complete control of Greece and Albania. Venice never regained her prestige in Levantine trade. For the Ottoman empire, the victory was one of the most significant in its annals: it sealed the fate of the eastern Mediterranean for the following three hundred years, established Turkey as a great sea power, and gave it the economic strength that underpinned the later campaigns of Selim I and Suleiman the Magnificent.

Relations with Hungary

During the reign of Mehmed II, relations between the Porte and Hungary had been marked by great enmity, and hostilities opened upon the change of sultans. In 1481, forces under Iskender Pasha of Serbia crossed into Hungary but were repulsed by Kinizsi Pal at Temesvar.

Korvin Matyas, king of Hungary, tried in many ways to embarrass Bayezid. He was in constant communication with D’Aubusson, Sixtus IV, and Louis XI, seeking to become Cem’s jailor. When this failed, a false pretender — purporting to be a brother of Mehmed II — was produced, but this came to nothing. In 1483, after Bayezid had fortified the Danube and Korvin had become engaged in a quarrel with the king of Bohemia, a five-year truce was signed between Turkey and Hungary.

From the treaty of 1483 until Korvin’s death in 1490, peace existed between the Porte and Hungary. Friendly embassies journeyed back and forth, and in 1488 the truce was renewed for three additional years. Nevertheless, Korvin was hoping to acquire Cem and attack the Turks. He began organizing a three-year crusade in 1489 that was to include the Pope, all Italy, France, Spain, England, the Empire, and Poland — but his death in 1490 ended all plans.

After Korvin’s death, a succession struggle ensued between his son, the Emperor Maximilian, and Vladislav of Bohemia. Vladislav was victorious. Bayezid, hoping to take Belgrade, sent the bey of Semendra to storm it. The Turks took the castle of Narenta in 1490, but Kinizsi Pal inflicted a severe defeat when he forced them to raise the siege of Szoreny in 1492.

Bayezid turned toward Albania and, with his fleet, ravaged parts of the country. Large raiding parties (regular ghazas) were sent into Dalmatia, Croatia, Carniola, Styria, and Carinthia almost yearly from 1483. In 1492, they reached Cilly, Villach, and St. Veit before being repulsed. In September 1493, Yakub Pasha with eight thousand sipahis advanced to Cilly, and in a Croatian civil war, captured the leader Derenczeny, whom Bayezid richly rewarded.

The Emperor Maximilian chased the ghazis from Styria in 1494 and pursued them into Serbia. Peter More was sent to Istanbul to seek peace. A treaty sworn in 1495 established peace for three years, with Bayezid agreeing to return all Christian prisoners taken during the two previous years.

After Cem’s death, hostilities resumed. In 1496-1498, the Porte was embroiled in the Polish-Hungarian conflict over Moldavia. A truce was signed between Bayezid and Maximilian, King Albert of Poland, and Vladislav of Hungary in July 1499, partly to free Bayezid’s hand for the coming war with Venice. During the Venetian war, Hungary was drawn in on Venice’s side, but Vladislav was reluctant to spend his subsidy, and Bayezid’s attention was diverted by the Persian threat. The Turco-Hungarian treaty of 1503 established peace for seven years. This was renewed for one year in 1510 and for five more years in the winter of 1511-1512.

Relations with the Mamluk Sultanate

Relations between the Ottoman Porte and the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt had been unfriendly since the reign of Mehmed II. The Mamluk sultan Kaitbay had received Cem in the fall of 1481 and aided his return to Turkey in the spring of 1482, which Bayezid regarded as a hostile act. Since the decline of Seljuk power, the Ottoman and Mamluk states had met with considerable opposition whenever moves were made to bring the tribes of southern Anatolia and northern Syria into one empire or the other. Border emirs played off Mamluk against Ottoman incessantly.

One such prince was Alaeddevlet of Sulqadr, who attempted to become independent of Kaitbay in the summer of 1482. Kaitbay sent an army against him; Alaeddevlet called upon Bayezid for support. The resulting conflict drew both empires into war. Simultaneously, the Karaman tribes under Karagoz occupied Adana and Tarsus, which were delivered to the Porte.

Bayezid desired peace and sent an ambassador to Egypt, but the Porte was unwilling to return the Adana district. Kaitbey regarded the Ottoman acceptance as stolen property and would not recognize Bayezid’s lordship there. Fighting escalated through 1485, with Bayezid committing larger forces after establishing peace with Hungary, Venice, Ragusa, and the Danubian provinces.

The war was marked by dramatic reversals. In 1486, the Egyptian army under Uzbey captured Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha, who was paraded through Cairo in chains and forced to kiss Kaitbay’s foot. But Bayezid used the defeat to reorganize the armed forces: janissary numbers were increased, artillery strengthened, the fleet put into order with new galleys, and a marine corps established.

The major campaign of 1488 saw Ali Pasha lead about 60,000 men south, taking Adana and Tarsus, while Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha as kapudan (admiral) commanded a fleet of eighty sail. Venice, to protect Cyprus, sent a fleet of forty galleys under Francesco di Prioli, arriving two days before the Ottoman fleet and preventing Hersekoglu from landing at Famagusta. The battle near Adana on August 17, 1488, was indecisive — the Ottoman right wing crumpled, the Karamanians fled, and Ali Pasha was nearly surrounded. Adana fell to the Egyptians on April 1, 1489.

Both nations were ready for peace by 1491. The Tunisian ambassador, passing through Egypt on his way to seek Ottoman aid against Spain, helped mediate. The mufti of Bursa, Zeineddin Ali, brought about the rapprochement. Adana, Tarsus, and a fortress on the frontier were ceded to Egypt but were to be considered pious foundations belonging to Mecca and Medina. The treaty was signed in 1491. “Both Turkey and Egypt had suffered greatly from loss of men and money,” Fisher notes. Egypt was confirmed in her possession of the Adana district but did not succeed in drawing the border tribes under her protection. The six campaigns had been very costly (estimated at one hundred thousand losses), but the war greatly weakened the non-Ottoman tribes in the south, who remained quiet and submissive for ten years.

After 1491, relations improved. Bayezid married a daughter to the Mamluk sultan in 1501 to cement the friendship, and ambassadors were exchanged to discuss Persian affairs and the Syrian frontier. The rise of Shah Ismail created common cause between the two Sunni powers. In 1510, an Egyptian ambassador came to Edirne to secure iron, artillery, and ships for the Mamluk fleet fighting the Portuguese in the Indian Ocean. Kemal was sent from Istanbul with a small fleet heavily laden with naval supplies and experts, though it was wrecked near Samos. Bayezid sent timber for thirty ships, three hundred iron guns, one hundred fifty masts, three thousand oars, sails, and much other equipment to Egypt.

Relations with Safavid Persia

The rise of Shah Ismail and the Safavid dynasty presented the Ottoman Empire with an entirely new challenge — one that was not merely political but religious and ideological. Ismail made the Shiite faith the official religion of Persia in 1502, exiling or executing those who did not conform. His followers, the kizil-bashis (red-heads) who wore distinctive red hats, belonged to the Bektashi order of dervishes, the same order to which many Ottoman janissaries belonged. This religious affinity among the border tribes of Anatolia posed a grave internal threat.

Ismail’s ancestry was formidable: on the paternal side, he claimed descent from the Prophet through Fatima and Ali and the seventh Imam; on the maternal side, he had the blood of the kings of Persia and the Christian emperors of Constantinople (his mother was the daughter of Uzun Hasan and a princess who was the daughter of Kalo Johannes, a Greek emperor of Trebizond). He was still in his teens when he began his political career.

Ahmed Mirza and Initial Persian Contacts

Even before Ismail’s full rise, Bayezid had attempted to build ties with Persia. In 1489, Bayezid gave three of his daughters in marriage, one to Ahmed Mirza, a Persian noble and grandson of Uzun Hasan. In 1496, Persian nobles invited Ahmed Mirza to become their ruler. He accepted and assumed the throne, but the country did not receive him. Ahmed Mirza was given little support by Bayezid because of the impending Venetian war, and he was soon replaced by Ismail, who attacked the Taurus region in 1499. This was the beginning of a much larger affair.

The Ismail Threat and Ottoman Response

Bayezid initially tried to downplay the threat, attempting to brand Ismail’s followers as non-Muslims, but the border tribes of Karaman, Turgud, Warsak, and Sulqadr — traditionally ghazi in spirit and not interested in fine theological distinctions — flocked to Ismail. Many fled the Ottoman empire to join him. Ismail’s generosity (he divided the booty of captured cities wholly among his followers) and his common Turkish language and customs (he wrote popular mystic poems under the pen name Hatayi) made him immensely attractive.

In 1502, Ismail’s cousins Murad and Alvand defied him from the Taurus Mountains, and Ismail set out from Erzingan toward Karahisar, within the Ottoman frontier. Bayezid gathered an army under the beylerbey of Anatolia and ordered the Rumelian troops to be ready to cross to Asia. Minor skirmishes ensued, but Ismail was forced to retire to crush an uprising in Persia. Ambassadors came to Istanbul to propose peace. Bayezid, after the loss of Santa Maura, preferred to make peace.

Diplomacy to Avoid War (1503-1511)

For the remainder of Bayezid’s reign, the policy was one of containing Ismail through diplomacy rather than open war. When Ismail conquered Baghdad in 1504 and moved toward Mardin and Diarbekir, Bayezid dispatched an ambassador to congratulate him on the conquest of Fars and Iraq — though the Turkish envoy was reportedly forced to eat pork and witness the execution of Sunni theologians. The Persian ambassador who came to Istanbul in 1505 was guarded closely; even the money he spent was collected and the holders reimbursed to prevent the circulation of Shiite heresy.

When Ismail entered Ottoman territory near Karahisar in 1507, Bayezid sent Yahya Pasha to Ankara with many janissaries, azabs, and timarjis, and established camps at Akseray, Kaysari, and Amasya — but all were ordered not to come to blows with the Persians. Bayezid feared the Sofi greatly, not only because of the proximity of his army but because of the great number of Ottomans, both soldiers and civilians, who were deserting the Porte and joining the Sofis. One of Bayezid’s sons wrote that it would be impossible to form an army against the Sofi if the sultan did not come in person.

In 1508, a Persian ambassador arrived simultaneously with an envoy from the Tartar leader Shahabey Khan, who was invading Persia from the east. Bayezid “accorded honor to both embassies, hoping that neither Ismail nor Shahabey Khan would be successful.” Gifts and expressions of friendship were exchanged. In 1509, Ismail sent envoys to Venice to secure artillery and possibly an alliance against Turkey, but Venice was occupied with Italian wars and in no position to offend the Porte. The envoys passed through Egypt, almost bringing Turkey and Egypt to blows, but the Mamluk sultan arrested all the Venetians in Aleppo, satisfying Bayezid.

The Shahkulu Rebellion (1511)

The greatest crisis came in 1511 with the rebellion of Shah Kuli (also called Karabik) in Anatolia. Shah Kuli had escaped persecution for years and now gathered forces from Attalya and Tekke. The revolt grew like a giant snowball. In May 1511, Shah Kuli and his kizil-bashis assaulted and took Kutahya, capturing Karagoz Pasha, the beylerbey of Anatolia, and then proceeded toward Bursa.

The news “almost paralyzed Istanbul.” Grand Vizir Ali Pasha crossed to Asia with four thousand janissaries and sipahis. The decisive battle occurred at Sarmusaklu near Kaysari. Ali Pasha attacked boldly, perhaps to prevent his own Bektashi janissaries from having time to consider the beliefs of those they were fighting. Ali Pasha was killed, but so was Shah Kuli. The rebellion subsided, though the Ottoman soldiers then turned to pillaging in Anatolia.

Throughout the rebellion, Ismail’s ambassador was at the Porte, closely guarded to prevent him from learning of the dissension among Bayezid’s sons. Bayezid feared that if Ismail learned of the succession struggle, he might actively aid the Turkish Sofis. Ismail executed some of the fugitive rebels who fled to Persia, likely to show that the rebellion had not been sponsored by him. For the remaining year of Bayezid’s life, harmony reigned between the two governments officially, though Ahmed would later ally with Persia against Selim.

Relations with Poland-Lithuania and Moldavia

The region north of the Danube was a persistent area of Ottoman expansion. In 1484, Bayezid led a campaign in person against Kilia and Akerman. The Ottoman fleet carried food, guns, war supplies, and siege artillery to the Danube by way of the Black Sea. Ottoman troops crossed the Danube and with the aid of Wallachian troops besieged Kilia, which surrendered in July. Reinforced by a band of Tartars under Mengli Khan, they proceeded to Akerman, which capitulated in August. Kadis were appointed, churches converted into mosques.

Karabogdan (Stephen the Great) of Moldavia, after being defeated, submitted and sent tribute. Bayezid sent him bodies of akinjis to augment his forces for action against Poland, but Karabogdan was not loyal and aided revolts in Kilia and Akerman. Ali Pasha marched into Moldavia to re-subject the province. In 1486, Malkochoglu Bali subdued the region, and in 1487 Wallachia and Moldavia sent envoys with tribute and two years’ arrears.

The first treaty between the Porte and Poland was signed in 1490 and renewed in 1493 for three years. In 1496, King Albert of Poland invaded Moldavia. An army of eighty thousand set out under Ali Pasha of Silistra to recover Kilia and Akerman. The Turks were routed several times, but the Christian league had no overwhelming victories. Bayezid sent an ambassador to Vladislav to separate Hungary from Poland. A truce was signed between Turkey and Hungary. Ali Pasha crossed the Danube again in April 1498 with 60,000 men, returning with more than 10,000 captives. A larger army in September crossed the Dniester on a bridge of boats, plundering a large area and taking many fortresses. King Albert sought aid from Venice and Hungary, both of whom refused.

Winter and a shortage of food saved the Poles. In July 1499, a truce was drawn up between Bayezid and Maximilian, Albert, and Vladislav. Eight camels carried presents from the Porte to Hungary; Vladislav sent costly gifts to Bayezid.

Relations with Russia

The Ottoman advance to Akerman impressed Tsar Ivan III with Ottoman power. In a conference at Bielgor’od, Russia indicated she wished good relations with the Porte. Mengli Khan of the Crimea was appointed to represent the tsar at Istanbul. In a letter dated August 31, 1492, Ivan III complained that Russian merchants in Azov and Kaffa had been forbidden to trade and discriminated against in taxation. The letter went unanswered.

In 1495, Ivan III sent Michel Plesttscheief to Istanbul, instructed to secure privileges for Russian traders, not to bend the knee to the sultan, to treat directly with the sultan, and not to cede his place to any other ambassador. Plesttscheief remained cold and haughty, refusing even to attend a dinner in his honor. Bayezid wrote to Mengli Khan that he “blushed to think of the affronts to which he had submitted” and did not intend to send a return ambassador. Nevertheless, he granted all the privileges requested — a striking illustration of Bayezid’s caution during the Cem years.

In 1499, Ivan III sent a new ambassador, Alexis Golokvastof, who was received more favorably. He returned with a commercial treaty.

Relations with the Crimean Khanate

The Crimean Khanate under Mengli Khan was a crucial Ottoman ally. Crimean Tartars participated in the 1484 campaign against Kilia and Akerman. The khans were also used as intermediaries with Russia. Mengli Khan represented the Russian tsar at Istanbul in the early 1490s.

Bayezid gave a daughter in marriage to a Tartar prince. One of Bayezid’s sons, Mahmud, who resided at Kaffa, married a Tartar princess — though this led to conflict when Mahmud closed all Crimean ports during a grain shortage in 1505, so angering Bayezid that he sent poisoned gifts to his son. The Tartar alliance continued to be important during the succession struggle, with Selim securing the support of the Tartar khan.

Tartar forces also attacked Russia and Poland on Bayezid’s behalf during the Venetian war, providing a useful diversion. In 1508, the Tartars under Shahabey Khan invaded Persia from the east, forcing Ismail to divide his attention and giving Bayezid a diplomatic opening.

The Barbary Corsairs and the Western Mediterranean

The Ottoman relationship with the Barbary corsairs was still in its early stages during Bayezid’s reign. Spanish attacks on the Moors of North Africa led them to seek aid from the Porte. Bayezid’s involvement was limited — he sent Kemal with some ships to Tunis, but the Porte was primarily concerned with its own wars and the Persian threat.

The corsairs in the Aegean and the eastern Mediterranean, however, were actively encouraged. Such names as Kemal, Karahasan, Enrichi, Barbeta, and Karafa struck fear in Christian traders. Each of these pirates, at one time or another, served as a captain or squadron leader in the Ottoman navy during peacetime and was called to the fleet in time of war. Enrichi had been appointed subashi of Negroponte and from there raided Christian commerce, dividing the profit with Bayezid. The capture of Modon in 1500 gave the Ottomans a base of operations that would later support the activities of the Barbary corsairs in the central and western Mediterranean.

Ottoman Naval Policy and Shipbuilding

Bayezid II’s reign saw the transformation of the Ottoman Empire into a major naval power. The war with Egypt (1485-1491) had already spurred significant naval construction: a fleet of 150 sail was prepared in 1485, and during the reorganization after the 1486 defeat, the fleet was “put into order, new galleys were added to it to enable it to aid the land forces more readily, and a marine corps was established.”

But it was the Venetian war (1499-1503) that truly established Ottoman naval supremacy. Shipbuilding was conducted at Istanbul, Gallipoli, Ismid, Sinope on the Black Sea, Salonica, Negroponte, Lepanto, Prevesa, and Valona. The scale was immense: in 1499, 15,000 azabs were recruited as oarsmen; 3,000 shipbuilders and carpenters were gathered in Istanbul; at night, work was done by torchlight to complete the fleet. Galleys were modeled along Venetian lines, while galleons followed French designs.

After the war, Bayezid ordered the building of light galleys at multiple ports. He was “greatly pleased with the lines of a captured Venetian galley” and sent it to Sinope to have fifty more built on the same model. No more large galleons were built because galleys were found more manageable and practical. Turkish galleys built at Prevesa were made of great double oak timbers and were two feet wider and two yards longer than Venetian galleys.

By 1496, the Ottoman fleet numbered about 250 ships, including 100 galleys, 50 fuste, 50 brigs, and 10 large ships. The largest ships at that time were two of 1,800 tons each. The war demonstrated that Ottoman sea captains, initially afraid of Venetian galleys, gained confidence and repeatedly proved their superiority. Fisher concludes: “The numbers and the power of the Turkish ships gave to the Ottoman empire the strength to rule the Greek Archipelago, and from this war until the nineteenth century most of the islands in the Aegean were tributaries of the Ottoman sultans.”

The Role of Tribute (Harac) and Gifts in Ottoman Diplomacy

Tribute payments and gift-giving were central to Ottoman foreign relations. The payment of 45,000 Venetian ducats annually to the Knights of St. John for Cem’s confinement was a humiliating but necessary form of tribute. Bayezid also received tribute: Karabogdan of Moldavia paid annual tribute after 1484; Ragusa paid an annual tribute reduced from its earlier level to 3,000 ducats; Zante paid 500 ducats annually to Turkey after the 1503 treaty.

Gift-giving was used strategically. Bayezid sent the alleged right hand of John the Baptist to D’Aubusson. He offered Charles VIII the Holy Sepulcher and Jerusalem, many holy relics, and fifty thousand ducats annually if Cem were kept in France. He sent holy relics (the lance that pierced Christ’s side and the sponge soaked with vinegar) to Pope Innocent VIII. After the peace with Egypt in 1491, the keys of Adana and Tarsus were presented to Cairo as pious foundations — “no doubt this scheme was arranged to save Bayezid’s face.”

Presents flowed in both directions. Eight camels carried presents from the Porte to Hungary; Vladislav sent costly gifts in return. The Tunisian ambassador brought about peace between Egypt and Turkey. The Indian ambassador arrived with elephants, giraffes, gold, and fine spices — though his presents were robbed by the Egyptians. When conflict arose between Bayezid and Ivan III, it was partly over protocol: Plesttscheief refused to bend the knee or attend a dinner, and the breach was only mended through Mengli Khan’s mediation.

The Diplomatic Machinery: Ambassadors, Envoys, Gifts, Letters

The diplomatic apparatus of the Porte under Bayezid was sophisticated and active. Ambassadors traveled constantly between Istanbul and the courts of Europe. Venetian ambassadors who visited the Porte included Antonio Vituri (1482), Giovanni Dario (1483, 1485, 1487), Domenico Bollant and Francesco Aurelio (1483), Benedetto Trevisano (1485), Alvise Sagudino (1496, 1497), Andrea Zanchani (1499), Alvise Manenti (1500), and Andrea Gritti (1503). Ottoman ambassadors were sent to Venice, Naples, Rome, France, Hungary, Poland, Egypt, and Persia.

The Venetian bailo — a resident ambassador — was regarded by the Porte as an official spy. Bayezid refused to accept a bailo from Venice for years after 1492. Only in the 1503 treaty did he concede a bailo, limited to one year (later extended to three years by Gritti). The Porte preferred that Venetian merchants elect one of themselves as representative.

The grand vizirs and pashas conducted the actual negotiations. Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha, who was the son of the last Duke of Herzegovina and had been educated in the palace school, was particularly active in Venetian relations. He had been captured twice by the Egyptians (1486, 1490) and knew the diplomatic terrain intimately. Ibrahim Pasha, the grand vizir before him, had been one of Bayezid’s teachers. Daud Pasha, a later grand vizir, was noted for being friendly to Florentine and other Italian merchants. These vizirs often had their own sources of income from foreign powers — Hersekoglu was “liberally bribed by the Venetians,” while Daud was “bought by the other Italians.”

Intelligence was gathered through multiple channels. Venetian merchants in Istanbul informed their government when the vizirs tried to deceive them. Andrea Gritti, the wealthiest Venetian merchant in Istanbul, kept Venice well informed. Ottoman envoys and spies also reported on European affairs. Bayezid’s questioning of visiting ambassadors — asking about Italy, France, the relative power of rulers — was a form of diplomatic intelligence gathering.

Diplomatic correspondence was an important tool. Bayezid’s letter of September 15, 1494 to Pope Alexander VI — proposing Cem’s death for 300,000 ducats — remains one of the most remarkable diplomatic documents of the age, though its authenticity has been questioned. Treaties were written in Latin (which the Porte considered less binding than Turkish versions) or in Turkish. A treaty in Latin, Zanchani learned, “would not be considered valid by the Porte.”

Relations with Other Italian States

Beyond Venice, Bayezid maintained active diplomacy with a constellation of Italian states. Florence sent Andrea de’Medici in 1488 to draw up a commercial treaty, securing a two percent duty on imports. The Florentines were the Porte’s favored Italian merchants, and their consul in Istanbul was formally recognized as the emino (administrator) of Florentine affairs. The duke of Milan, Lodovico Sforza, was the archenemy of Venice at the Porte, sending skillful diplomats who corrupted pashas and goading Bayezid toward war. The dukes of Ferrara and Mantua also sent envoys urging attack on Venice. King Federico of Naples signed a treaty in 1498 that was conditional on the return of Cem’s body, and Bayezid threatened to send twenty-five thousand Turkish soldiers to aid Naples against its enemies in exchange for Taranto.

Relations with France

France entered the picture primarily through Cem’s captivity. Charles VIII held Cem from January 1495 until Cem’s death a month later. Bayezid sent an ambassador to persuade Charles VIII to keep Cem in France, offering the Holy Sepulcher, holy relics, and fifty thousand ducats annually. After the Venetian war began, Louis XII sent two ambassadors to mediate in April 1500, but the French mission could not reconcile the conflicting demands. Louis XII’s alliance with Venice was a major factor in Bayezid’s decision to declare war. Bayezid was not afraid of the French king — when French envoys threatened that France and all her allies would attack, Bayezid named his terms and dismissed them.

Relations with the Knights of Rhodes

After Cem’s death, Bayezid’s relations with the Knights of Rhodes deteriorated. He broke relations when he learned they had no further power over Cem. During the Venetian war, Rhodes ordered artillery and munitions from France and Venice, preparing for a Turkish attack. But Bayezid signed a treaty with the grand master in 1499 that eliminated Rhodes as an immediate target and allowed the fleet to sail for Corfu instead.

Legacy

Bayezid II’s reign saw the Ottoman Empire emerge from a defensive crouch forced by the Cem crisis to become the dominant power in the eastern Mediterranean. The acquisition of Lepanto, Modon, Coron, Navarino, and Durazzo gave the Ottomans near-complete control of Greece and Albania. The victory over Venice established Turkey as a great sea power. Peace with Egypt neutralized the southern front, while diplomacy kept the Safavid threat at bay. Commercial relations flourished with nearly all the known world, and Bayezid “was undoubtedly the wealthiest monarch of his age.” The revenues and strategic positions he secured made possible the spectacular campaigns of his son Selim I (conquest of Syria, the Holy Land, and Egypt) and his grandson Suleiman the Magnificent (capture of Rhodes and Belgrade, the siege of Vienna). As Fisher concludes, although Bayezid has been unfairly overshadowed by his father and his successors, “during the years of his reign, the empire which his father so brilliantly created became firmly established as one of the great powers of the world.”
