The Devsirme and the Palace School (Enderun)

The Ruling Institution as Slave-Family

Perhaps no more daring experiment has been tried on a large scale upon the face of the earth than that embodied in the Ottoman Ruling Institution. Its nearest ideal analogue is found in the Republic of Plato, its nearest actual parallel in the Mameluke system of Egypt. The Ottoman system deliberately took slaves and made them ministers of state; it took boys from the sheep-run and the plow-tail and made them courtiers and the husbands of princesses; it took young men whose ancestors had borne the Christian name for centuries, and made them rulers in the greatest of Mohammedan states, and soldiers and generals in invincible armies whose chief joy was to beat down the Cross and elevate the Crescent. It never asked its novices, “Who was your father?” or “What do you know?” or even “Can you speak our tongue?”; but it studied their faces and their frames and said, “You shall be a soldier, and if you show yourself worthy, a general,” or, “You shall be a scholar and a gentleman, and if the ability lies in you, a governor and a prime minister.”

The members of this system — numbering perhaps eighty thousand in Suleiman’s time — were, as long as they lived, at once slaves, proselytes, students, soldiers, nobles, courtiers, and officers of government. Every one who belonged to the Ruling Institution in any capacity from gardener to grand vizier, save only the members of the royal family, bore the title of kul (قول, slave) of the sultan. The power of the sultan over the lives, persons, and property of the kullar, and his right to their absolute obedience, derived directly from the idea of slavery. The very word despot means by derivation the master of slaves, and it was only over his kullar that the sultan’s power was despotic in the fullest sense.

Recruitment: The Devsirme

Entrance to the system came by the door of slavery, which was open regularly only to Christian boys from ten to twenty years of age. The preferred ages were between fourteen and eighteen. Four methods were employed for obtaining recruits: capture, purchase, gift, and tribute. Of these, the devşirme (دوشیرمه, “collecting” or “gathering”) — the tribute of children — is the most famous, though it furnished perhaps less than half of the annual recruits. The annual levy of tribute boys averaged about three thousand in the sixteenth century.

The Levying Process

The devşirme was accomplished by a regular process. Normally every four years, but oftener in case of need, a body of officials — described by contemporaries as more skilled in judging boys than trained horse-dealers in judging colts — was sent out by the government to the Christian provinces. The whole of the Balkan Peninsula, Hungary, the western coast of Asia Minor, and the southern and eastern shores of the Black Sea were included in the territory visited; the strongest and ablest youths came from the mountain regions inhabited by Albanians and the Southern Slavic peoples.

The recruiting officers were commissioned each to bring in a certain number apportioned to them out of a total determined at the capital. There was no principle of tithing, and no fixed proportion or number of boys was levied from each village or family. A regular procedure was followed: the officers obtained from the Christian priest of the village a list of the boys he had baptized between the ages of twelve and twenty years; all these were brought before the officers, who selected the best. Parents who had strong and well-favored sons might lose them all, while those who had weaklings would lose none. On leaving each village, the officer took with him the boys he had selected and delivered them to the capital.

Reaction of the Subject Population

The grief at parting was often a heart-breaking thing to witness; the mother whose son was taken by force to an unknown life among enemies of all that she had been taught to hold dear would hardly have suffered more at the death of her son. Yet she might hope to see him one day in possession of great wealth and power. Parents who wished to keep their sons would sometimes marry them in tender years, since married boys were ineligible; those who had means bought exemption from the recruiting officers. On the contrary, many parents were glad to have their sons chosen, knowing that they would escape from grinding poverty, receive a first-rate training suited to their abilities, and enter upon the possibility of a great career. Some parents came to regard the process as a privilege rather than a burden; Turkish parents envied them the opportunity, and sometimes tried to evade the regulations by paying Christians to take their Moslem sons and declare them as Christian children, so that they might be enrolled as the sultan’s slaves.

Selection and Classification

All recruits were brought before trained officials, carefully registered, and divided into two classes. Those who best satisfied the criteria of bodily perfection, muscular strength, and intellectual ability — about one in every ten of the whole number — were chosen for a superior quality of training, especially on the intellectual side. These regularly became pages (iç oğlan, ايج اوغلان, “inside youth”) and kapıkulu (قپوقولی) Spahis of the Porte, and the ablest rose to the great offices of army and government. The remainder were destined for a different education, mainly physical: they became acemi oğlan (عجمی اوغلان, “foreign youth” or apprentice Janissaries) and later Janissaries. But the ablest of these might also rise to positions as Spahis of the Porte and even as generals and officers of state. Merit was recognized everywhere and regularly led to promotion.

The Missionary Motive

Although almost every member of the governing group in the Ottoman Empire had been born a Christian, it was absolutely necessary for his advancement that he should profess the Moslem faith. The fundamental rule was very simple: every member of the Ruling Institution must have been born a Christian and must have become a Mohammedan.

The missionary purpose stood out clearly in the words attributed to Kara Khalil Chendereli, the traditional founder of the Janissary corps: “The conquered are slaves of the conquerors, to whom their goods, their women, and their children belong as a lawful possession; in converting the children to Islam by force, and in enrolling them as soldiers in the service of the faith, one is working for their happiness in this world and their eternal salvation.”

Conversion was not ordinarily forcible. The Ottomans were too wise to believe that the best results could be accomplished by such means. Their policy was rather to throw every difficulty in the way of remaining a Christian, and to offer every inducement to make the Moslem faith and system seem attractive. The educational scheme involved complete isolation from Christian ideas of every sort and complete saturation in all the ideas of Mohammedanism — religious, moral, social, and political. Even those whose education was mainly physical were isolated from Christians in a strict Moslem environment. “The Turks rejoice greatly when they find an exceptional man,” wrote one observer, “as though they had acquired a precious object, and they spare no labor or effort in cultivating him.”

Yet not all who turned Moslem did so without mental reservation. It was possible to hold fast to an inward belief in the superiority of Christianity through many years spent in the sultan’s service. The grand vizier Rustem told Busbecq, after offering him great rewards if he would turn Moslem, that he believed in the salvation of those of other faiths. A deli (scout) in his service confided to a French gentleman that while he pretended to follow Mohammedanism, he was a Christian at heart. The probability is that large numbers of the sultan’s slaves were merely nominal Mohammedans in religious belief, though they necessarily followed the larger part of the Moslem scheme of life.

The Palace School: Enderun

The Enderun (اندرون, “inside” or “interior”) — the palace school — was the most remarkable educational institution of its age. Those selected for the higher training, to the number of perhaps two hundred annually, or twelve to fifteen hundred in all, were taken into three palaces of the sultan as iç oğlanlar (pages). Three or four hundred were in the palace at Adrianople, a like number in one at Galata, and from five to eight hundred in the principal palace at Stamboul (the Saray-ı Cedid, or Topkapı Palace). These were all handsome boys, physically perfect, and of marked intellectual promise. A Venetian writer recorded that the pages included Bulgarians, Hungarians, Transylvanians, Poles, Bohemians, Germans, Italians, Spaniards, a few French, many Albanians, Slavs, Greeks, Circassians, and Russians.

The Colleges and Their Curriculum

The pages were organized into chambers (odalar, اوده لر). The two principal schools at the main palace were the Yeni Oda (New Chamber) and the Eski Oda (Old Chamber). Above these stood the chambers of personal service — the Seferli Odası (Campaign Chamber), the Kiler Odası (Pantry Chamber), the Hazine Odası (Treasury Chamber), and at the summit the Has Oda (خاص اوده, Private Chamber), containing thirty-nine gentlemen of the bedchamber who were the elite of all, chosen by selection after selection.

Menavino, who passed through the school, described the course of study in the Yeni Oda, which contained from eighty to a hundred boys: “When a boy has remained five or six days in that school, they set him to learning the alphabet. There are four teachers in the school. One drills the boys in reading during their first year. Another teaches the Koran in the Arabic language, giving explanations of the different articles of their faith. After this a third teaches books in the Persian tongue, and some write a little, but they do not teach writing willingly. A fourth teaches Arabic books, both vulgar and literary.”

The chief object of the course was to teach reading and writing for the purpose of giving inspection into the books of law and religion, especially the Koran. Arabic was taught to enable the boys to inspect the writings of the judges and to have knowledge of religion; Persian to give them quaint words and handsome and gentle deportment. They were not taught logic, physics, metaphysics, mathematics, or geography, but they received thorough instruction in Turkish history and the various Kanun-nameh (قانون نامه) — the imperial law codes — of the sultans. Most of the teachers were Anatolian Turks, chosen for imparting better pronunciation and more orthodox religious views.

Physical and Military Training

The pages were trained in the art of war, the use of all sorts of arms, and good horsemanship. Suleiman took especial delight in watching their cavalry evolutions, and occasionally summoned a page who pleased him, conversed with him, and dismissed him with presents. By old Oriental custom, every page was taught some handicraft useful in his master’s service, intended to provide for his own support in case of need.

Discipline and Daily Life

Discipline was severe but kept within bounds. A page could be beaten on the soles of his feet with no more than ten strokes, and not more than once on any one day. The boys were organized in groups of ten, watched carefully by eunuchs both day and night. Absolute obedience, modest behavior and decorum, and good manners were taught with great insistency. Rewards in the form of pay were given from the start. Menavino recorded: “These boys have a daily allowance of two aspers during the first year, three during the second year, four during the third year, and thus their allowance increases each year. They receive scarlet garments twice a year, and some robes of white cloth for the summer.”

Graduation and Placement

On reaching the age of twenty-five, every page was sent out from the school. Those from the Has Oda passed at once to places in the Muteferrika (متفره, Noble Guard) or to governorships of towns. The majority passed into the regular cavalry, the kapıkulu sipahileri (Spahis of the Porte). Those who left the school were honored by a ceremony of farewell: the sultan personally commended each one, gave him encouragement for good conduct, presented each with an embroidered coat and one of his most beautiful horses, and often a gift in money. The young man, with all the presents he had received during his stay, was escorted to the great gate, where he mounted his horse triumphantly and departed from the palace forever.

The Harem as Educational Space

The imperial harem partook of the characteristics of the schools of pages. There were two odalar for the recruits of the harem, in which they were taught housework, sewing and embroidery, manners and deportment. The women were organized in groups of ten, each group under a matron. Those with a taste for music and dancing learned those accomplishments; those who were studious learned to read and write. All were carefully instructed in the system of Islam. Like the pages, nearly all of them passed out of the palace at the age of twenty-five, being given in marriage to Spahis of the Porte or to other officials. Thus the harem might be considered a training-school of slave-wives for the sultan himself and for the most highly honored of his kullar.

Suleiman’s harem contained about three hundred women, kept in a separate palace well fortified and guarded. All its inmates except his children were purchased or presented slaves. These women, brought for the most part from the region of the Caucasus, and including in their number some of the fairest female captives of many lands, were nearly all daughters of Christians. Khurrem (Roxelana) herself was a Russian.

The Acemi Oğlan System

The acemi oğlanlar (“foreign youth” or apprentice Janissaries) — those not selected for the palace school — probably numbered about twenty thousand. Their training was largely physical, industrial, and military, with oral instruction in the Turkish language and the principles of the Mohammedan system.

The acemi oğlanlar usually passed through two or three stages. First, unless they already knew Turkish and Turkish ways, they were scattered through Asia Minor in the service of Moslem country gentlemen. There they were set at hard agricultural labor, to strengthen their bodies to the utmost. They were expected to learn to speak and understand the Turkish language and to learn the faith, the laws, and the customs of the Turks. The gentlemen whom they served supplied them with food and clothing.

At the end of two or three years, officers came to examine them. Those who knew enough Turkish and were strong and well-grown passed to the next stage: brought to Constantinople, they were once more carefully inscribed and estimated and then assigned to service — about two thousand to the fleet at Gallipoli, another two thousand (probably the most intelligent) as bostancı (بستانجی, gardeners) to the sultan’s palaces, and five or six thousand to work in the shipyards or on public buildings, or hired out in bands of one hundred or more to private citizens for hard labor.

In the midst of such a variety of occupations, two objects were always kept in mind: the acemi oğlanlar were to develop the utmost strength of body, and they were to learn some trade useful in war. They were filled with the spirit of youth and conscious of their superior physical strength and privileged position, and were not easy to manage — they frequently raised disturbances in the city. Those who wished were allowed to learn to read and write, but they were not obliged to do so.

After a certain time, the majority were assigned one by one to the service of the odalar (messes) of the Janissaries. The Janissaries then became responsible for their training in the art of war. In the course of time, as the acemi oğlanlar acquired sufficient skill and as vacancies occurred, they were enrolled as full-fledged Janissaries.

Advancement Based on Merit

The entire system from start to finish was designed to reward merit and fully to satisfy every ambition that was backed by ability, effort, and sufficient preparation. Two parallel lines of reward were established: the honorable and the financial. In the page school, the first was represented by promotion from class to class and, for the most suitable, by advancement through the chambers of personal service to the Has Oda. Among the acemi oğlanlar, individuals were carefully observed and tested, advanced from stage to stage, and entrusted in the later stages with greater and greater responsibilities.

The system continued without a break through the entire institution. The lowest Janissary might aspire to promotion through the hierarchy of office in his own corps, or by being lifted out of it for service in the cavalry or the active administration. The pages who had passed out of the school were already well up in the scale of advancement, and every place except the sultan’s own was within their grasp. The losses occasioned by fierce and frequent wars, and by not infrequent depositions and executions, gave abundant opportunity for men to rise from below.

“The Turks,” wrote Busbecq, “do not measure even their own people by any other rule than that of personal merit. The only exception is the house of Ottoman; in this case, and in this case only, does birth confer distinction. Among the Turks, therefore, honours, high posts, and judgeships are the rewards of great ability and good service. If a man be dishonest, or lazy, or careless, he remains at the bottom of the ladder, an object of contempt; for such qualities there are no honours in Turkey!”

The career of Ali Pasha illustrates the cursus honorum. A Dalmatian levied with the tribute boys, he was admitted to the principal palace when Ibrahim was Oda-bashi (head of the Inner Chamber). He became Kapuji (gatekeeper), then Chasnejir (chief taster) to Suleiman, then Agha of the Ghurebas (the lowest division of the regular cavalry), then Agha of the Spahi-oghlans (the highest cavalry division), then second equerry and later first equerry (Emir-al-Akhor), then Agha of the Janissaries, then Beylerbey of Rumelia, then pasha of Egypt, then third vizier, and finally grand vizier in 1561.

Punishment and Discipline

The system did not rely wholly upon the glittering attractions of indefinite promotion. The discipline of pages and acemi oğlanlar was severe; their teachers and eunuch masters were required to keep punishment within bounds by the certainty of yet severer punishment visited upon themselves. Acemi oğlanlar might be beaten or sold out of the sultan’s service.

After the close of the strictly educational period, punishment followed the law of proportionate increase. The higher the position, the heavier the punishment of being passed over in promotion or actually degraded. Fines and confiscations grew with rank. At no great height in the scale, personal punishments reached that of death, and death was always very near the highest officials. Any tendency toward treason or revolt, any act of disobedience, sometimes a plot against a higher official, sometimes even a disagreement with the sultan in a matter of policy, would lead to sudden execution. The viziers of Selim I carried their wills in their bosoms; the heads of seven are said to have fallen at his command.

Thus was the system carefully kept clear of all the human material that seemed to endanger its working or threaten its unity. There was no sympathy for weakness, no accepting of excuses, no suspension of sentence, no mercy. Suleiman did not always have the heart to execute promptly, but in the end he had no alternative, so remorseless was the system. Even his best friend, Ibrahim, went too far and had to be removed. Two of his sons, the oldest and ablest, threatened the system in turn and one after the other suffered the bowstring.

The Slave Status and Its Character

Whether captured, purchased, presented, or levied, the young men who entered the system were the slaves of the sultan — his personal property for life. They were never allowed to forget the responsibilities of their condition. They must to the end of their days go where the sultan chose to send them, obey his slightest wish, submit to disgrace as readily as to promotion, and though in the highest office of state, accept death by his order from the hands of their humblest fellow-slaves.

Yet Ottoman slavery was a very different institution from that which Anglo-Saxons have practised. In it there could ordinarily be no color-line and therefore no ineffaceable distinction. Slavery had no inherent quality; it was merely an accidental misfortune from which complete recovery was possible. The Moslem religion teaches kindness and benevolence to all but armed enemies of the faith. Moslem masters, in constant personal association with persons whose condition led them to strive to please, were apt to become very friendly toward them. Emancipation was common, and not only the sultan but many of his subjects did not hesitate to give their daughters in marriage to worthy slaves. A slave was often beloved above a son — a son possessed a character which was more or less a matter of chance, but a slave had been selected.

Lybyer’s Assessment

Lybyer considered the Ruling Institution, with the devşirme at its base and the Enderun as its educational engine, to be “perhaps no more daring experiment” ever tried on a large scale. He argued that human history has never known a political institution which during so long a period was so completely dominated by sheer intellect, and thereby so unerringly held to its original plan and purpose, as was the Ottoman Ruling Institution. The democracy of Athens attained an unexampled level of average intelligence, but under its sway the exceptional mind received discouragement rather than exceptional training. The free democracies of the present age allow the gifted individual opportunities to fight his way upward, but against obstacles which sometimes become insuperable. “As regards sheer efficiency,” Lybyer concluded, “unobstructed opportunity, and certainty of reward, their operation is wasteful, clumsy, and blind by comparison.”

The system produced the ablest statesmen and generals of the Ottoman golden age: grand viziers like Ibrahim Pasha, Rustem Pasha, Sokollu Mehmed Pasha, and Ali Pasha; admirals like Hayreddin Barbarossa; and the entire governing class that administered the largest empire of the sixteenth century. It was, as Mohammed the Conqueror wrote, the lamp of the empire kept burning “with oil from the hearts of the infidels.”
