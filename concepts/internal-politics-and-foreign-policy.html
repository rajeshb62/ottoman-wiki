Internal Politics and Foreign Policy

Overview

The reign of Bayezid II (1481–1512) is a case study in the inseparability of domestic politics and foreign policy in the early modern Ottoman Empire. Unlike his father Mehmed II, who ruled with an iron hand, Bayezid was a sultan who ascended the throne through the patronage of court factions and who remained, for the entirety of his reign, constrained by the forces that had elevated him. The central fact of his foreign policy — the fourteen-year captivity of his brother Cem (Jem) in Christian hands — was simultaneously a domestic threat, a diplomatic weapon, and a financial hemorrhage. Every major foreign policy decision Bayezid made between 1481 and 1495 was shaped by the fear that Cem might return, and after Cem’s death in 1495, by the consequences of having deferred expansion for so long.

The Janissaries as Kingmakers

Bayezid’s Accession

The Janissaries were the decisive force in Bayezid’s accession, and they never let him forget it. When Mehmed II died unexpectedly in May 1481, the grand vizier Karamani Mehmed Pasha and the chief judge Manisali Chelebi — both partisans of Cem — attempted to conceal the sultan’s death and summon Cem to Istanbul. Their plot failed because the janissaries, learning the truth, seized rude transports, crossed to Istanbul, beheaded the grand vizier, and paraded through the streets shouting “Long live Bayezid!”

The janissaries did not act out of pure loyalty. They were driven by a network of marriage alliances: the agha of the janissaries, the beylerbeys of Rumelia and Anatolia, and several European pashas were all related to Bayezid by marriage. These were graduates of the Palace School, members of the Ruling Institution, who saw in Bayezid a conservative alternative to Mehmed II’s centralizing policies. They installed Bayezid’s son Korkud as regent to hold the throne, and when Bayezid arrived, they extracted explicit concessions:

  • 1,000 akçes to each sipahi and 300 to each janissary
  • Increased daily pay of 5 akçes for sipahis and 3 for janissaries
  • A promise that viziers would be appointed only from the janissary officers, court sipahis, and palace pages
  • Immunity for the pillaging and violence the janissaries had already committed

Bayezid assented to all but the last, spurring his horse through the crowd without reply.

The Gedik Ahmed Crisis (1482)

The most dramatic demonstration of janissary power came in November 1482. Bayezid, nursing a personal grudge against Gedik Ahmed Pasha dating from the 1473 campaign against Uzun Hasan, had the great general imprisoned. Gedik Ahmed had risen from the ranks of the common janissaries — he was not a Palace School graduate — and was the most popular commander in the empire. The reaction was instantaneous:

  • The janissaries, sipahis, and silihdars (sword-bearers) marched on the palace
  • They forced the outer gate and had Bayezid at their mercy
  • They demanded Gedik Ahmed’s release, the dismissal of those who had counseled his arrest, and fulfilment of the donatives promised before the Battle of Yenişehir
  • They accused Bayezid of favoring timar-holders and peasants over the soldiers who had built the Ottoman state

Bayezid capitulated. Gedik Ahmed was not only freed but promoted to vizier with special authority. Mustafa Pasha (Bayezid’s maternal uncle) was imprisoned, and Rustem (the janissary agha and Bayezid’s son-in-law) fled. The episode demonstrated that Bayezid was not an absolute sovereign but was often at the mercy of the court and the army.

Yet Bayezid was patient. Later that same year, after Cem had fled to Rhodes and the immediate threat receded, Bayezid had Gedik Ahmed seized at a banquet in Edirne and executed. The charges against him were many: he had criticized Bayezid’s military leadership in 1473; he had protested abandoning the Otranto campaign; he was too popular with the troops; he opposed the treaty with Rhodes that amounted to paying tribute for Cem’s imprisonment; and he was outspoken, especially when drunk. Some janissaries rebelled at the execution, but they were crushed. Bayezid was slowly moving toward personal control, but the pattern was set: janissary power was a permanent constraint on Ottoman foreign policy.

Post-Cem Unrest

After Cem’s death in 1495, with the restraining hand of the Cem threat removed, the janissaries became increasingly restive. In the summer of 1496, a major outbreak occurred in Istanbul between janissaries and sipahis. Neither Bayezid nor his officials dared intervene for some time, even as officials were being robbed. The riot was eventually quelled and some agitators executed, but the message was clear: prolonged peace was intolerable to the professional soldiery. This pressure was a significant factor in the deterioration of relations with Venice that culminated in the war of 1499–1503.

The Palace School vs. the Ghazi Tradition

A fundamental tension ran through Bayezid’s court: the division between palace-trained pashas and commanders who had risen through the ranks of the janissaries and the ghazi tradition.

The Palace School (Enderun), established and expanded by Mehmed II, produced a cadre of administrators and commanders who were loyal to the sultan as an institution rather than to any particular faction. These men were typically of devşirme origin, educated in the palace, and imbued with an esprit de corps that transcended regional and ethnic loyalties. The ghazi tradition, by contrast, emphasized frontier warfare, personal valor, and independence from centralized control.

Mehmed II had relied heavily on the Palace School graduates and the ulema, but this alienated the ghazi elements. Bayezid, having spent twenty-four years as governor of Amasya — an old ghazi center — was seen as sympathetic to the ghazi tradition, yet he was placed on the throne by Palace School men.

This tension erupted during the Egyptian war. Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha — son of the last Duke of Herzegovina, educated in the Palace School, and Bayezid’s son-in-law — was appointed beylerbey of Anatolia and sent to campaign against the Mamluks. But when he took command, Karagöz Pasha (a Karamanian) and Khizrbeyoglu Mehmed Bey (Prince Ahmed’s preceptor) refused to cooperate, each considering himself superior to Hersekoglu. The reason was clear: to Anatolian-born officers like Karagöz, Hersekoglu was a Christian convert, a representative of the Ruling Institution that was centralizing authority in Istanbul at the expense of local power. When battle was joined near Adana, the two offended officers withdrew without fighting, and Hersekoglu was captured.

The same fault line appeared among Bayezid’s sons. Selim (later Selim I) was loved by the janissaries — he was a member of the Bektashi order, the traditional janissary religious order — while Ahmed was fat, pleasure-loving, and favored by the Palace School graduates and the high officials. When Bayezid tried to make Ahmed his successor, the janissaries refused: “Ahmed is fat and cannot lead us in war,” they said. They preferred Selim, who would maintain the empire and pay them liberally.

The Role of the Ulema and Their Partisanship for Cem

The ulema represented the settled, civilized, orthodox face of Ottoman society — the theologians, jurists, and educators who staffed the mosque-schools (medreses) and the judicial system. In the ghazi-ulema dichotomy that defined early Ottoman statecraft, the ulema were the counterweight to the frontier warriors. They were conservative, learned, and oriented toward the established Islamic centers of the older Seljuk and Abbasid traditions.

Bursa was their stronghold — dar al-‘ulema, “the city of theologians” — and Bursa was Cem’s power base. When Cem occupied Bursa in 1481, the ulema of the city supported him. The deputies sent to negotiate with Bayezid on Cem’s behalf — Ahmed Chelebi (son of Khudayi Shukr), and the mollas Ayas and Hamdi Chelebi — were all from the ulema. They proposed dividing the empire: Bayezid taking Europe, Cem taking Asia. Bursa would have been the capital of this Anatolian empire.

The grand vizier Karamani Mehmed Pasha, who plotted to put Cem on the throne, was himself a product of the ulema — born in Karamania, educated in a medrese, a descendant of Jalal al-Din al-Rumi. He was the leader of the “Moslem Institution,” the civil administration of theologians and lawyers who wished to re-establish an oriental monarchy. His co-conspirator, Manisali Chelebi, was the head of the ulema.

After Cem’s defeat, Bayezid punished the ulema who had supported him. At Bursa, when the janissaries wanted to sack the city and execute the dervishes who had aided Cem, Bayezid asked that Bursa be “given to him” — but each janissary had to be bribed first. Some dervishes were executed anyway.

During the succession crisis at the end of Bayezid’s reign, the poets and theologians supported Korkud, the scholarly son. Korkud was steeped in theology and philosophy, but the janissaries deemed him unfit to rule. The alignment was consistent: the ulema and literati preferred the contemplative, the soldiers preferred the martial.

The Succession Struggle and Foreign Exploitation

The Three Sons

By the last decade of Bayezid’s reign, three sons remained in contention: Ahmed, Korkud, and Selim. Each cultivated a different constituency:

  • Ahmed (governor of Amasya): Bayezid’s favorite, supported by the high officials, the grand vizier Ali Pasha, and Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha (Ahmed’s brother-in-law). He was amiable and regal but not generous — his parsimony cost him janissary support.
  • Korkud (governor of Attalya, later Manisa): A poet and scholar, supported by the ulema and theologians. He had briefly been regent in 1481 and claimed priority as a former sultan.
  • Selim (governor of Trebizond): Cunning, ruthless, energetic, and generous. Loved by the janissaries and a member of the Bektashi order.

Foreign Exploitation

The succession struggle drew in foreign powers:

Korkud’s flight to Egypt (1509): Feeling squeezed by his brothers and humiliated by Ali Pasha’s seizure of his lands, Korkud sailed from Attalya in May 1509 to seek aid from the Mamluk sultan of Egypt. He was received royally in Alexandria and Cairo, but Bayezid sent word to Egypt that any support for Korkud would be considered an attack on the Ottoman Empire. Egypt, already in a disastrous war with the Portuguese, backed down.

Ahmed’s alliance with Persia (1511–1512): After being passed over for the succession, Ahmed married one of his daughters to Shah Ismail of Persia, while one of his sons donned the red hat of the Safavid heretics. This was a direct appeal to the Kızılbaş (Shiite) elements in Anatolia. The result was that in the final struggle, Selim controlled Europe with Tartar support, while Ahmed controlled Asia with Persian aid. The Safavid heresy that Selim would later crush so brutally had been, in its origins, intertwined with Ottoman dynastic politics.

Selim’s Tartar alliance: Selim married the widow of his brother Mahmud, who was the daughter of a Tartar khan. This gave him a reserve of Tartar cavalry that proved decisive.

Venetian and European intelligence: Throughout the succession crisis, Venetian bailos and merchants in Istanbul sent detailed reports to Venice on the movements of the princes, the health of Bayezid, and the shifting balance of power. The Venetians understood that the succession question directly affected Ottoman foreign policy — a militant sultan like Selim would be far more dangerous than Bayezid.

The Financial Dimension

The Cost of Cem

The Cem affair was an immense financial burden. By the treaty of December 1482, Bayezid agreed to pay the Knights of St. John 45,000 Venetian ducats annually for Cem’s confinement, plus a like sum for the past year — 90,000 ducats in the first year alone. When Cem was transferred to the Pope in 1489, the payments continued. Between 1482 and 1495, Bayezid paid well over half a million ducats for his brother’s imprisonment.

These payments were deeply unpopular. Gedik Ahmed Pasha had protested that paying for Cem’s imprisonment was “prostituting the dignity of the Ottoman state.” The treaty with Rhodes was a bitter pill: Bayezid, the sultan of a rising empire, was paying tribute to a small crusader order.

Bayezid’s letter to Pope Alexander VI in 1494 suggested — in veiled terms — that Cem should be killed, offering 300,000 ducats for the deed. Cem died naturally (or was poisoned) in February 1495, and Bayezid then faced demands from France, Naples, and the Pope for the promised sum. The body was held for ransom: 5,000 ducats was demanded for its release. Bayezid eventually retrieved Cem’s remains in 1499 and interred them at Bursa.

Donatives and Pay Increases

The financial demands of the soldiery were constant. Bayezid’s accession cost donatives of 1,000 akçes per sipahi and 300 per janissary, plus daily pay increases. Before the Battle of Yenişehir, Bayezid renewed the promises his son Korkud had made. After Gedik Ahmed’s imprisonment crisis, he was forced to pay the bonuses again.

By 1496, Bayezid’s annual income was estimated at 2,400,000 ducats, with about 3,000,000 ducats in the treasury. His army included 8,000 janissaries, 8,000 sipahis, and roughly 115,000 other troops, plus a fleet of about 250 ships. The cost of maintaining this establishment was enormous, and the Egyptian war of 1485–1491 was described as “very costly in men” — losses estimated at 100,000.

Tribute and Trade

Bayezid used tribute payments as diplomatic tools. The Ragusans paid an annual tribute, reduced to 3,000 ducats on Bayezid’s accession. Venice was relieved of the 10,000 ducat annual tribute in the 1482 treaty — a concession Bayezid made to keep Venice neutral on the Cem question. The Knights of St. John and the Pope received the largest payments: 45,000 ducats annually for Cem.

Bayezid was also conscious of commercial interests. He reduced Venetian customs duties from five to four percent, granted favorable treaties to Florence, and carefully managed the Ragusan trade route. His position on the Bosphorus made him “undoubtedly the wealthiest monarch of his age.”

Marriage Alliances

Bayezid’s political sagacity in arranging marriages was one of the keys to his success. The faction that placed him on the throne was bound together by family ties:

  • Kasım, agha of the janissaries, was related to Bayezid by marriage
  • Rüstem, janissary pasha, was Bayezid’s son-in-law
  • Hersekli Ahmed Pasha, beylerbey of Rumelia, was Bayezid’s son-in-law
  • Sinan, beylerbey of Anatolia, was related by marriage
  • Ishak Pasha and Daud Pasha, the viziers in Istanbul, were Palace School graduates

After Bayezid’s accession, he continued this pattern. Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha became one of the most important figures of the reign, serving as grand vizier, admiral of the fleet, and twice being captured by the Mamluks. His relationship with Bayezid — he was both son-in-law and brother-in-law — made him uniquely trusted.

In 1489, Bayezid gave three of his daughters in marriage in a single grand celebration in Istanbul, each to a great lord. One was a Persian, Ahmed Mirza, a grandson of Uzun Hasan — a marriage that tied Ottoman interests to Persian dynastic politics and would later be used as a pretext for intervention in Persian affairs.

Alaeddevlet of Sulqadr, the border prince who repeatedly switched sides between the Ottomans and Mamluks, gave his daughter to the son of the Mamluk general Uzbey, cementing his defection from the Ottoman cause.

In the succession struggle, marriage became a weapon: Selim married the Tartar khan’s daughter (his brother Mahmud’s widow), giving him a reservoir of steppe cavalry; Ahmed married his daughter to Shah Ismail of Persia.

Fratricide and Succession Law

The Ottoman practice of fratricide — the execution of all male rivals by the successful heir — had been in place for at least a century before Bayezid. It set the stakes of inheritance high and virtually guaranteed civil war at every succession.

Bayezid’s response to Cem’s envoys who proposed a division of the empire — “Kings have no relatives” — illustrated the logic of the system. Yet Bayezid did not execute Cem when he had the chance, perhaps because he feared the reaction of the ulema, perhaps because he was genuinely reluctant to kill his brother. Instead, he offered Cem a pension of one million akçes and retirement in Jerusalem. Cem refused.

The system had direct foreign policy consequences. European powers understood that a defeated prince was a valuable asset. The Knights of St. John, the Pope, the King of France, Hungary, Naples, and Venice all tried to use Cem as a lever against the Ottomans. The mere existence of a rival claimant constrained Ottoman foreign policy for fourteen years.

At the end of Bayezid’s reign, the system produced another civil war. Bayezid wanted Ahmed to succeed, but the janissaries wanted Selim. Bayezid was forced to abdicate on April 24, 1512. Selim was girded with the sword of Osman. Thirteen days later, on May 26, Bayezid died en route to Demotika, almost certainly poisoned on Selim’s orders. The reason was political: Selim could not cross to Asia against Ahmed with Bayezid still living in Europe, and he needed the treasury to pay the janissaries.

The viziers were not merely administrators but active participants in the dynastic struggle. Ali Pasha, the grand vizier from 1503, was a staunch partisan of Prince Ahmed. He opposed Selim at every turn and was largely responsible for the policy of trying to install Ahmed. When Bayezid wanted to abdicate in favor of Ahmed in 1511, the viziers counseled delay because they feared Selim’s revenge — an accurate fear.

Hersekoglu Ahmed Pasha, though Ahmed’s brother-in-law, was more cautious. During the janissary riot of September 1511, when a mob came to his palace, he coolly appeared at his door, asked why they had come, and told them to present their grievances to Bayezid. This action may have saved the capital from a general uprising.

The viziers were also deeply involved in foreign policy, sometimes acting independently of Bayezid. During the prelude to the Venetian war, the viziers (especially Ibrahim Pasha and Hersekoglu) conducted the negotiations with Andrea Zanchani, offered opinions on French and Imperial power, and ultimately convinced Bayezid to proceed with the attack on Corfu when he wanted to recall the fleet.

Intelligence and Espionage

Bayezid maintained an extensive intelligence network in Europe, focused primarily on tracking Cem and monitoring the plans of Christian princes.

Ottoman Agents

The most notable Ottoman agent was Assam Bey, who carried Cem’s pension to Pope Alexander VI in 1494, along with Bayezid’s letter suggesting Cem’s elimination. He was robbed at Sinigaglia by Giuliano della Rovere, who stole both the money and the correspondence.

Bayezid sent kapujibaşı Mustafa to Rome in 1490 with 120,000 ducats (three years’ pension) and holy relics — the lance that pierced Christ’s side and the sponge that quenched his thirst — to negotiate directly with the Pope.

Ottoman ambassadors were regularly deployed to European courts. Iskender Pasha, Bayezid’s former preceptor, was sent to Venice in 1482 to confirm the treaty and to negotiate about Cem — offering the Morea as a reward if Cem fell into Venetian hands. The same Iskender Pasha, as sanjakbey of Bosnia, led raids into Dalmatia and Hungary throughout the 1480s and 1490s.

The Venetian Network

The Venetian bailo (resident ambassador) in Istanbul was effectively an intelligence officer. Bayezid refused to accept a Venetian bailo for most of his reign, considering the office “an official spy.” When Andrea Gritti — the wealthiest Venetian merchant in Istanbul — served as an unofficial representative, his reports to Venice were detailed and accurate. He warned Venice in January 1498 that the viziers were not inclined to peace, that a great fleet was being prepared, and that Bayezid had the means to wage war.

Venetian consuls and merchants in Chios, Corfu, Modon, Alexandria, and other ports sent a constant stream of reports that were summarized in the diary of Marino Sanuto. These dispatches tracked:

  • Shipbuilding at Gallipoli, Ismid, Salonica, Negroponte, and Valona
  • Grain shortages and trade embargoes
  • Troop movements and army sizes
  • The health and mood of the sultan
  • The ambitions of the princes

Intercepted Communications

Bayezid intercepted letters between Cem and his supporters. In 1485, through Venice, he learned that Cem in France was corresponding with his mother in Egypt. Bayezid sent for Cem’s mother and family, but the Mamluk sultan refused to surrender them.

The Venetian bailo Hironimo Marcello was caught sending secret letters describing Ottoman naval preparations and was expelled; Bayezid notified Venice not to replace him.

Counter-Intelligence

During the succession crisis of 1511, Bayezid closely guarded the Persian ambassador to prevent him from learning of the dissension among the princes. He feared that if Shah Ismail knew how divided the Ottomans were, he would actively aid the Shia heretics in Anatolia.

Conclusion

The intersection of internal politics and foreign policy during Bayezid II’s reign reveals a sultan who was never truly master in his own house. He was placed on the throne by the janissaries and a marriage alliance of Palace School graduates; he was forced to cede to their demands at swordpoint; he paid humiliating tribute to Christian powers for fourteen years to neutralize his brother; he fought an indecisive and costly war with Egypt while fending off crusade threats; and he was finally deposed by his own son at the head of the janissaries he could no longer control or satisfy.

The pattern that Fisher describes — “the soldiers and the officials at the Porte, who had begun to show their spirit and their influence in the reign of Mehmed II, became in reality all-powerful” — was a direct consequence of the Cem crisis. Bayezid’s conservatism and caution kept the empire intact, but at the price of empowering the very factions that would dominate and weaken it in the following century. As a contemporary Venetian observer wrote: “They are the ones who dominate and lord it over the country.”
