Ottoman Culture and Learning

The Medrese (مدرسه) System

The first Ottoman medrese was created in Iznik in 1331, when a converted church building was assigned as a medrese to the famous scholar Davud of Kayseri. In later years, when an Ottoman sultan wished to establish a new medrese, he would invite scholars from the old Anatolian cultural centres — Konya, Kayseri, Aksaray — or from Persia, Turkestan, Egypt or Syria. During the formative period of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, Ottoman ulema travelled to Egypt, Persia or Turkestan to complete their education under the great scholars of those lands. For the study of Koranic exegesis and jurisprudence they went primarily to Egypt and Persia; for mathematics they went usually to Samarkand.

A student, according to Taskopruzade, must study all the sciences, since they complement each other in forming a single whole. The essential element in an Islamic education was the muderris (مدرس — professor), a man of recognized authority in the religious and spiritual sciences. It was the muderris himself who was important and not the institution.

The Hierarchy of Medreses

Ottoman medreses fell into two major categories:

The Haric (خارج — Exterior) Medreses

These gave preparatory instruction in the “Fundamentals of Knowledge” — Arabic and the intellectual sciences:

(a) Ibtida-yi Haric (Lower Medreses): Taught the rudiments of Arabic grammar and syntax, logic, scholastic theology, astronomy, geometry and rhetoric. Known as “Tajrid medreses” after Sayyid Sharif’s commentary on Nasir al-Din of Tus’s al-Tajrid, also as “medreses of twenty” since the muderris received twenty akces daily.

(b) “Medreses of Thirty” or “Miftah medreses”: Named after their chief textbook, al-Sakkaki’s Miftah al-ulum on rhetoric. Gave instruction in rhetoric and the literary sciences. Most medreses of twenty and thirty were in the provinces.

(c) “Medreses of Forty” and “Medreses of Fifty”: Founded by princes, royal ladies or viziers in Istanbul, Edirne and Bursa. Gave elementary instruction in the interpretation of the Miftah, intermediate scholastic theology based on Adud al-Din’s al-Mawaqif, and higher jurisprudence based on al-Marghinani’s al-Hidaya.

The Dahil (داخل — Interior) Medreses

These gave instruction in “Higher Knowledge” — the religious sciences:

(a) Ibtida-yi Dahil (“Medreses of fifty”): Founded by sultans’ daughters, princes or viziers. Taught jurisprudence (al-Hidaya), the principles of jurisprudence (al-Taftazani’s al-Talwih), and Koranic exegesis (al-Zamakhshari’s al-Kashshaf).

(b) Tetimme or Musile-yi Sahn: Mehmed the Conqueror’s eight preparatory medreses.

(c) Sahn-i Seman or Semaniye: The highest level — Mehmed the Conqueror’s eight medreses where students studied jurisprudence, Koranic exegesis or scholastic theology, rhetoric, and related subjects, receiving specialized training.

The Sahn-i Seman (صهن ثمن — Eight Courts)

Between 1463 and 1470, Mehmed the Conqueror built the Conqueror’s Mosque in Istanbul and around it established the eight famous medreses known as the Sahn-i Seman or the Semaniye. These stone medreses, fine examples of Ottoman architecture, were built on either side of the mosque. There were eight higher medreses for specialized studies and eight lower medreses which prepared students for these. The daily pay of the muderris in charge of each was fifty akces, equal to about one gold ducat. Each medrese had nineteen rooms and one classroom. Fifteen rooms were allocated to specialist students known as danismends, who each received two akces daily from the endowment income and food from the hospice.

At this period, the Semaniye medreses were the highest ranking educational institutes in the empire. Below them came the Darulhadis medrese which Murad II had founded in Edirne, and below this the medreses which earlier sultans had established in Bursa.

The Suleymaniye Medreses

Suleyman I made an important change in the hierarchy. Around the Suleymaniye mosque in Istanbul (built 1550-56), he established four general medreses and two more for specialized studies — one devoted to the science of hadith (traditions of the Prophet) and the other to medicine. To these he gave the highest rank, thus establishing the hierarchy of medreses which was to continue until the end of the empire. All the hundreds of medreses throughout the empire were categorized according to these eleven grades.

By the seventeenth century there were as many as ninety-five medreses in Istanbul alone; by the nineteenth century their number had risen to 170. The medrese was always supported by a vakif (وقف — pious foundation) and was usually one component in a complex of mosque, hospice and other charitable institutions.

The Ulema (علماء) — The Learned Class

The ulema were recruited from the medreses. They had the dual role of interpreters and executors of Islamic law — the muftis performing the first of these duties and the kadis the second. There was a rigid hierarchy of ulema — muderrises, muftis and kadis — and a strict system of promotions.

The muderrises of haric medreses or graduates of the Semaniye could become kadis of small towns. The senior kadiships, at three hundred akces or more, reached the most elevated posts and were known as mollas. A muderris at the Semaniye could become a molla earning five hundred akces, with the possibility of promotion to kadi of Istanbul and later kadiasker. The highest ranks in the bureaucracy, not only the religious and legal professions, were open to them; many became viziers and attained positions of political power.

The seyhulislam (شيخ الإسلام) was the head of the ulema, appointed by royal warrant. The Kanunname of Mehmed the Conqueror placed his rank on a level with that of the grand vizier, and protocol required that he should receive the greater respect. His duty was to issue fetvas — written answers based on standard religious authorities to any problem falling within the scope of the seriat.

Intellectual Figures

Taskopruzade Ahmed (1495-1561)

Taskopruzade Ahmed (تاشكوپرى‌زاده) was the Ottoman encyclopaedist and medrese teacher best known for his Sakaik al-numaniya, a biography in Arabic of 522 Ottoman sheikhs and ulema, and Mawduat al-ulum, an encyclopaedia of the sciences. His concept of knowledge and division of the sciences provides a starting point for any study of Ottoman learning. He recognized four stages of knowledge — spiritual, intellectual, oral and written — and divided the spiritual sciences into theoretical rational sciences (theology, natural sciences, mathematics), practical rational sciences (ethics, political science), theoretical religious sciences (Koran, traditions, exegesis, theology, jurisprudence) and practical religious sciences.

In his religious views Taskopruzade inclined to mysticism. Following al-Ghazali, he maintained that contemplation is a necessary complement to the spiritual sciences, and that the scholar who studies only the exoteric religious sciences is “a poor man, excluded from the greater realities.”

Kinalizade Ali (c. 1510-71)

Kinalizade was a prominent Ottoman scholar and moral philosopher, best known for his work Ahlak-i Alai (علاقى اخلاق — The Alai Ethics), a treatise on practical philosophy and ethics based on the Islamic and Greek philosophical traditions. He served as a kadi in several major cities and was an important figure in the intellectual life of the sixteenth century.

Kâtip Çelebi (1609-57) — Hacı Halife

Kâtip Çelebi was the greatest Ottoman encyclopaedic scholar and a secretary in the imperial council. His bibliographic work Kashf al-Zunun (كشف الظنون — The Removal of Doubt) remains a standard reference book for Islamic manuscripts to this day. He wrote on history, geography, philosophy and the principles of the calendar, and represents the bureaucratic tradition of secular learning as distinct from the medrese-centred scholarship of the ulema.

The most important Ottoman writings in practical and useful fields — literature, language, calligraphy, law, history, philosophy, geography, surveying and agriculture — were the work of professional secretaries like Kâtip Çelebi, who together with the slaves educated in the royal Palace played a vital role in the creation of Ottoman culture.

Other Notable Scholars

Mehmed al-Fanari (1350-1431): Traditionally regarded as the first seyhulislam of the empire. He founded the Ottoman medrese tradition and specialized in the intellectual sciences; his work on logic was an essential part of medrese courses until the last days of the empire. As a follower of the Konya school of Ibn al-Arabi, he was censured by the Arab ulema in Egypt.

Molla Husrev (d. 1480): Ottoman scholar and jurist who became muderris, kadiasker and seyhulislam under Murad II and Mehmed II. His works on jurisprudence became standard textbooks in Ottoman medreses.

Ibn Kemal (Kemal Pasazade) (1468-1534): A typical broad-ranging Muslim scholar who composed over a hundred religious treatises and left a monumental ten-volume history of the Ottoman Empire. Many of his works were concerned with shiism, at that time a question of vital importance; he used religious arguments to prove the legality of holy war against the Shia.

Ebussuud Efendi (1490-1574): Seyhulislam under Suleiman the Magnificent, famous for his fetvas harmonizing the seriat with the kanun and for condemning the Melami heretic Hamza Bali.

Ali Kuscu (d. 1474): Mathematician and astronomer who worked at the Samarkand observatory under Ulugh Beg. Mehmed II invited him to Istanbul, inaugurating a brilliant era in Ottoman mathematics. He wrote classic works on arithmetic and astronomy.

Molla Lutfi (d. 1494): Muderris under Bayezid II, distinguished as a mathematician and theologian. His free thought and open mockery of superstition angered conservative ulema, who accused him of heresy; he was beheaded on the Atmeydani in Istanbul in 1494. Some ulema regarded the incident as slander, and public opinion came to regard him as a martyr to truth.

Ottoman Historiography

Ottoman historiography developed in two parallel streams. Side by side with folk histories written in simple Turkish, Palace poets and writers in the service of Mehmed the Conqueror composed works in high literary Persian. It did not take long for Palace histories to appear, written in Turkish but imitating the ornate Persian style, and in time a peculiarly Ottoman school of historiography came into being.

Early Chroniclers

Asikpasazade (1400-84): One of the earliest Ottoman historians, author of the Tevarih-i Al-i Osman (تاريخ آل عثمان — The History of the House of Osman). His work is written in simple Turkish and reflects the gazi and dervish traditions of the early frontier period, preserving many popular legends and epic motifs.

Nesri (d. c. 1520): Another early Ottoman historian whose Cihannuma (جهان‌نما — The World-Mirror) is a comprehensive history of the Ottoman state up to the reign of Bayezid II. Nesri is quoted by Inalcik describing how Mehmed the Conqueror “created Istanbul.”

Later Chroniclers

Selaniki Mustafa Efendi (d. c. 1600): A prominent late-sixteenth-century historian whose Tarih-i Selaniki covers the reigns of Selim II and Murad III. His chronicle is a crucial source for the administrative, social and economic history of the Ottoman Empire in the period of transformation.

Na’ima (Naima) (1655-1716): The first official Ottoman court historiographer (vak’anuvis). His Tarih-i Na’ima covers the years 1591-1659 and is one of the most important works of Ottoman historiography. He was influenced by Ibn Khaldun’s philosophy of history, which he introduced to Ottoman intellectual circles.

Ibn Kemal: Also wrote a monumental ten-volume history of the Ottoman Empire.

Ottoman Poetry — Divan Edebiyatı (ديوان ادبياتى)

Classical Ottoman literature developed as a Palace art. Most poets and writers had some connection with the Palace; Bayezid II’s account books show that about twenty poets regularly benefited from the sultan’s largesse. Classical Ottoman literature drew its main inspiration from Persian, and Persian language and literature were studied in Mevlevi tekkes since the ulema forbade their study in the medreses.

Major Poets

Baki (1526-1600): Known as “Sultan of the Poets” (سلطان الشعارا — Sultan al-Su’ara), Baki was the greatest lyric poet of the classical age. A native of Istanbul, he was a close friend of Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and enjoyed the patronage of Suleiman the Magnificent and Selim II. His Divan is the supreme achievement of Ottoman lyric poetry.

Fuzuli (c. 1494-1556): The most famous mystical poet of the Ottoman tradition, writing in Azerbaijani Turkish, Persian and Arabic. A native of Karbala (in Ottoman Iraq), his works include the Divan, the Leyla ve Mecnun (ليلى و مجنون) mesnevi, and the Hadikat al-Suada (حديقت السعدا — The Garden of the Blessed). His poetry is deeply infused with the sufi mystical tradition.

Nedim (1681-1730): The greatest poet of the Tulip Age (1718-30), Nedim’s poetry reflects the pleasure-seeking, worldly atmosphere of the court of Ahmed III. His style broke from the high Persianate tradition toward a simpler, more Turkish idiom.

The Mevlevîs were in the first rank of Ottoman musicians and poets from the sixteenth century onward, creating their own music and literature entirely derived from Mevlevî tradition.

Libraries and the Culture of the Book

The library had an important place in Ottoman society. The Ottomans founded libraries in mosques, hospitals and tekkes and collected private libraries in their own residences. Many private book collections found their way to vakif libraries, since it was considered meritorious to leave books to a pious foundation. The library was a single unit in a vakif complex, established in a stone room or separate building. The vakfiye stipulated how the books were to be preserved and used and appointed a librarian paid from vakif funds.

These libraries still preserve more than two hundred thousand manuscripts from all lands and epochs of the Islamic world, forming the richest source for the history and culture of Islam.

Ottoman writers used Arabic in their religious and legal works, but by the fourteenth century they had already begun to make translations into Turkish — at first useful or instructive works on history, politics, morals, etiquette, astrology, natural history and gemmology, made for the sultans or influential men of state. Two popular books, dating from 1449, are still among the most widely read Turkish works: the poem Muhammediye (The Book of Muhammed) and the prose work Envar al-Asikin (The Lights of the Lovers) of the Yazicizade brothers, who belonged to the Bayrami sect.

The Patronage System

Ottoman culture remained essentially a Palace culture. From the fifteenth century, Palace architects erected buildings in a distinctive Ottoman style throughout the empire, from Konya to Sarajevo. There grew up in the Palace an Ottoman artistic style and aristocratic way of life which the sultan’s slaves diffused to all corners of the empire. Classical Ottoman literature and architecture developed as Palace arts.

The patronage of the sultans was crucial. Mehmed II lamented that although he had established a great empire, his realms contained no ulema comparable with those of other lands; among native ulema he could take pride only in Molla Husrev and Hocazade. He actively sought to remedy this by converting eight churches into medreses after the conquest and by establishing the Sahn-i Seman. Suleyman I similarly raised the highest-ranking medreses around his mosque complex.

The reforming grand viziers Koca Ragib, Halil Hamid and Mustafa Resid were all professional bureaucrats from the scribal class. The bureaucrats represented the political and secular interests of state as against the ulema who represented the seriat, and were generally responsible for introducing traditional Near Eastern principles of administration to the empire.

The medrese was a self-governing unit within a vakif, itself an autonomous institution. The mutevelli (trustee) of the vakif complex entrusted to the muderris the funds allotted to the medrese; the muderris selected students, disbursed funds to students and servants, and administered the medrese.

The Intellectual Sciences

Following al-Ghazali, the Ottoman medreses maintained that the intellectual sciences (logic, mathematics, astronomy) contained essential elements for all the sciences — they accustomed the mind to correct thinking and thus helped to reveal divine truths. The result was the early adoption of the intellectual sciences into the curricula of Ottoman medreses.

In the fifteenth century, under the patronage of Mehmed II who attached great importance to these sciences, the Ottomans achieved real distinction in mathematics and astronomy within the Islamic world. The mathematical genius was Musa Pasha, called Kadizade, whose commentaries on Euclid formed part of medrese courses until the latest period. His student Ali Kuscu inaugurated a brilliant era in Ottoman mathematics.

Mehmed II also reopened the famous controversy between al-Ghazali and Ibn Rushd (Averroes) over the relationship between religion and philosophy, inviting the two great theologians Ala al-Din of Tus and Hocazade of Bursa to write treatises on the subject. Hocazade’s work, defending the seriat against the claims of philosophy, was judged the superior and maintained its reputation in the Islamic world until the present day. While Averroism was studied in Italy and became a major factor in Renaissance thought, a thoroughgoing scholasticism established itself in Ottoman medreses.
