The Silk Route under the Ottoman Empire

The Silk Route (İpek Yolu / ايپك يولو) was the economic backbone of the early Ottoman Empire. Under Ottoman dominion in the fourteenth through sixteenth centuries, the overland caravan routes linking Iran to Anatolia and the Mediterranean shifted decisively westward, making Bursa the foremost international emporium for raw Persian silk in the Mediterranean world. The Ottoman state’s control over these routes, its regulation of the silk trade through customs and the bedestan system, and the role of Venetian, Genoese, and Florentine merchants in Bursa together created a commercial system that sustained Ottoman prosperity until the rise of European maritime routes and Ottoman-Persian conflicts disrupted it.

The Shift of Trade Routes to Western Anatolia

With the establishment of the Mongol Empire in the thirteenth century, Anatolia became the highway for east-west trade. Italian merchants from the maritime republics met caravans from the Far East and Iran at Ayas on the Cilician coast, Trebizond on the Black Sea, and inland at Sivas and Konya. An imperial highway linked Tabriz (تبريز) to Konya through Erzurum (ارض روم), Erzincan (ارزنجان), and Sivas (سيواس). Twenty-three caravanserais from the Seljuk period still stand on the Sivas-Konya road, attesting to the density of this traffic.

The fall of the Ilkhanid Empire in Iran in the fourteenth century and the simultaneous rise of the Ottomans in western Anatolia caused the commercial centre of gravity to move westward. The old emporia of Palatia, Altoluogo (Ephesus), and Smyrna (Izmir / ازمير) came under Ottoman control and were linked to Bursa. By extending his dominions eastward to Erzincan through Amasya (آماسيه) and Tokat (توقات), Bayezid I (يلدريم بايزيد) gained control of the Tabriz-Bursa caravan route. Persian silk caravans now abandoned the Trebizond route and travelled overland to Bursa instead. In the fifteenth century, Amasya and Tokat — situated on this route — became after Bursa the most important economic and cultural cities in Anatolia.

Bursa as the Primary Silk Centre and Entrepot

Bursa (بروسه) was the heart of the Ottoman silk trade. Already in 1333, Ibn Battuta described it as “a great city with fine bazaars and broad streets,” and Orhan Ghazi was noted as the richest of the Turcoman sultans in Anatolia. In 1340 Orhan constructed a bedestan (بيدستان) — an enclosed, fortified market for the sale of valuable goods — which laid the institutional foundation for Bursa’s commercial rise.

By the end of the fourteenth century, the German traveller Schiltberger compared Bursa’s silk trade and industry with that of Damascus (دمشق) and Caffa (كفه), recording that Persian silk was sent from Bursa to Venice (ونيزيك) and Lucca — then the centre of the European silk industry. Trade in Persian silk was the mainstay of Bursa’s development and prosperity. In the fifteenth century, as the European silk industry expanded greatly, Bursa became the international marketplace for the basic raw material of the industry: the extremely fine silk of Asterabad (استرآباد) and Gilan (گيلان) in northern Iran.

The scale of the trade was substantial. In Bursa, about a thousand silk looms worked through five fardelli (each approximately 150 kg) of raw silk per day. An average caravan brought about two hundred fardelli. The price of a fardello rose from fifty akces (آقچه) in 1467 to seventy in 1488 and eighty-two in 1494. In Italy, each fardello yielded a profit of seventy to eighty gold ducats. The customs receipts from silk in Bursa reflect both the volume and the volatility of this trade:

The Tabriz-Bursa Route

The primary artery of the silk trade ran from Tabriz, the great commercial capital of northwestern Iran, across Anatolia to Bursa. The route passed through Erzurum, Erzincan, Sivas, and Tokat before descending to Amasya and then westward to Bursa. Caravans also followed a southerly branch from Tabriz through Van (وان), Bitlis (بتليس), Diyarbekir (ديار بكر), and Birecik (بيرجك), reaching Aleppo (حلب) rather than Bursa.

Security along these routes was maintained by the Ottoman state. Caravanserais (كاروانسرا) built by sultans and high-ranking statesmen dotted the roads at intervals of a day’s march, providing shelter, stabling, and provisions. The state also policed the routes. When in 1501 a load of Florentine silk was stolen near Foca (فوشه), the sultan immediately sent officers who recovered part of the load and compelled the local population to pay for the remainder — demonstrating the importance the Ottomans placed on maintaining the security and reliability of the silk route.

Venetian and Genoese Merchants in Bursa

For the Italian maritime republics, Bursa was the closest and richest market in which to purchase eastern goods and sell European woollens. Venetian and Genoese merchants from Constantinople and Galata (غلطه) regularly travelled to Bursa to trade. The Genoese had concluded a commercial agreement with the Ottomans as early as 1352 and enjoyed a long-term monopoly over alum production in the region — alum being essential for fixing dyes in the textile industry.

Florentine merchants became increasingly active in the Bursa market after Sultan Mehmed II granted new trade privileges to Florence in 1469, during the Ottoman-Venetian war of 1463-79. At that time some fifty Florentine houses were trading in the empire, and Bursa became an emporium for the sale of Florentine cloth to Anatolia and Iran, with Florentine traders buying Persian silk in return. The representative in Bursa of the Medici and other Florentine houses, Giovanni Maringhi, left detailed letters recording that in 1501 several silk caravans arrived in Bursa from Iran each year. His correspondence reflects the impatience with which the Italian merchants awaited the caravans, the rush to buy the goods, and the fierce competition among them.

In the broader Mediterranean context, Levantine trade remained dominated by Venice, whose merchants operated under capitulations (امتيازات / imtiyazat) that fixed customs duty at 2 per cent. The Venetian bailo resided in Istanbul, and Venetian galleys regularly carried Bursa silks, spices, and cottons to the markets of Europe.

The Ottoman Regulation of the Silk Trade

The Ottoman state regulated the silk trade through a carefully structured system. The bedestan in Bursa was the central clearing-house for all transactions in valuable goods. Here, raw silk imported from Iran was sold under the supervision of state-appointed brokers, doorkeepers, and night-watchmen. The government controlled weights, measures, and pricing, and customs duties (gumruk / گمرك) were collected on all silk entering the city.

At the level of production, the silk industry of Bursa operated through a hierarchically organised guild system. The silk merchants who dealt in raw silk formed a guild that acted as entrepreneur. They bought imported raw silk in the bedestan, gave it to the twisters’ guild to be worked into thread, passed it to the dyers’ guild to be coloured, and finally sold the prepared skeins to the weavers. These dependent guilds were known as yamaks (ياماق). The state typically supported the dominant merchant guild in disputes, since disruptions caused unemployment and a drop in tax revenues.

Customs receipts from silk were a significant source of state revenue. At their height in 1512, silk customs in Bursa yielded 43,000 gold ducats annually. The state also controlled the alum trade (essential for textile dyeing), granted as a monopoly to the Genoese and later to Venice for 25,000 gold ducats in 1546.

The Impact of Ottoman-Persian Conflicts on Silk Routes

The ongoing conflict between the Ottoman Empire and Safavid Iran had a direct and disruptive effect on the silk trade. The Safavids, who established Shiism as the state religion of Iran in 1501, presented both a political and ideological challenge to the Sunni Ottomans. The wars that followed — particularly the campaigns of Selim I (1514, Battle of Chaldiran), Suleyman I (1534-35, capturing Tabriz and Baghdad), and the exhausting war of 1578-90 — repeatedly interrupted the flow of Persian silk to Bursa.

The customs receipts table above shows the impact. Receipts fell from 43,000 gold ducats in 1512 to 13,000 in 1521, a drop of 70 per cent. This collapse was directly attributable to the disruption of the Tabriz-Bursa route by the Ottoman-Safavid wars. Although a partial recovery followed the Peace of Amasya (1555), which established a temporary boundary between the two empires, receipts reached only 24,000 gold ducats by 1557 — barely more than half the 1512 level.

The Ottomans attempted to address the problem by bringing the silk-producing regions themselves under their control. In the sixteenth century they tried to bring the north Persian centres of silk production at Shirvan (شيروان) and Gilan directly under Ottoman rule. Suleyman’s campaign of 1534-35 forced the local dynasts in Gilan and Shirvan to recognise Ottoman suzerainty. Later, during the war of 1578-90, the Ottomans annexed all the western provinces of Iran from the Caucasus to Nehavend, including the silk lands. However, these gains proved temporary, and the Safavids eventually reasserted control.

The Silk Trade through Aleppo and the Levant

After the Ottoman conquest of Syria and Egypt in 1516-17, Aleppo emerged as a rival centre of the silk trade. Silk caravans from Iran reached Aleppo through Erzurum and along the Euphrates valley, or more often from Tabriz through Van, Bitlis, Diyarbekir, and Birecik. With the conquest of the Mamluk lands, the Ottomans gained control of all outlets for Persian silk open to Europeans — every route from Iran to the Mediterranean was now in Ottoman hands.

Aleppo functioned as both a terminus for overland silk caravans and a distribution centre for the Levantine ports. European merchants, particularly the Venetians and later the French and English, established consulates and fondacos (merchant compounds) in Aleppo. The city’s bedestan and covered markets handled not only silk but also spices, dyestuffs, drugs, and Indian textiles. The Aleppo route thus complemented the Bursa route, providing a southern alternative that remained active even when the northern route through Anatolia was disrupted by war.

Damascus also played a role. The caravan route from Damascus to Bursa was described by Bertrandon de la Brocquiere in 1432. He joined a group of pilgrims and merchants returning from Mecca with a caravan of three thousand camels near Damascus, and after a journey of some fifty days reached Bursa, where he found Florentine merchants and Genoese from Pera who had come to buy spices.

The Bedestan and Caravanserai System

The institutional infrastructure of the silk route rested on two Ottoman institutions: the bedestan (fortified market) and the caravanserai. The bedestan served as a secure repository for valuable goods, with stone domes and iron doors that resisted both fire and pillage. It functioned as a central exchange where merchants could buy and sell silk under government supervision, and where state-appointed brokers facilitated transactions.

Caravanserais provided the physical security that made long-distance overland trade feasible. Along the major routes, these inns were spaced a day’s march apart — typically 30 to 40 kilometres. They offered accommodation for merchants, stabling for animals, storage for goods, and often a small mosque, bath, and market. Many were built as vakifs (وقف / charitable endowments), ensuring their perpetual maintenance. The most famous surviving examples from the Ottoman period include the Buyuk Han in Bursa and the Kurlu Han on the Istanbul-Bursa road.

The Decline of the Overland Silk Route

Several converging factors brought about the decline of the overland silk route in the late sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.

First, the Ottoman-Persian wars of 1578-90, while bringing temporary Ottoman gains, devastated the silk-producing regions of Iran and disrupted the caravans for decades. The cost of continuous warfare strained Ottoman finances and diverted state attention away from commercial infrastructure.

Second, the Portuguese discovery of the sea route to India around the Cape of Good Hope (1498) and their subsequent domination of the Indian Ocean began to redirect the spice trade away from the Levant. Although the overland spice trade proved surprisingly resilient — Venetian purchases in Alexandria remained at pre-Vasco da Gama levels into the 1560s — the long-term trend was toward maritime routes.

Third, European maritime powers — first Portugal, then England and Holland — developed direct sea routes to the sources of silk and spices. The English Levant Company, chartered in 1581, and the Dutch, who received capitulations in 1612, increasingly brought Asian goods to Europe by sea rather than through the overland Ottoman routes. The English attempt to establish a route through Moscow, Iran, and Hormuz, though initially blocked by the Ottomans, pointed toward a future in which the overland silk route would be bypassed entirely.

Fourth, the emergence of the Atlantic economy shifted the centre of European commercial gravity away from the Mediterranean. The rise of Antwerp, Amsterdam, and London as entrepots for Asian goods eroded the competitive position of Bursa, Aleppo, and the other Ottoman silk markets.

Fifth, the influx of cheap European silver from the Americas after the 1580s — the Price Revolution — disrupted Ottoman monetary stability and undermined the traditional foundations of the silk trade. Silver coins flooded the Levantine market, causing inflation that eroded the profits of both merchants and the state.

By the seventeenth century, Bursa had lost its status as the premier silk emporium of the Mediterranean, though its silk industry survived in diminished form. The great age of the overland Silk Route, in which Persian silk travelled from Tabriz to Bursa and on to Venice and Lucca, had passed. The Ottoman Empire, once at the centre of this trade, became progressively more dependent on European commercial intermediaries — a transformation that Inalcik identifies as a turning point in Ottoman economic history.
