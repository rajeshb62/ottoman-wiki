Ottoman Maritime Infrastructure and Naval Practice

Overview

The Ottoman navy developed from a modest raiding fleet based at Gallipoli into the dominant galley navy of the Mediterranean under the classical age. Its growth was driven by three imperatives: the struggle with Venice for control of the Aegean and Adriatic; the need to secure the Dardanelles and the sea route between Rumelia and Anatolia; and, after the conquest of Egypt and Syria (1516-17), the defence of the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean against the Portuguese. The navy was both a war-fighting instrument and the enforcer of the empire’s closed economic system, particularly in the Black Sea, which after 1475 became an Ottoman lake.

1. The Imperial Arsenal (Tersane-i Amire / ترسانه عامره)

The chief naval dockyard and administrative centre of the Ottoman fleet was the Tersane-i Amire (Imperial Arsenal), located on the Golden Horn (Haliç) at Kasımpaşa, Istanbul. Under Mehmed II it was expanded into a major industrial complex. After the destruction of the Ottoman fleet at Lepanto (1571), the Arsenal’s ceaseless labour rebuilt the navy over a single winter, enabling Venice to make peace in 1573 — testament to the Arsenal’s productive capacity.

The Arsenal was administered by the tersane emini (arsenal superintendent), who oversaw shipbuilding, provisioning, and the supply of naval stores (timber, hemp, sailcloth, pitch, and iron). The grand vizier retained the right to visit and inspect the Arsenal at any time.

From the second half of the 16th century, the kapudan-i derya (grand admiral) presided over a separate naval council at the Arsenal, dealing with matters of the fleet, lawsuits, nominations, appointments, and dismissals.

Secondary dockyards existed at Gallipoli (Gelibolu), Sinop on the Black Sea, Suez on the Red Sea, and later at Basra on the Persian Gulf.

2. Shipbuilding and Vessel Types

Ottoman naval architecture reflected the Mediterranean galley tradition, adapted and scaled up for imperial requirements.

Kadırga (قاديرغا) — The standard Ottoman galley, a narrow, shallow-draft, oar-and-sail warship powered primarily by rows of oarsmen (slaves, prisoners, or levend volunteers) and carrying a lateen sail. Kadırgas were the backbone of the Aegean and Mediterranean galley fleet. They were fast, manoeuvrable in coastal waters, and could be beached for landing operations.

Baştarde (باشترده) — A larger, heavier galley, sometimes described as a “great galley” or bastarda. These were built for flagships and carried heavier armament, including cannon mounted on the prow. During the Venetian war of 1499-1502, Bâyezid II’s Ottomans constructed two warships of 1,800 tonilato, the largest then known — likely baştarde-type vessels built to overawe Venice.

Kalyon (كاليون) — The sailing galleon, a later introduction. At the Battle of Lepanto (1571) the Ottoman fleet was still composed overwhelmingly of galleys, which proved ineffective against the tall ships of the Christian allies capable of firing powerful broadsides. The Ottoman navy adopted the kalyon only very late and with great difficulty. By 1607 Sir Thomas Sherley observed that one English warship could defeat ten Turkish galleys. Technological conservatism around the sailing ship marked the beginning of Ottoman naval decline.

Other vessels: The fleet included smaller craft — kalita (light galley), pergende, firkate (frigate), and şayka (river/coastal vessel). A Danube river fleet of eighty to a hundred light vessels was maintained as early as 1442. The Cossacks, from the 1590s, used small, agile şayka fleets to raid the Black Sea coast with devastating effect, a challenge the Ottoman galley navy could not meet.

3. Main Ports and Naval Bases

Istanbul — The imperial capital and home of the Tersane-i Amire. The Golden Horn served as the main fleet anchorage. The arsenal complex at Kasımpaşa provided the empire’s largest concentration of shipbuilding capacity, naval stores, and dock labour.

Gelibolu (Gallipoli) — The earliest Ottoman naval base. Under Bayezid I the Ottomans built a fortress and an inner harbour enclosed by a wall at Gallipoli, mooring a small raiding fleet there. In 1416 the Venetians burned the Ottoman fleet at Gallipoli and tried to destroy the base. By 1442 the Ottomans maintained sixty ships there. Under Mehmed II, Gallipoli’s role shifted to secondary status after the Arsenal at Istanbul was built, but it remained an important outport for the Dardanelles. After 1453 the fortresses at Çanakkale (the twin castles) and the fortification of Bozcaada (Tenedos) secured the straits.

İzmir — A major Aegean port and commercial hub. In the later 16th and 17th centuries it became the leading export port for Anatolian goods and a centre of English and French Levant trade. Its strategic position made it an important staging post for the Aegean fleet.

Trabzon (Trebizond) — After the conquest of the Comnene empire in 1461, Trebizond became the main Ottoman naval base on the eastern Black Sea, controlling the sea route to the Caucasus and linking with the Crimean khanate.

Kefe (Caffa) — The Genoese colony of Caffa in the Crimea was conquered in 1475. It became the principal Ottoman port in the northern Black Sea and a key node for the grain, slave, and fish trades with the steppe. Ottoman customs registers for 1490 show seventy-five vessels calling at Caffa in four months — Greek, Italian, Russian, and Muslim — linking it with Istanbul, Trebizond, Sinop, Azov, and İzmit.

İskenderiye (Alexandria) — The great Egyptian port and the western terminus of the Red Sea-Indian Ocean spice route. Venetian merchants bought up to twelve thousand quintals of spices annually in Alexandria between 1560 and 1564. The Istanbul-Alexandria convoy route was the empire’s most valuable maritime artery.

Süveys (Suez) — After Selim I’s conquest of Egypt (1517), Suez was developed as the Ottoman Red Sea arsenal. Selim ordered a fleet built there to expel the Portuguese from the Indian Ocean. In 1538 a fleet of thirty ships sailed from Suez against Diu. Suez remained the base for Ottoman Indian Ocean operations throughout the 16th century.

Other ports: Sinop (Black Sea arsenal and shipbuilding centre), Antalya (southern Anatolia), Avlonya/Vlorë (Albanian coast, launch point for the Otranto expedition of 1480), and Basra (second Indian Ocean base after 1546).

4. The Aegean and Mediterranean Galley Fleet

The Mediterranean war fleet grew from thirty galleys in the 1470s under Mehmed II to ninety-two galleys by the end of his reign. Under Süleyman I the fleet reached its apogee: 110 galleys were sent to assist Francis I in 1543, and the fleet wintered in Toulon — a demonstration of Ottoman reach in the western Mediterranean.

The Aegean islands were gradually incorporated. Rhodes was taken from the Knights of St John in 1522, securing the route between Istanbul and Alexandria. Chios was annexed in 1566, Cyprus conquered from Venice in 1570-71.

The galley fleet operated on a seasonal cycle: fitting out in spring at the Istanbul Arsenal, summer campaigning in the Aegean or eastern Mediterranean, and wintering (often beached and covered) at principal bases. Convoys of merchant ships (often fifty vessels or more) sailed from Istanbul to Alexandria escorted by warships, the kapudan-i derya himself accompanying them into the Aegean.

5. The Kapudan Pasha and the Naval Organization

Kapudan-i derya / قپودان پاشا — The Grand Admiral of the Ottoman fleet. In the glossary of Inalcik’s work, the title is defined as “the admiral of the sea, the grand admiral of the Ottoman fleet.”

In the classical period the kapudan-i derya was initially a military commander elected from the sea captains and corsair leaders. With the appointment of Hayreddin Barbarossa in 1533 as kapudan-i derya and beylerbeyi of Algiers, the office was elevated. The beylerbeyilik of Algiers was created to unify all naval forces against Charles V, and Barbarossa united under his administration Algiers and thirteen sanjaks on the shores and islands of the Mediterranean.

From the second half of the 16th century, as the fleet grew in importance, the kapudan-i derya came to be chosen from among the viziers and gained the right to sit in the imperial council. He also presided over a separate naval council (the kapudan paşa divanı) which dealt with naval matters, lawsuits arising in the fleet, and all nominations, appointments, and dismissals of naval personnel.

The kapudan-i derya’s authority extended not only over the fleet but also over the coastal sanjaks of the Mediterranean and Aegean, including parts of the Morea, the Archipelago, and later Cyprus. This vast maritime province was known as the Kapudanlık or Cezayir-i Bahr-i Sefid (Islands of the White Sea, i.e. the Mediterranean).

Supporting officers included:

  • Tersane emini — superintendent of the Arsenal
  • Tersane kethüdasi — steward of the Arsenal
  • Kapudan kethüdasi — admiral’s deputy
  • Reis — captain of an individual vessel or squadron

6. The Barbary Corsairs and Ottoman Naval Power

The Barbary corsairs of North Africa — based at Algiers, Tunis, and Tripoli — were integral to Ottoman naval strength in the western and central Mediterranean. The greatest of them, Hayreddin Barbarossa (Hayreddin Pasha, originally the corsair Hızır Reis, called Barbarossa by Europeans), was appointed kapudan-i derya in 1533 after his conquest of Algiers. Under him the Ottoman fleet defeated the combined Christian fleet under Andrea Doria at Preveza in 1538, leaving the Ottomans undisputed masters of the Mediterranean for several decades.

The corsair tradition was inherited from the gazi frontier ethos of the early Ottoman beylik. Uruç Reis (Barbarossa’s elder brother), Turgut Reis (Dragut), Piyale Pasha, and Kılıç Ali Pasha were all products of this Mediterranean frontier. The corsair economy — raiding Christian shipping, capturing slaves and booty — was both self-financing and strategically useful, projecting Ottoman power far beyond the reach of the imperial fleet alone.

After the 1590s, however, the central government began to lose control over its North African provinces. Naval forces from Tripoli, Tunis, and Algiers no longer formed a regular part of the sultan’s fleets, but acted as corsairs on their own initiative, sometimes even making separate treaties with European powers.

7. The Black Sea as an Ottoman Lake (after 1475)

The transformation of the Black Sea into an Ottoman lake was one of Mehmed II’s most enduring strategic achievements. In 1454, the year after the conquest of Istanbul, the Ottoman fleet sailed into the Black Sea and compelled the Genoese colonies, the Comnene kingdom of Trebizond, and Moldavia to pay tribute.

The decisive step came in 1475 with the conquest of the Genoese colony of Caffa and the fortresses of Azov. In 1484 Kilia and Akkerman at the mouths of the Danube and Dniester were taken. With the Crimea becoming an Ottoman vassal state under the Giray khans, the entire littoral was under Ottoman suzerainty. The sultan forbade Italian ships from entering the Black Sea, and foreign shipping was closely inspected at Istanbul and Gallipoli.

The Black Sea trade became an internal Ottoman economy: grain, fish, oil, salt, slaves, and timber flowed from the northern coasts to Istanbul, the Aegean, and the Balkans. Ottoman subjects — Armenians (especially those from Caffa), Greeks, Jews, and Muslim Turks — displaced the Italian merchants.

This monopoly held until the late 16th century, when Cossack raids from the Dnieper began to threaten Ottoman shipping. From the 1590s the Cossacks intensified their attacks, burning Sinop in 1614 and Yeniköy on the Bosphorus in 1625. Between 1637 and 1642 they held Azov. The Black Sea, the lifeline of the Ottoman economy, was no longer secure.

8. The Red Sea Fleet and Indian Ocean Expeditions

The Ottoman entry into the Indian Ocean was a response to Portuguese expansion. When the Portuguese destroyed a Mamluk fleet at Chaul in 1509, the Mamluk sultan appealed to the Ottomans, who sent shipbuilding materials and craftsmen to Suez.

After the Ottoman conquest of Egypt (1517), Selim I ordered the construction of a fleet at Suez to drive the Portuguese from the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean. The Ottoman admiral Selmân Reis repulsed Portuguese attacks on Jidda in 1517 and 1525, then advanced on Yemen and Aden.

In 1538 a fleet of thirty ships sailed from Suez against the Portuguese at Diu in northern India. The expedition failed because the local Muslim ruler of Gujarat refused to cooperate, fearing Ottoman domination. Nevertheless, the Ottomans successfully subjugated Yemen and Aden, and made Basra a second base for Indian Ocean operations after 1546.

In 1552 an Ottoman expedition failed to oust the Portuguese from Hormuz. But the empire continued to receive spices directly from India and Indonesia throughout the 16th century. As late as 1554-64 Venetian purchases of twelve thousand quintals of spices annually in Alexandria matched pre-Vasco da Gama levels. Twenty ships laden with spices arrived at Jidda every year.

In 1585, an Ottoman fleet from Suez captured Portuguese factories on the East African coast, and the Prince of Mombasa recognized Ottoman suzerainty. These gains were short-lived: superior Portuguese and Spanish forces drove the Ottomans back, and by the early 17th century the Dutch and English dominated the Indian Ocean with their superior ships.

9. Naval Logistics and Provisioning

The galley fleet required prodigious logistical support. A galley of 150 oarsmen consumed enormous quantities of fresh water, biscuit, and salted provisions for a summer campaign. Naval timber came chiefly from the forests of the Black Sea coast (especially around Sinop) and the mountains of southern Anatolia. Hemp for ropes and sails was produced in the Balkans and Anatolia.

The extraordinary taxes levied to finance the fleet caused widespread discontent. After Lepanto, provincial military forces did everything in their power to avoid participating in naval campaigns. By the late 16th century the empire had, in Inalcik’s words, “exceeded its material capabilities.”

The kürekçi (oarsmen) were a persistent problem. While slaves and prisoners-of-war rowed some galleys, the fleet relied heavily on levend volunteers and provincial levies. The introduction of sail over oar in European warships after Lepanto rendered the Ottoman logistical model obsolete.

10. Decline of the Ottoman Navy in the Late 16th Century

Inalcik identifies several causes for the decline of Ottoman naval power:

Technological stagnation: The Ottoman fleet at Lepanto (1571) was still composed entirely of galleys, ineffective against the tall, broadside-firing ships of the Christian allies. The arrival of the Dutch and the English in the Mediterranean at the end of the 16th century accelerated this disadvantage. One English warship, a contemporary observed, could defeat ten Turkish galleys. The Ottoman navy adopted the new sailing ships only very late and with great difficulty.

Fiscal overstretch: The combined wars with Austria and Safavid Iran (1578-1606) coincided with the naval rebuilding after Lepanto. The extraordinary taxes levied to finance the fleet caused widespread unrest. Provincial timar-holders and sipahis evaded naval service.

Loss of control over North Africa: Naval forces from Tripoli, Tunis, and Algiers ceased to be regular elements of the sultan’s fleet and became independent corsairs. English and Dutch pirates — sometimes cooperating with the Algerian corsairs — began raiding Ottoman merchant fleets.

Cossack raids on the Black Sea: From the 1590s, Cossack fleets of small, fast şayka boats raided the Black Sea coast with impunity. They burned Sinop (1614), Yeniköy on the Bosphorus (1625), and held Azov (1637-42). The Ottoman galley fleet could not effectively engage these agile raiders. The Black Sea, once the secure heartland of the Ottoman economy, was now a zone of insecurity.

By the opening of the 17th century, Ottoman naval supremacy was a memory. The empire could still project power in the eastern Mediterranean, but it could no longer contest the open sea against a determined European coalition.
