Ottoman Law — Kanun and Seriat

The legal system of the Ottoman Empire under Suleiman the Magnificent was a dual structure that balanced two distinct bodies of law: the Şeriat (شرع, Sheri or Sacred Law of Islam) and the Kanun (قانون, the written decrees of the sultans). These were supplemented by Adet (عادت, established custom) and Urf (عرف, the sovereign will of the reigning sultan). This arrangement gave the Ottoman state a flexible and durable legal framework, one that Lybyer describes as the empire’s “written constitution” — a constitution not subject to amendment by the sovereign, but capable of limited evolution through judicial and juristic interpretation.

The Four Sources of Ottoman Law

Lybyer identifies four distinct layers of law operating within the Ottoman Empire:

Şeriat — the Sacred Law of Islam, based on the Qur’an, supplemented by the hadith (traditions of the Prophet’s legal decisions and sayings), the decisions of the early caliphs, and the interpretations of early judges. It was first formulated by Abu Hanifa, the earliest of the four great orthodox Islamic legal schools, whose code became the accepted teaching tradition for all Turkish peoples. The Şeriat was above the sultan and unchangeable by him. It served as a written constitution for the empire, and any attempt by the sovereign to transgress it was understood to absolve the Moslem community from allegiance.

Kanun — the written decrees of the sultans, issued in accordance with a formula of the Sacred Law that recognised the ruler’s right to make all civil and political regulations demanded by prudence and the public welfare. Kanuns dealt with military, financial, feudal, criminal, and police law, as well as the law of ceremonies. Within the fields of feudal law and the law of ceremonies — matters entirely non-existent in the early Islamic state — the sultans had a free hand; in all other areas their Kanuns were strictly supplementary and administrative.

Adet — established custom, corresponding primarily to the unwritten regulations under which the Turks of the steppe lands had lived. Adet supplemented the Sacred Law and the Kanuns in matters they did not cover, and differed from district to district. It carried such binding force that to the unlettered citizen it formed an indistinguishable whole with the Sacred Law and the Kanuns, seeming almost a feature of the natural order.

Urf — the sovereign will of the reigning sultan, the seat and organ of sovereignty. When expressed and written, Urf became Kanun and annulled all contradictory Kanuns and Adet. However, against the Şeriat, Urf had no force; any attempt to exercise it in transgression of the Sacred Law was an invitation to disaster and justified revolution.

The Sultan as Legislator

Under the Ottoman system, the sole power to issue legislation rested in the sultan — but this power was sharply limited by the supremacy of the Sacred Law. Suleiman’s authority as legislator rested on his position as caliph, head of the Islamic state, defender and executor of the Sacred Law. The Sacred Law recognised no power of legislation in the head of state, since God through Mohammed had legislated once for all; it entrusted to him only the functions of administration and justice, to be exercised subject always to the prescriptions of the Law.

Suleiman was named Kanuni (قانونی, the Legislator) by comparison with his predecessors, who had been men of the sword rather than of the pen. By his accession, the feudal system, taxation, land tenure, and criminal law had come into great disorder. Egyptian affairs were in wild confusion after the Mamluk conquest. Suleiman therefore issued a large number of Kanuns dealing especially with timars (fiefs), rayahs (subjects), ceremonies, criminal and market regulations, and comprising a constitution for Egypt — the Kanun-nameh Misr (قانوننامه مصر), issued in 1532.

Lybyer emphasises that Suleiman’s legislation was doubly hindered: first by the conservatism of his people and his religion, which believed the old ways were best, and second by the weakness inherent in despotic legislation, in which the distance of the lawgiver from the subjects affected made true adaptation to circumstances impossible. Most of his laws professed to restore a former better state of affairs; they likely did little more than formalise a confused body of practice that had grown up gradually.

The Kanun-nameh of Suleiman and Mufti Ebu Su’ud

The best-known collection of Suleiman’s laws was compiled by the Mufti Ebu Su’ud (ابو السعود), one of the most distinguished ornaments of Suleiman’s reign. Ebu Su’ud had passed through all the stages of advancement among the Ulema and had served as Kaziasker for eight years before being appointed Mufti. He wrote a great commentary on the Qur’an and assembled the most famous Kanun-nameh (قانوننامه, book of laws) of Suleiman.

Lybyer cautions against thinking of a Kanun-nameh as resembling the codes of Theodosius or Justinian. The Kanun-nameh of Suleiman as arranged by Ebu Su’ud was not a unified body of law but a collection of Kanuns issued at different times, dealing chiefly with:

  • Land tenure and hereditary tenancy — laws concerning the tapu (hereditary lease of land), fallow fields, uncultivated lands, the relationship of tenants and absent tenants, and the rules for when a spahi (fief-holder) died or was dispossessed before delivery of the lease
  • Taxation — laws on the ground tax, the tithe (öşür), the fifth, taxes on vineyards, mills, green produce, honey, bees, sheep, the fold-tax, the half-hide-tax (bride-tax), the hide-tax, the obligations of subjects, and the tax on prisoners and slaves
  • The feudal system — regulations for fief-holders of both large and small fiefs, laws concerning the intendants of the fiscus and receivers of taxes, regulations for the Beylerbeys, Sanjak Beys, and Kapuji-bashis, and rules governing lawsuits between spahis and non-Moslems or between two spahis
  • Criminal and police law — judge’s fees, the contents of judge’s berats (diplomas of appointment), laws concerning fugitives, wandering hordes, wagoners, and irregular cavalry
  • Imperial foundations and vakfs — regulations concerning the religious endowments that constituted perhaps one-third of the empire’s land
  • Procedural and judicial matters — rules concerning lawsuits over land, the timing of harvest for legal purposes, and the dating and registering of fiefs

The Kanun-nameh begins at folio 27 of the Vienna manuscript (Fluegel No. 1816, Imperial Library, Vienna) and runs through folio 60, containing over forty distinct laws. It covers the full range of relations between the state, the land, and the subject population.

Şeriat and Kanun: The Relationship

The relationship between the two bodies of law was hierarchical, not parallel. The Şeriat was supreme; the Kanuns were subordinate and supplementary. The judges applied the Sacred Law first — as given in the Qur’an, the traditions of Mohammed, codified by the school of Abu Hanifa, and interpreted in collections of fetvas — and then applied the Kanuns of the sultans and the local customs of the regions in which they served.

Lybyer notes that the distinction between sacred and secular law corresponded inversely to the distinction in the West: to the canon law of the West corresponded the Şeriat, and to the civil or national law of the West corresponded the Kanuns. But the Şeriat had wider sway in sixteenth-century Turkey than the canon law ever had in the West. It dealt with a far larger field, its judges sometimes administered the Kanuns as well, and they had the support of the national government — whereas the rival courts of the great officials had ordinarily a very limited jurisdiction.

The practical regulations of the Sacred Law went by the Arabic name fikh (فقه); it included both jurisprudence and positive law. In the Ottoman Empire of the sixteenth century, the Şeriat held the place of overwhelming preeminence in legal matters, both in usefulness and in honour. Its practical precepts to the full extent of their formulated scope were the private law of the land; its judges were of equal or greater authority than the high officers of government.

The Mufti and the Fetva

Among the Ulema (علماء, learned men) who had completed the law course, a distinct body — the müftis (مفتون) — served as jurisconsults rather than judges. One mufti was assigned as associate to the judge of every important city, to the number of about two hundred in all, while others were counsellors for the Beylerbeys and Sanjak Beys. Appointed for life and living in retirement, they had no initiative of action. When a judge, Bey, or private citizen submitted a written question involving a learned knowledge of the Sacred Law, the mufti’s duty was to give an answer that applied the Sacred Law to the matter concerned. These answers, called fetvas (فتوى), were extremely concise, unaccompanied by reasoning, and prepared and sealed in solemn form.

When a judge or Bey proposed to his mufti a question touching a pending law case, the mufti’s response ordinarily settled the case. Since there was no class of professional lawyers in the Ottoman Empire, the muftis were a necessary and highly useful body.

The Şeyhülislam: Supreme Guardian of the Law

The mufti of Constantinople became par excellence the Mufti (مفتى). Mohammed II had assigned him the title of Şeyhülislam (شيخ الإسلام, the Ancient of Islam), which in later times became his ordinary designation. Suleiman definitively constituted the Şeyhülislam the head of the Ulema, outranking all officials of government except that he yielded place to the grand vizier on ordinary occasions. He was nearly the equal of the sultan himself, since he was the expounder and representative of the Sacred Law, which stood above the sultan.

Sixteenth-century Western observers compared the Mufti with a cardinal, but more often with the Pope of Rome. The comparison was apt in dignity and in the magnitude of the matters with which he dealt. He alone could proclaim that war should be begun, and send out preachers to declare it holy and incumbent on all Moslems. He was frequently consulted by the sultan on the conformity of proposed Kanuns with the Sacred Law. In his hands rested the ultimate responsibility of pronouncing that a sultan had transgressed the Sacred Law and ought to be deposed.

Lybyer draws a direct parallel between the Mufti’s power and that of the United States Supreme Court: the Mufti was the final earthly authority in interpreting the Sacred Law, exercising the power of defending the constitution. The fetvas of the muftis amounted in practice to a body of legislation intermediate between the Şeriat and the Kanuns, partaking of the sacred character of the former while being of a modern and practical nature derived from recent application to actual cases.

Ebu Su’ud’s role in the execution of Suleiman’s eldest son Mustapha illustrates the Mufti’s enormous moral authority. Suleiman, seeking to satisfy religious scruples before ordering his son’s death, presented a hypothetical case to the Mufti: a merchant left a slave in charge of his property, wife, and children; the slave embezzled and plotted against the family. Ebu Su’ud answered that such a slave deserved to be tortured to death — and this fetva, as Busbecq records, greatly influenced Suleiman’s decision.

The Judicial Hierarchy

The judges belonging to the Ulema corps had jurisdictions based on territory, covering the entire empire to an even wider extent than did the administrative government. The tribunals of the judges were competent for all kinds of cases — civil or criminal, whether covered by the Şeriat, the Kanuns, Adet, or none of these. However, they were not competent to try all persons: the kullar (sultan’s slaves), the Seids (descendants of the Prophet), and members of foreign colonies had their separate systems of justice.

The judges were carefully graded in five principal classes:

Kaziaskers (قاضي عسكر) — the two chief judges of the empire: the Kaziasker of Rumelia (Europe) and the Kaziasker of Anatolia (Asia). They ranked next to the viziers in the Divan, had first right of audience with the sultan at the close of each meeting of the council, and until Suleiman’s reign had held all the authority over the Ulema that later came to the Mufti. They controlled the appointment of all judges of other classes, subject to confirmation by the sultan. In the Divan and as “Pillars of the State,” they were among the four loftiest supports of Ottoman government, alongside the viziers, the defterdars (treasurers), and the nishanjis (chancellors).

Greater Mollas — in six groups: the Kaziaskers themselves; the judge of Constantinople; the judges of Mecca and Medina; the judges of Adrianople, Brusa, Cairo, and Damascus; and the judges of the three suburbs of Constantinople (Galata, Scutari, Eyub) and of Jerusalem, Smyrna, Aleppo, Larissa, and Salonica — seventeen judges in all.

Lesser Mollas — the judges of ten cities of second rank, including Marash, Baghdad, Sarajevo, Sofia, Belgrade, Konya, and Diyarbekir.

Mufettishes — five special judges dealing with disputes over vakf (religious endowment) lands.

Kazis (قاضى) — the vast majority of judges, about four hundred and fifty, stationed in smaller cities. About two hundred in Europe and the Crimea were under the Kaziasker of Rumelia; about two hundred and twenty-five in Asia and thirty-six in Egypt were under the Kaziasker of Anatolia.

Naibs (نائب) — inferior judges in several groups: judges of villages, lesser judges of cities, temporary substitutes for higher judges, and inspectors of public morals.

The Kaziaskers

The two Kaziaskers (قاضي عسكر, judge of the army) held a pivotal position in Ottoman law. Their titles reveal their original function as judges of the armies of Rumelia and Anatolia — offices they continued to exercise in time of war. In this capacity, appeals came up to them from the Subashis and Sanjak Beys in matters touching the kullar.

Under Mohammed II, it was provided that cases concerning Moslems in Constantinople should come before the Kaziasker of Rumelia, and those concerning non-Moslems before the Kaziasker of Anatolia. Their power had been extended to include the headship of all judges of their respective regions and the appointment of all judges. Each had a large corps of subordinate officials. They held court in their own houses, in addition to their duties in the Divan and at the palace gate.

Court Procedure and Appeals

In every court a single judge sat, with his clerks and other subordinates. Cases were presented by the parties themselves, and decisions were usually rendered immediately in very concise form. The judge cooperated with the Subasi (subashi) of the city, who brought summoned persons before the judge and executed the sentences — an arrangement akin to the ecclesiastical courts of the West, which could condemn but left execution to the secular arm.

Appeal went up through the judicial hierarchy: from the Naibs to the Kazis, to the Mollas, and finally to the two Kaziaskers. The highest courts were those of the Kaziaskers, the grand vizier (who was actual head of the Moslem Institution as substitute for the sultan, and whose court was the highest court of appeal for ordinary civil cases), and the Divan (the imperial council, which tried capital cases of great officials).

Costs and fines were moderate and fixed by Kanun, but they constituted a large part of the income of judges and their subordinates. The judges were salaried; some had in addition large irregular earnings. The judges also attended to all the notarial work of the empire.

Significance

Lybyer’s analysis of Ottoman law reveals a system that was both rigid and flexible — rigid in its supreme commitment to the unchanging Sacred Law, flexible in the space it allowed for sultanic legislation in areas the Sacred Law did not touch. The dual structure of Şeriat and Kanun, held in balance by the Mufti’s power of constitutional interpretation, gave the Ottoman Empire a stability that outlasted many other early modern states. But Lybyer also notes the system’s fundamental weakness: the idea of the changelessness of the Sacred Law was essentially hostile to progress. While considerable flexibility was possible in the application of the law to particular cases, the law itself could hardly change. The Ulema, who had built and maintained the Ottoman legal structure, also held the empire nearly stationary while the rest of the world moved on.
