Ottoman Trade with Venice, Genoa, and Europe

Ottoman Ticâreti — Venedik, Ceneviz ve Avrupa ile Münâsebetler

The Capitulations (Ahidnâmeler / İmtiyâzât) with Venice and Genoa

The framework of Ottoman trade with the Italian maritime republics rested on a series of commercial treaties known in Ottoman Turkish as ahidnâmeler or, later, imtiyâzât (capitulations). These were unilateral grants by the sultan, not bilateral treaties in the modern sense: the sultan conferred privileges on foreign merchants as an act of sovereign grace, revocable at his pleasure, and the grants applied only for his lifetime, requiring renewal by each successor.

The earliest recorded capitulation dates from 1352, when the Genoese — already established at Galata (Pera) across the Golden Horn from Constantinople — concluded a commercial agreement with the Ottoman ruler Orhan Gazi. This was a natural alliance: Genoa was Venice’s great rival, and the Ottomans exploited this rivalry skilfully. The Genoese received preferential access to Anatolian trade, including a long-term monopoly over alum production in the Manisa region — the principal source of alum for the European textile industry. The Genoese colonies at Foça (Phocaea) on the western Anatolian coast and the island of Chios became the primary ports of entry for Anatolian goods. In return, Genoese ships rendered the Ottomans vital military assistance, transporting Ottoman troops across the Dardanelles in 1421 and 1444, at times when Venetian naval power controlled the straits. During the siege of Constantinople in 1453, the Genoese of Galata observed a carefully calculated neutrality.

For Venice, the first major Ottoman capitulation came after the capture of Constantinople in 1453. Mehmed II (Mehmed the Conqueror) understood that the economic life of his new capital depended on continued commercial intercourse with the West. In the immediate aftermath of the conquest, he granted Venice a capitulation permitting a Venetian ambassador — the bailo — to reside in Istanbul and Venetian merchants to trade freely throughout the empire, on the condition that they pay customs duties. The rate was set at a remarkably low 2 per cent, a figure that reflected Mehmed’s desire to keep Venetian commerce flowing into the city.

The terms of these early capitulations were detailed and specific. They guaranteed the security of merchants and their goods, freedom of navigation, the right to worship, the right to settle disputes among themselves according to their own laws, and freedom from arbitrary taxation beyond the agreed customs. The Venetian bailo and the Genoese podestà in Galata functioned as both diplomatic representatives and judicial authorities over their respective mercantile communities.

The Venetian Bailo in Istanbul

The Venetian bailo (baylos in Ottoman usage) was the senior Venetian diplomatic and commercial representative in Istanbul, a position of extraordinary influence that lasted from the Byzantine era through the entire Ottoman period. The bailo resided in a palatial compound in Galata, the Palazzo del Bailo (later the Venetian Embassy), and exercised jurisdiction over all Venetian subjects in the Ottoman Empire.

The bailo’s functions were manifold: he represented Venetian interests at the Sublime Porte, negotiated the renewal of capitulations on the accession of each new sultan, adjudicated commercial disputes among Venetian merchants, maintained intelligence networks across the Levant, and reported regularly — in the famous relazioni — to the Venetian Senate on Ottoman political, military, and economic affairs. The bailo’s reports constitute one of the richest archival sources for Ottoman history in the sixteenth century; they were read with the utmost seriousness in Venice and often shaped the Republic’s strategic decisions.

During the 1463–1479 Ottoman-Venetian war, Mehmed II imprisoned all Venetian merchants and confiscated their goods, and the bailo was expelled. But with the return of peace, the bailo resumed his post, and his office continued without interruption into the seventeenth century. The bailo was assisted by a chancellor, interpreters (dragomans), and a staff of clerks. He oversaw the Venetian merchant colony in Galata, the Venetian fondaco (warehouse and trading compound), and the church of San Francesco. The bailo’s position was so central to Ottoman-Venetian relations that even periods of open war did not entirely sever the connection: messages continued to pass through intermediaries, and peace negotiations almost always began with the re-admission of the bailo.

Venetian Merchant Colonies in Galata

Galata — called Galata or Pera in European sources, and غلطه (Galata) in Ottoman Turkish — was not a single colony but a mosaic of Italian merchant communities, of which the Venetians were the most prominent after 1453. Under Byzantine rule, the Genoese had dominated Galata, but after the Ottoman conquest, Mehmed II confirmed the Genoese in their privileges while also granting the Venetians extensive commercial facilities.

The Venetian colony in Galata was organized as a self-governing community under the bailo’s authority. Venetian merchants resided in a designated quarter, the Fondaco dei Veneziani, which functioned as a combined warehouse, market, and residential compound. The colony possessed its own church (San Francesco), its own bakery, its own scales and measures, and its own court for civil matters involving Venetians.

The merchant population was not static. Venetian merchants came and went with the sailing seasons, but a core of permanent residents — fattori (factors) representing the great Venetian trading houses — maintained year-round operations. These factors cultivated relationships with Ottoman officials, Jewish and Greek intermediaries, and Muslim merchants. They extended credit, negotiated contracts, and arranged the shipping of goods to Venice via the regular convoys that sailed from Istanbul to the Adriatic.

The Venetian presence extended beyond Galata itself. Venetian merchants were active in Bursa, the great silk market of western Anatolia; in Edirne (Adrianople), the former Ottoman capital; in Salonica; and in the ports of the Morea such as Modon and Coron, which remained Venetian possessions well into the sixteenth century. But Istanbul — and specifically Galata — remained the nerve centre of Venetian Levantine commerce.

Genoese Trade in the Black Sea and the Loss of Caffa

The Genoese presence in the Black Sea preceded the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople by more than two centuries. From their fortified colony at Caffa (modern Feodosia) in the Crimea, and from their other bases at Tana (at the mouth of the Don), Trebizond, and Amasra on the Anatolian coast, the Genoese had dominated Black Sea commerce during the late Byzantine and Mongol periods. The trade was lucrative and varied: Caffa exported grain, fish, caviar, salt, wax, honey, and — most notoriously — slaves.

The slave trade was the backbone of the Genoese Black Sea economy. Tatar raids on Russian, Polish, and Circassian territories supplied a steady stream of captives, who were brought to Caffa and sold to Genoese merchants. From Caffa, the slaves were shipped across the Black Sea to Istanbul and the Mediterranean, where they were sold in the markets of Egypt, Italy, and the broader Islamic world. In pre-Ottoman times the Caffa slave trade was entirely in Genoese hands, and the revenues it generated were enormous.

The Genoese also traded in the products of the Pontic steppe and the Russian forests: furs (sable, fox, ermine), timber, iron, and honey, which they exchanged for Mediterranean goods — wine, olive oil, textiles, and spices — that they brought from Italy and the Levant.

With the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453, the Genoese position in the Black Sea became precarious. Mehmed II, having secured control of the Bosphorus and the Dardanelles, was determined to close the Black Sea to Italian merchants and develop the region as an integral part of the Ottoman imperial economy. The Straits were the key: by inspecting all shipping at Istanbul and Gallipoli, the Ottomans could regulate access to the Black Sea at will.

The decisive blow fell in 1475, when the Ottoman navy under Gedik Ahmed Pasha conquered Caffa, along with the other Genoese colonies at Azov, Taman, and Kerch. The Genoese were expelled, their commercial network dismantled, and their monopoly replaced by Ottoman subjects — Muslims, Greeks, Armenians, and Jews, particularly the Armenians of Caffa itself, who adapted to Ottoman rule and continued much of the transit trade under the sultan’s sovereignty.

In 1484 the Ottomans added Kilia and Akkerman at the mouths of the Danube, closing the last significant access points for Italian shipping to the Black Sea. Henceforth, Italian ships on the Black Sea were rare: a few Venetian vessels carrying wine from Crete or Chios, or ships based in the Crimea belonging to Italians who had become Ottoman subjects. The great Genoese and Venetian commerce of the Black Sea was effectively ended.

The Grain Trade

Grain — primarily wheat — was the most politically sensitive commodity in Ottoman trade with Europe. The wheat of Anatolia, Macedonia, Thrace, and Thessaly was vital not only to Istanbul but also to Venice and the entire Po valley. The Ottoman sultans used the grain trade as a deliberate instrument of policy.

During the reign of Bayezid I (1389–1402), the Ottomans blockaded the Bosphorus and restricted grain exports to pressure Venice in times of conflict. Mehmed II, before the siege of Constantinople in 1453, granted the Venetians permission to export wheat in order to lull them into inaction. After the conquest, however, he forbade the export of foodstuffs to Italy and subjected Italian ships to close inspection at Istanbul and Gallipoli. The Black Sea grain trade — which had once supplied Italy with Ukrainian and Crimean wheat — was redirected entirely to Istanbul. In 1600 the Palace kitchens placed a single order for two thousand kantârs of clarified butter from Caffa alone.

Throughout the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, the export of grain to Venice and the Italian states was permitted only intermittently, as a diplomatic concession. The principle was consistent: the needs of Istanbul — the imperial capital, the largest city in Europe — came first. Wheat surpluses could be exported, but only under license and with the payment of customs duties. This policy gave the sultan powerful leverage over Venice, for the Republic depended on Ottoman grain to feed its population and its colonial possessions in the Aegean and the Ionian Sea.

The Black Sea continued to supply Istanbul with vast quantities of grain throughout the classical age. Wheat, flour, tallow, clarified butter, cheese, and honey were shipped from the Crimea and the Kuban steppe through Caffa to the capital. To increase production, the Crimean Tatar aristocracy settled colonies of Russian prisoners on the steppe as agricultural labourers, and the semi-nomadic peoples of the northern Black Sea region also grew cereals for export. This steady supply was the foundation of Istanbul’s prosperity and a pillar of the Ottoman provisioning system (iaşe).

The Slave Trade

The slave trade was a major component of Ottoman commerce with the Black Sea region and, through it, with Europe. In the pre-Ottoman period, the Genoese at Caffa had dominated the trade, exporting slaves captured by the Tatars from Russian, Polish, and Circassian lands. After the Ottoman conquest of Caffa in 1475, the trade continued under Ottoman auspices, but now the slaves were primarily destined for the Ottoman market rather than for Italian buyers.

Caffa and its dependent ports — Azov, Kerch, Taman, and Kopa — became the principal markets for slaves in the Ottoman world. Captives were typically taken from Taman to Caffa, where they were exchanged for cloth and other goods brought by Anatolian merchants. The state derived a yearly income of eighteen thousand gold ducats from the slave trade in the mid-sixteenth century, and the tax on each slave was four gold ducats. Most slaves went to Istanbul, and some to Sinop and Inebolu on the Anatolian Black Sea coast.

The slave trade was not confined to the Black Sea. Antalya on the Mediterranean coast of Anatolia was also a centre of the slave trade, exporting white slaves to the south and importing Negro slaves from Africa. The Bursa customs registers also record the transit of slaves. Slaves were used in the Ottoman economy as domestic servants, agricultural labourers, soldiers (the Janissary corps was recruited through the devşirme system of child levy), and concubines. The slave trade thus intersected with nearly every aspect of Ottoman economic and social life.

Luxury Goods: Silk, Spices, and Fine Textiles

The luxury trade was the most profitable sector of Ottoman-European commerce. The great commodity was Persian silk, which passed through Ottoman territory on its way to the textile industries of Italy and, later, of France and England.

Bursa, the early Ottoman capital, was the entrepôt of the silk trade. Persian silk caravans from Tabriz travelled the overland route through Erzincan, Amasya, and Tokat to Bursa, avoiding the old route through Trebizond. In Bursa, Italian merchants — Venetians, Genoese, and Florentines — purchased the raw silk and shipped it to the looms of Venice, Lucca, and Florence. The profit margins were enormous: each fardello (about 150 kg) yielded a profit of seventy to eighty gold ducats in Italy. Around a thousand silk looms operated in Bursa, consuming five fardelli of silk per day. The price of silk rose steadily from fifty akçes per fardello in 1467 to eighty-two in 1494.

Spices were the second great luxury commodity. Despite Portuguese competition from the Cape route, the Ottoman spice trade remained robust throughout the sixteenth century. The Venetians alone bought six thousand quintals of spices in Alexandria in 1554, and their annual purchases of twelve thousand quintals between 1560 and 1564 equalled the volume before Vasco da Gama’s discovery. Spices arriving through the Red Sea and Persian Gulf routes were distributed through Aleppo, Damascus, Cairo, Bursa, and Istanbul. The customs registers of Bursa show that European merchants continued to buy spices there throughout the century, and by 1582 customs receipts on spices in Bursa had reached 7,250 gold ducats — four times the level of 1487.

Other luxury goods in the trade included: Bursa brocades and velvets, Ankara camlets (fine mohair fabrics), Anatolian carpets and rugs (especially the famous rugs of Uşak and Gördes), cotton textiles, mohair, opium, dried fruits, wax, and the fine woollens of Florence and Flanders that were imported into the Ottoman Empire in exchange.

The Impact of the Ottoman-Venetian Wars on Commerce

The relationship between Venice and the Ottoman Empire was characterized by a paradoxical intertwining of war and trade. Periods of open conflict — the wars of 1463–1479, 1499–1503, 1537–1540, and 1570–1573 — disrupted commerce severely, yet trade was never entirely extinguished, and peace treaties invariably restored commercial privileges.

During the long war of 1463–1479, Mehmed II imprisoned all Venetian merchants in the empire and confiscated their goods. At the same time, he actively encouraged Florence and Ragusa (Dubrovnik) to take Venice’s place in Ottoman commerce. In 1469, he granted new trade privileges to Florence, and some fifty Florentine houses were soon trading in the empire. By annexing Bosnia and Herzegovina in 1463, Mehmed opened a new overland trade route to Italy through Ragusa and Ancona, bypassing Venetian-controlled sea lanes. The war also forced Venice to relinquish Negroponte (Euboea), Scutari, and other Aegean and Albanian possessions, reducing the Republic’s eastern Mediterranean colonial empire.

The peace treaty of 25 January 1479 imposed harsh terms on Venice. The Republic surrendered Scutari and other fortresses, paid an indemnity of 100,000 gold ducats, and agreed to an annual tribute of 10,000 ducats for permission to trade in Ottoman domains. On the other hand, the sultan granted the Venetians freedom of trade and confirmed the bailo’s residence in Istanbul.

The war of 1499–1503 saw the Ottomans capture Lepanto (Naupaktos), Modon, and Coron, key Venetian bases in the Morea. The loss of these ports dealt a severe blow to Venetian commercial routes in the Ionian Sea. The wars of 1537–1540 and 1570–1573 continued the pattern: Ottoman territorial gains, Venetian commercial losses, and then a restoration of trade under renewed capitulations. After 1570–1573, when the Ottomans conquered Cyprus, Venice lost its last major colonial possession in the eastern Mediterranean, and the Republic’s commercial pre-eminence in the Levant began its irreversible decline.

Through all these conflicts, a pattern held: even at war, the Ottomans allowed trade to continue through intermediaries — Ragusan ships, Jewish merchants, and neutral Greeks — and peace treaties always contained detailed commercial clauses restoring Venetian privileges. Trade was too essential to both powers to be sacrificed entirely to military ambitions.

Trade Routes Across the Mediterranean

The Mediterranean trade routes connecting the Ottoman Empire to Europe formed a complex network shaped by politics, geography, and the shifting balance of naval power.

Istanbul to Venice

The primary sea route ran from Istanbul through the Sea of Marmara, the Dardanelles, and the Aegean, calling at the Venetian-held islands of Crete and Corfu, then across the Ionian Sea and up the Adriatic to Venice. Venetian convoys (mude) sailed this route on a regular schedule, protected by armed galleys. The outward journey carried European woollens, paper, glassware, and silver; the return voyage brought silk, spices, cotton, mohair, carpets, and wheat.

The Adriatic: Ragusa and Ancona

After the Ottoman conquest of Bosnia and Herzegovina in 1463, a new overland route opened through the Balkan interior. From Istanbul, the route passed through Edirne, Plovdiv, Sofia, and Sarajevo to the Adriatic port of Ragusa (Dubrovnik). From Ragusa, goods were shipped across the Adriatic to Ancona, a free port in the Papal States, and then forwarded to Florence and the rest of Italy. This route bypassed Venetian-controlled sea lanes and was actively promoted by Mehmed II as a counterweight to Venetian dominance.

Ragusa, though an Ottoman tributary paying an annual tribute of 12,500 gold ducats, prospered enormously as the intermediary of this trade. Ragusan merchants paid only 2 per cent customs duty, half the rate levied on Venice, and Ragusan ships carried Ottoman goods to Italy, the Adriatic ports, and even to London. During the Ottoman-Venetian wars, Ragusa acted as the essential commercial bridge between the belligerents.

The overland route was secured by a chain of Ottoman caravanserais and fortified stations, and the famous Vişegrad bridge across the River Drina — one of the masterpieces of sixteenth-century Ottoman architecture — was built along this very route. Settlements such as Sarajevo, Skopje, Foça, and Mostar grew into flourishing Ottoman cities thanks to their position on this international trade corridor.

The Aegean and the Levant

A separate network of sea routes connected Istanbul to the ports of the Aegean — Salonica, Izmir (Smyrna), and the islands of Chios, Lesbos, and Rhodes — and from there to the Levantine ports of Alexandria, Beirut, Tripoli, and Alexandretta. These routes carried the spice trade from Egypt and Syria, the silk trade from Aleppo, and the cotton, grain, and olive oil of western Anatolia.

Other Routes: Hungary and Poland

From the Black Sea ports of Caffa, Kilia, and Akkerman, overland routes ran northward through Moldavia to Lwow (Lemberg) in Poland and to the Hungarian plain. These carried Ottoman silks, spices, and cotton northwards, and brought back Polish and Hungarian woollens, Russian furs, and German metals. The wine trade was particularly important: Cretan and Morean wines were shipped to Kilia and then forwarded to Poland and Muscovy.

The Shift from Italian to French and English Dominance (Late 16th Century)

The last quarter of the sixteenth century witnessed a fundamental shift in the structure of Ottoman-European trade. The Italian maritime republics — Venice and, to a lesser extent, Genoa — which had dominated Levantine commerce for centuries, were gradually eclipsed by the Atlantic powers: France, England, and the Dutch Republic.

France

France obtained its first capitulations from the Ottoman sultan in 1569, following negotiations that had begun under Süleyman the Magnificent. The French capitulations of 18 October 1569 were the first to extend to a western monarchy the commercial privileges that had previously been granted only to Venice. They served as the model for all subsequent European capitulations. French consuls took up residence in Istanbul, Alexandria, Beirut, and Tripoli (in Lebanon). French ships bound for the Levant carried Normandy textiles, paper, and German hardware, returning with wool, cotton, silk, spices, perfume essences, drugs, mohair, and Anatolian rugs.

After the Ottoman-Venetian war of 1570–1573, France began to displace Venice as the leading European commercial power in the Levant. By the beginning of the seventeenth century, about a thousand French vessels were active in Levantine commerce, and the volume of trade had risen to thirty million livres — half of France’s total foreign trade. Marseilles became the great French emporium for Levant goods. Other European merchants, especially the English and the Dutch, initially traded under the French flag.

England

The English entry into the Levant trade was more gradual. Süleyman I had granted English merchants the right to trade freely in the empire as early as 1553, but the English did not exploit this privilege immediately, preferring to seek a route through Moscow and Iran to the spice markets of the Indian Ocean. In 1578, the Ottomans invaded Azerbaijan and Shirvan, gaining control of this northern route and cutting off the English option.

With Spain as a common enemy, the English and Ottoman governments saw the advantage of cooperation. Despite opposition from France and Venice, the sultan granted the English a capitulation in 1580, confirmed by a more extensive grant in 1583. On 11 September 1581, the Turkey Company was founded by royal charter; in 1592 it merged with the Venice Company to form the Levant Company, which would dominate Anglo-Ottoman trade for two centuries.

The Ottoman government gave the English preferential customs treatment: English merchants paid only 3 per cent duty, while the French and other foreigners paid 5 per cent (until 1673, when the French also secured the 3 per cent rate). The Dutch traded under the English flag until they received their own capitulation in 1612.

The English competed fiercely with France and Venice. English woollens — fine quality kerseys — were sold at low prices in Ottoman markets, while imports of English tin and steel became vital for the Ottoman arms industry. English pirates, sometimes in cooperation with the Algerian corsairs, raided Venetian and French merchant fleets, and Queen Elizabeth turned a deaf ear to complaints. Venetian trade fell sharply, and by 1630 French trade had declined by half, while the Levant Company dominated Ottoman markets. English consuls established themselves in Istanbul, Izmir, Aleppo, and Alexandretta.

By the end of the sixteenth century, the old pattern of Italian commercial hegemony in the Levant was broken. Ragusa, once a thriving intermediary, declined as the Ottoman economy oriented itself toward the Atlantic states. Venice, confined increasingly to the Adriatic, never recovered its former position. The capitulations, originally a tool of Ottoman statecraft to manage foreign merchants, became the instrument through which European powers progressively penetrated and eventually dominated the Ottoman economy.
