Ottoman Military Organization Under Suleiman the Magnificent

The Military Aspect of the Ruling Institution

The Ottoman government had been an army before it was anything else. Like the Turkish nations of the steppe lands, the Ottoman nation was “born of war and organized for conquest.” Fighting was originally the first business of the state and governing the second. After the capture of Constantinople the necessity of administering immense territories transferred the preponderance to the governmental aspect, but even in Suleiman’s time the two great functions of the Ruling Institution were very closely united. War carried practically the whole government into the field. So completely was the government an army that the more important judges, who did not belong directly to the Ruling Institution, were taken into the field. Suleiman on his last campaign had 48,316 men under pay. Acceptance of the sultan’s pay signified that the recipient was a kul (قول, slave of the sultan). Army and government were one: war was the external purpose, government the internal purpose, of one institution composed of one body of men.

The Janissary Corps

The body of regular infantry known as Yeniçeri (يڭیچرى, “new troops”), which the West called Janissaries, stood in the Western imagination for the sultan’s entire slave-family, though in the sixteenth century it formed not more than a fourth of the whole number. Its members were physically trained beyond comparison with their intellectual education, kept in poverty and hence comparatively irresponsible, and in time of peace largely idle — liable to act as an organized and dangerous mob. Yet in battle they drew up an invincible line behind which the person of their sovereign was safe; their devotion to him was the greater because they were in a special sense his kullar and because he was inscribed in one of their odas and received his pay regularly.

Size and Organization

The Janissaries under Suleiman numbered between twelve and fourteen thousand. They were organized in messes of ten; ten messes constituted an orta or oda (اوطه, chamber), of which there were 165 in Suleiman’s time. Each orta had its own officers promoted from its ranks, and above all the ortas was a graded set of officers under the Agha of the Janissaries (Yeniçeri Ağası, يڭیچری آغاسی). This official had never been a Janissary himself but came through the colleges of pages. He acted as a sort of minister of war for the corps, aided by his Kiaya (lieutenant), his chief Yaziji (scribe), and a bureau of clerks who directed enrolment, pay distribution, promotions, location, supply, and clothing. The Agha was well paid and of great authority, outranking all other generals, though occasionally obliged to yield precedence to two generals of cavalry whose corps were older.

Pay and Privileges

Each Janissary received from three to eight aspers per day. They were clothed once a year by the sultan in coarse blue cloth. They had their residence in two great barracks in Constantinople; married men lived at home in various parts of the city. They contributed a daily sum to a common fund for living expenses, with a steward and a cook providing necessities. Every hundred men transported a tent in the field. They fought on foot as musketeers, halberdiers, or scimitar-wielders. Every three men led a horse to carry clothing. In old age or when service no longer pleased the sultan, they were stricken from the roll and sent as hissarlis (castle guards), while deposed officers were sent as castellans with a feudal grant equal to their previous pay.

They were punishable only by their own officers; not even the grand vizier had direct jurisdiction over them. They were immune from taxation, and they were constantly drained of their ablest men by promotion to the regular cavalry. One hundred and fifty of their best bowmen were detailed as Solaks (solak, صولاق), the sultan’s foot-guards who marched beside him wherever he went.

Discipline

The Janissaries kept marvellous order in camp and were completely obedient to their officers except at crises. They had a strong sense of maintaining their privileges. Busbecq records how the grand vizier Rustem warned him that in war time the Janissaries were “masters, so that not even Solyman himself had control over them, and was actually himself afraid of receiving violence at their hands.” Suleiman succeeded on the whole in keeping them in hand, and they never revolted against him.

The Sipahis of the Porte

The regular cavalry were all included under the general name of Sipahis (sipahi, سپاهی, horsemen), but the name was also applied to one of the four divisions into which Osman’s corps of riders had been organized after the model of the cavalry of the Caliph Omar I. Their organization was older than that of the Janissaries. The members were not organized into a single body, they had high pay, and they were in the presence of excellent opportunities to acquire wealth and rise with rapidity; they appear never to have caused Suleiman any special trouble.

The Six Divisions

The four corps — divided into six regiments — were:

  1. Spahis (Spahi-Oghlan, سپاهی اوغلان) — most honored and best paid; each had to bring five or six armed slaves on horseback.
  2. Silihdars (silahdar, سلحدار, weapon-bearers) — less pay than Spahis; each brought four or five horsemen.
  3. Ulufajis (ulufeci, علوفه‌جی, paid troops) — in two divisions, left and right; each furnished two or three horsemen. Recruited from pages, Janissaries, and occasional promotions from irregular troops.
  4. Ghurebas (gureba, غربا, Foreign Legion) — in two divisions, left and right; least pay and small honor; members came alone, often not even as Ottomans.

Each corps and each division was organized separately with its own general who supervised all its affairs. The actual members of the four corps counted from ten to twelve thousand men, but since most had to bring from two to six additional horsemen, the total force assembled was from forty to fifty thousand.

Battle Formation

In time of battle the regular troops drew up to protect the sultan: the Janissaries were aligned in front, the Spahis proper on the right, the Silihdars on the left, and the Ulufajis and Ghurebas in the rear.

The Feudal Sipahis

Outside the towns, the greater part of the European dominions and a large part of Asia Minor were granted in fief to Moslems who were for the most part not kullar of the sultan. They collected the revenues and exercised seigniorial jurisdiction in their estates, and were officered by the sultan’s kullar.

Land Tenure: Timars, Ziamets, and Khasses

The estates were of three classes:

  • Timars (تیمار) — yearly revenue under twenty thousand aspers
  • Ziamets (زیامت) — yearly revenue from twenty to one hundred thousand aspers
  • Khasses (خاص) — yearly revenue over one hundred thousand aspers

Every fief-holder must appear in person when summoned to war. A Timarji with income reaching six thousand aspers must bring an armed horseman, and another for each additional three thousand aspers. Holders of larger fiefs began at ten thousand aspers and added a horseman for each additional five thousand. In the sixteenth century this service was strictly exacted and the fief-holders were held to residence on their estates.

Heredity and Central Control

One son of a small fief-holder had a right to the fief; not more than three sons of a large fief-holder were entitled to small fiefs. The sons of kullar in high position might receive fiefs large in proportion to the rank of their father, by which they were honorably conveyed from the Ruling Institution into the Moslem population.

In 1530 Suleiman issued a Kanun attaching the granting of all fiefs above a certain size to the central government. Each holder of such a fief must obtain a teskereh (document) from Constantinople to have good title. Only those holding by teskereh were entitled to be called Spahis; others were known as Timarjis or Timariotes. The feudal Spahis of Anatolia were more under the authority of the governor, less well paid, and not so highly esteemed as those of Europe. Through this system the country gentry were kept under good control, the accumulation of estates was prevented, and the territorial army was automatically increased in proportion to the increase of the empire by the granting of new fiefs in newly-conquered lands.

Officers of the Feudal Sipahis

Local government and the command of the feudal Sipahis was cared for by officials who belonged to the sultan’s slave-family:

  • Subashis (subası, subaşı, captains) — in peace, governors of towns with Janissaries and Azabs for police
  • Alai Beys (alay beyi, آلای بكی, colonels) — ready with two to five hundred troops to move as needed
  • Sanjak Beys (sancak beyi, سنجاق بكی) — governed important cities and districts
  • Beylerbeys (بكلربكی, lords of lords) — supreme over all Beys of a region; the Beylerbey of Rumelia resided in Constantinople in peace; the Beylerbey of Anatolia spent much time in his dominions

The enrolled feudal troops of Europe numbered about fifty thousand, and those of Asia (including Anatolia, Karamania, Amasia, and Avandole) thirty thousand. These numbers should be doubled or tripled to allow for the additional horsemen all Spahis were required to bring. About half remained on duty at home in time of war. The feudal troops were brave and eager but lacked the physical strength and practice of fighting in squadrons of the regular troops.

Auxiliary Troops

Azabs (azab, عزب)

Irregular infantry, numbering perhaps ten thousand in peace and forty thousand in war. Some served in garrisons and some with the fleet. In siege and battle they were sent forward to break the enemy’s charge or to fill moats with their own bodies. Survivors were rewarded generously; the rest were believed to pass at once by a martyr’s death to heavenly reward.

Akıncıs (akıncı, آقنجی)

Irregular cavalry numbering perhaps thirty thousand in peace, mainly near the European frontier where they made a living by raiding. They received no pay in peace or war but gathered booty and slaves and hoped for promotion. With the Azabs, their numbers were greatly augmented in war by volunteers, many of whom were criminals and ruffians. These irregular troops were the terror of invaded lands, carrying fire, rapine, and sword over wide areas, while the regular army was held under iron discipline.

Topçus (topçu, طوپجی, artillerymen)

Under the Topçu-başı (طوپجی باشی, chief of artillery), who received seventy aspers per day with a Kiaya and secretary. About two thousand Topçus served under him with six to ten aspers per day, all on foot. They managed the empire’s artillery train.

Cebecis (cebeci, جبه‌جی, armorers)

Under the Cebeci-başı (جبه‌جی باشی, chief armorer), who received sixty aspers per day with a Kiaya and secretary. About fifteen hundred Cebecis served under him with seven to twelve aspers per day, all on foot when the sultan took the field. They maintained the army’s weapons and armor.

Müteferrika (متفرفه, Noble Guard)

A body of from one to two hundred of the choicest graduates from the page schools and sons of high officials. Among them in 1575 were brothers of the Voivodes of Wallachia and Moldavia. The Müteferrika followed immediately after the sultan on horseback and in time of battle were ready to defend him to the end.

Other Corps

The army also included Arabacıs (عرباجی, wagoners) — three thousand under an Arabacı-başı; musicians and trumpeters under a Mihter-başı (مهتر باشی); transport personnel; commissaries; and tribute contingents from the Crimean Tartars, Moldavians, and Wallachians. The Deli (دلی, “crazy”) company of scouts served as advance skirmishers.

Weaponry

The Janissaries carried muskets, halberds, and scimitars. The Spahis of the Porte were armed with bows and painted arrows, light spears (painted green), jewelled scimitars, steel maces, and shields (targes) proof against arrows. The Silihdars were the weapon-bearers. The cavalry wore distinctive headdresses with fluted peaks of fine purple silk, often adorned with black plumes. On campaign the Janissaries alone among the regular troops carried firearms — their primary weapon being the musket — while the rest of the army relied on bows, spears, scimitars, and maces. The artillery corps (Topçus) operated the sultan’s cannon and siege train, in which the Ottomans were recognized masters of their age.

Discipline and Ardor

Contemporary observers were strongly impressed with the wonderful discipline and intense zeal for fighting seen among the Ottomans. The silence, order, and cleanliness of the camps, absolute obedience enforced by severe punishments, submissiveness to long marches, hard labor, and scanty food, the eagerness for battle and recklessness of life — these presented a perfection of discipline that seemed miraculous.

“The Turks come together for war as though they had been invited to a wedding.”

“The Great Turk is the best obeyed by his subjects of all the lords that I know.”

“Their military discipline has such justice and severity as easily to surpass the ancient Greeks and Romans.”

The discipline was summed up by one observer: “They keep the divinest order in the world.”

The Supreme Command

The sultan was commander-in-chief of the entire army — standing, feudal, and irregular. When the army was assembled for a great campaign, it gathered about him. On the march and in camp every body had its place with reference to him; in formation of battle he was the central point about which the whole vast display was organized. His kullar gathered about him in devotion of body and soul, going forth under his leadership against the infidel or the heretic.

In 1529 Suleiman issued to Ibrahim a commission as Seraskier (سرعسكر, general of the army) which placed the entire Ruling Institution, the Moslem Institution, the Ottoman nation, and all subject nations under his command. Four years later Ibrahim added the title of Sultan to that of Seraskier in issuing his daily orders. Suleiman, realizing the empire could have but one head, executed Ibrahim. He appointed no more Seraskiers with such exalted powers, but himself led the army when assembled as a whole. The campaign of Szigeth was the thirteenth he directed in person.

The Indivisibility of the Army

The essential oneness of the army, based on the sultan’s ownership of the standing body and its attachment to his person, was a fact that ultimately set bounds to Ottoman conquest. The empire, bordered by Persia on the east and Austria on the west, had only one great army and could conduct only one serious war at a time. If war was in progress on one frontier and conditions became intolerable on the other, peace had to be made on whatever terms could be had. As Lybyer concludes: “The sultan had but one arm; it was a long arm and a strong one, yet it could reach only a fixed distance, and it could strike but one blow.”

Relationship Between Military Service and Land Tenure

The timar system represented the most systematic link between land tenure and military obligation. The empire’s conquered territories were parceled into fiefs (timars, ziamets, khasses), each granting the holder the right to collect revenues from the lands and peasants in exchange for military service. This was not feudal in the Western sense — the fiefs were not hereditary fiefdoms but conditional grants revocable by the sultan, and they were administered by the sultan’s own kullar as officers. The fief-holders (Timariotes and Zaims) constituted a class of country gentry described by Lybyer as “honest, sober, true to the Moslem faith and to the sultan, better in morals than the kullar if not so able of intellect, the substantial middle class of the empire.” Through this mechanism the army was fed, the countryside governed, and the tax revenues collected, all in a single integrated system. The devşirme (child levy) system that supplied the kapıkulu corps was the other great pillar connecting service to the state with the state’s claim on persons — just as timars represented the state’s claim on land.
