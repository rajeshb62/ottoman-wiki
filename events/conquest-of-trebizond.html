The Conquest of Trebizond (1461)

The Empire of Trebizond

The city of Trebizond (Trabzon) on the southeastern coast of the Black Sea was “from ancient times not only a very great and very beautiful city, but also a very old Hellenic city, which the Ionians and Athenians had founded” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §2). Situated on the eastern gulf of the Black Sea in Asia, it commanded a vast and richly producing region and was the common marketplace of Upper Asia — Armenia, Assyria, and all the surrounding territories.

In later times, Trebizond had become the imperial seat of one of the Comneni, an imperial lineage that had fled there from Byzantium. The Comnenian emperors ruled in succession over many peoples and cities, “in complete peace and without sedition, because the emperors were peace-loving and harmonious” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §5).

By the time of Mehmed II, however, the empire had fallen into factional strife and civil war. The ruling family raged against one another, the inhabitants fell out and attacked each other, and neighboring peoples rose up and frequently raided. As long as Constantinople held and the Romans were masters of the Straits, the Black Sea was closed to the sultan’s great fleet — but after the fall of Constantinople in 1453, everything changed.

Trebizond Becomes Tributary

After Mehmed captured Constantinople and became master of the Straits, the road to the Black Sea and the cities upon it lay open. Trebizond submitted and began to pay homage; its emperors “bending the knee before the Great Emperor, became tributaries” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §8). As long as they remained at peace, paid the tribute, and hatched no plots, Mehmed was at peace with them.

Under David Comnenus (r. 1459-1461), however, the situation deteriorated. The Trebizondines fell out among themselves and paid the tribute reluctantly. Worse, it became known that they were related by marriage to the neighboring princes — including Hasan the son of Tomuris (Uzun Hasan, the Aq Qoyunlu ruler of Tigranocerta/Amid, the Armenians and Medes) and even to the princes of the Iberians (Georgians) — and were plotting against their alliance with the sultan. Mehmed, “inflamed with wrath, launched a campaign against them, wishing to forestall them and deal with them before they could begin anything” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §9).

The Double Campaign: Land and Sea

During the winter of 1460-1461, Mehmed made extensive preparations. As soon as spring opened, he equipped the fleet for departure. Warships were fully armed — long galleys, fifty-oared vessels, and armored ships, about 300 in number, plus transport ships, cannon-carriers, and commercial vessels. They carried vast quantities of weapons: shields, helmets, spears, breastplates, lances, missile weapons, and implements for battering walls. The strongest and best-armed men were embarked as marines. Commanders of the fleet were Kasim Pasha, the governor of Gallipoli, and Yakub Pasha, “a man very experienced in naval affairs and the most excellent fleet commander” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §11).

The fleet sailed out through the Bosporus into the Black Sea with great speed and force, the crews rowing in rivalry, “shouting and exulting,” spreading terror wherever it appeared.

Meanwhile, Mehmed gathered the European cavalry and infantry, crossed into Asia, and transported horses, oxen, pack mules, camels, and every other preparation for war. Passing through Bithynia, he arrived at Prousa (Bursa), where he assembled the entire Asian army. After holding a memorial at his father’s tomb, he departed through Galatia into Paphlagonia.

The army, according to Kritobulos, amounted to 60,000 cavalry and at least 80,000 infantry, not counting pack animals and camp followers (Book IV, §15).

The Surrender of Sinope

Advancing through Cappadocia and crossing the river Halys, Mehmed arrived before Sinope, the most beautiful and richest city on the coast of the Black Sea. It was the common marketplace of the entire vicinity, abounding in every good thing — and its greatest treasure was copper, mined there in enormous quantities. The city had been governed for many years by Isma’il, a powerful man from a noble family, as a hereditary possession.

Mehmed pitched his camp before the city and found his fleet already anchored in the harbors — the fleet commanders had seized the harbors and surrounded the entire city and the small island with their ships.

Isma’il, seeing the vast host and that the camp by land and sea had surrounded him, “was astonished and began to consider what he should now do” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §19). After deliberation, he prepared vast and immensely valuable gifts and went out to meet the sultan.

Mehmed received him graciously and with due honor. After conferring at length, they came to an agreement: Mehmed took over Sinope and Isma’il’s entire realm, and in exchange gave Isma’il the governorship of Scopia (Skopje) in Europe — “the best and most fertile land, and in no way inferior to his own, either in revenue or in administration” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §21).

Kritobulos notes that Mehmed could adduce no fault against Isma’il. He took Sinope because its possession was valuable: it had safe harbors providing a suitable station for the fleet toward Trebizond, it lay in the middle of his empire, and he feared that Uzun Hasan might seize it first, since “Hasan was in every way striving after it and wishing to conquer it” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §23).

The Crossing of the Taurus Mountains

After settling affairs in Sinope, Mehmed commanded Kasim and Yakub to sail directly to Trebizond, occupy the harbors, and blockade the city. He himself advanced with the entire army into the interior toward Mount Taurus.

The Taurus is described as “the greatest mountain of Asia,” separating Lower Asia from Upper Asia, extending from Cape Mycale to the Black Sea near Sinope and uniting with the mountains of the Armenians and Medes. Only Alexander of Macedon (after Heracles and Dionysus) and the Roman Pompey had previously crossed it with an army — and in living memory, Tomuris (Timur), king of the Massagetae and Scythians.

Uzun Hasan, being related by marriage to the Emperor of Trebizond and wishing to help him (but also desiring to conquer Trebizond himself), gathered an army and went to the passes, intending to prevent Mehmed from crossing. Mehmed, learning of this, left Hasan to bide there while he himself cut a different, untrodden route — one that led directly to Tigranocerta, Hasan’s royal residence.

He sent Mahmud Pasha ahead with lightly armed infantry to seize the heights, passes, and hazardous places, then sent unarmed men to cut forest, level rocks, clear brush, and prepare a road. The Taurus, though bearing a single name, consisted of many difficult mountains: “sky-high, snow-covered peaks, very tall and steep crags, dizzying precipices, impassable rocky places, dense forests, and many difficult deep passes and terrible slopes” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §29). The route was so terrible that even lightly armed infantry could scarcely traverse it in eighteen days.

Mehmed organized his army carefully: infantry at the vanguard, baggage in the middle, cavalry with the rearguard. He himself stayed in the midst with a small cavalry force, drawing the vanguard into compact formation through narrow passes. He placed archers on both sides with strung bows, “the archers holding their strung and notched bows in their hands, and indeed crosswise toward the two sides, so that those on the left should shoot to the right and those on the right to the left” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §31).

The Assassination Attempt on Mahmud Pasha

During the crossing, a strange event occurred. A certain evil-minded man, “moved solely by his own madness and wicked soul,” sought the life of Mahmud Pasha. He shot an arrow that struck Mahmud on the forehead — but the wound was not mortal, as the assassin’s hands trembled from agitation. A great uproar arose; the soldiers seized the man and tore him to pieces. Mehmed, “seized by boundless sorrow, distress, and fear” for Mahmud, summoned his physician Yakub, who declared the wound not dangerous. The sultan richly rewarded the physician, and Mahmud soon recovered (Kritobulos, Book IV, §33-35).

Seventeen Days

Advancing incessantly for seventeen days, Mehmed forced his way through deep ravines, dizzying precipices, obstructed terrain, and untrodden wilderness “with great hardship and much exertion” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §36). He crossed the Taurus under arms and descended to the plain, pitching camp not far from Tigranocerta.

Uzun Hasan Sends His Mother

When Uzun Hasan learned that Mehmed had crossed the supposedly impassable Taurus with such a great army and was advancing directly under his royal residence, he was terrified — “as if struck by a thunderbolt” — and fell into terrible confusion. In his distress, he sent his aged mother to Mehmed with countless gifts, excusing himself for everything he had intended to do, asking forgiveness, and beseeching to be the sultan’s ally and friend (Kritobulos, Book IV, §37).

Mehmed received her in a friendly manner and with due honor, accepted Hasan as his friend and ally — but did not let her go immediately, taking her with him.

The Siege and Surrender of Trebizond

The Fleet’s Blockade

The fleet commanders, sailing before Trebizond, seized its harbors and landed. They joined battle with the Trebizondines who marched out before the city, routing them and driving them back with great force, shutting them up in the city. They became masters of the entire environs and the road leading to it, besieging the city by land and sea. They spent twenty-eight days in the siege, during which the citizens sometimes sallied forth — “and in these sallies were in no way inferior to the enemy; nevertheless, being few in number and heavily pressed by the multitude, they were driven back and again shut up in the city” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §40).

Mahmud Pasha’s Negotiations

Mahmud Pasha arrived with the land forces, preceding Mehmed by one day. He sent Thomas, the son of Katabolenos, as his envoy, offering the citizens and their emperor a surrender agreement. He told them it would be better to entrust themselves to the Great Emperor’s faith by agreement, under strong oaths and pledges — the emperor would be provided with much land and sufficient revenue, and the citizens might dwell in peace with their wives and children in their homeland. If they refused, they would “never again have the opportunity so much as to mention agreement and treaty,” once Mehmed took up the war with wrath and passion. Their fate would be “decided by arms and iron,” subjected to “slaughter, plunder, and slavery” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §41-43).

The citizens and their emperor inclined to the proposal, saying they would come to terms when the Great Emperor arrived.

The Surrender

The next day, Mehmed arrived and encamped before the city. He sent the same Thomas as herald, offering the same conditions. The Trebizondines prepared many beautiful gifts and selected the most eminent men among themselves, who went out, prostrated themselves before the sultan, and concluded a treaty. After giving and receiving oaths, they surrendered the city and themselves. They opened the gates and received Mahmud Pasha together with his army.

David Comnenus, the Emperor of Trebizond, came out from the city with his children and all his household to pay homage. Mehmed “received him graciously and humanely, and after shaking his hand and esteeming him fittingly, richly rewarded him, his children, and all who were with him” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §46).

The Inspection

Mehmed entered the city, walked around, and inspected its situation, strength, advantages, construction, and the great number of its inhabitants. He went up to the citadel and the imperial palace, examined and admired the strength of the fortifications, and “declared the city remarkable in every respect” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §47).

He then commanded that the Emperor of Trebizond and all those around him, as well as the most powerful and wealthiest men of the city — with their wives, children, and households — depart and board the galleys. He selected about 1,500 adolescent youths from the city and region, embarked them on the galleys, and sent the fleet away.

Aftermath

The Settlement

Mehmed appointed Kasim Pasha as governor of Trebizond, giving him 400 picked men from the imperial household troops as a garrison. After setting all affairs in order, he departed by the same route.

When he reached Uzun Hasan’s country, he dismissed the mother of Hasan with great gifts and honor, sending envoys with her to renew the treaty. Hasan reciprocated, sending envoys with gifts, thanking the sultan for the honor shown to his mother, and confirming the friendship and alliance.

Mehmed crossed the Taurus without any trouble, traversed the interior in a total of 28 days, arrived at Prousa, disbanded the army, and reached Constantinople toward the end of autumn.

Provision for David Comnenus

Upon arriving in Constantinople, Mehmed’s first care was to provide for the Emperor of Trebizond. He gave David and those with him sufficient land for their sustenance in the region of the Strymon River, from which he could collect an annual revenue of 300,000 silver coins (Kritobulos, Book IV, §53).

(Note: David Comnenus was later executed in 1463 on charges of plotting with Uzun Hasan, an event that falls outside the scope of Kritobulos’ account.)

The Philosopher George Amiroutzes

In the retinue of the Emperor of Trebizond was a certain George Amiroutzes, described as “a most excellent philosopher, who was entirely versed in all that pertains to natural science and metaphysics, arithmetic and geometry, and the theory of proportions” as well as rhetoric and poetics (Kritobulos, Book IV, §54). Mehmed, learning about him, summoned the man. After meeting and conversation, he admired him greatly, granted him a place beside himself, and deemed him worthy of frequent conversation — “presenting the opinions of the ancients and the difficult questions of philosophy, and discussing and solving them together with him; for the Emperor is very strong in philosophy” (Kritobulos, Book IV, §55).

Significance

The conquest of Trebizond eliminated the last surviving Byzantine Greek successor state. The Comnenian dynasty, which had ruled the Pontic Empire since the early thirteenth century, came to an end. The fall of Trebizond completed Ottoman mastery of the southern Black Sea coast and removed a potential ally for Uzun Hasan, with whom Mehmed would later clash at the Battle of Otlukbeli (1473). The sultan’s spectacular crossing of the Taurus — comparable in his mind, and in Kritobulos’, to the feats of Alexander the Great and Pompey — demonstrated his strategic boldness and the formidable reach of the Ottoman army.
