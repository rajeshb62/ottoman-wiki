Conquest of Bosnia (1463)

The Ottoman conquest of the Kingdom of Bosnia in 1463 was a decisive campaign by Sultan Mehmed II that destroyed the independent Bosnian kingdom, executed its last king, Stephen Tomašević, and brought nearly three hundred fortresses under Ottoman control. The campaign is narrated in detail by Kritobulos of Imbros in Book IV of his history of Mehmed II.

Background: The Bosnian Kingdom

The Kingdom of Bosnia, referred to by Kritobulos as the land of the “Paeonians dwelling within the Sava river, whom they also call Dalmatians and, in more recent times, Bosnians” (Section 95), was a substantial realm with extensive pastures, arable land, and natural defenses of vast forests, impassable mountains, and strong fortresses. The Bosnians maintained a firm alliance with the King of Hungary (the “Paeonians” in Kritobulos’ terminology), pledging mutual aid against any attacker.

Despite repeated Ottoman demands, the Bosnian kingdom refused to enter into any treaty with Mehmed II, pay annual tribute (as did their neighbors the Illyrians and Triballi/Serbs), or accept any form of vassalage. As Kritobulos writes, “they wished to remain independent, free, and completely autonomous” (Section 96). Mehmed had sent frequent summons for them to come to terms, but the Bosnians refused, trusting in their mountainous terrain and strong fortresses.

This refusal provoked repeated Ottoman punitive raids. Mehmed “frequently sent armies against them, and ravaged their country, overrunning it and carrying off countless men, women, children, and cattle as booty” (Section 97). Yet the Bosnians persisted in their defiance.

The Campaign of 1463

Angered by the Bosnian intransigence, Mehmed II prepared a major campaign in the winter of 1462-63. He assembled vast numbers of cavalry and infantry, along with all kinds of cannons and siege weapons. At the opening of spring 1463, he marched from Edirne (Adrianople) with his entire army.

The Ottoman advance was swift. Mehmed “quickly traversed his own realm” and upon reaching the Bosnian border, launched a lightning campaign, “destroying and devastating everything with fire and sword, capturing fortresses and placing garrisons in some, taking booty, and conquering everything that lay in his path, without anyone offering resistance” (Section 99). Within a few days, the greater part of the country was overrun.

The Siege and Capture of Jajce

King Stephen Tomašević had fled to the royal fortress of Jajce (,,Jajce” in the local tongue), which Kritobulos describes as “a very strong place.” Mehmed arrived before Jajce, surrounded it entirely, and offered the citizens terms of surrender. When they refused, he “palisaded the city on all sides, surrounded it with his camp, set up the cannons, and besieged it” (Section 101).

The Ottoman artillery quickly breached the walls. Kritobulos records that “within a few days he had brought down and demolished a large part of the wall with the cannons” (Section 101). Seeing the breach and fearing a full assault, the citizens secretly offered to surrender the city — without their king’s knowledge.

King Stephen Tomašević, perceiving the impending betrayal, fled the city by night in concealment. However, “the camp guards, however, recognizing him, pursued him vigorously, and overtaking him, took him prisoner and brought him to the Emperor; and the Emperor had him put to death immediately” (Section 103). With the king executed, the citizens surrendered Jajce.

Mehmed treated the population graciously, returning the city to them on condition of annual tribute, and entered the city to inspect it personally. He recognized Jajce’s strategic value: “it has a favorable situation, being neighboring to the country of the Paeonians (Hungarians), and being able to accommodate sufficient troops, it could be of great harm to them” (Section 105). He placed a strong garrison in Jajce under the command of one of his relatives.

The Submission of Three Hundred Fortresses

After securing Jajce, Mehmed turned to the remainder of the kingdom. Kritobulos reports that he “captured nearly three hundred fortresses. Of their leaders, he captured four” (Section 105). The entire Kingdom of Bosnia was thus subjugated in a single campaigning season.

Having settled affairs in Bosnia — placing garrisons and commanders in Jajce and the other fortresses, leaving a capable governor with sufficient troops — Mehmed returned to Edirne toward the end of autumn 1463, bringing vast booty. The year 6971 (1463 AD), the thirteenth year of his reign, came to an end.

The Hungarian Counterattack and Fall of Jajce

The Hungarian King Matthias Corvinus (referred to by Kritobulos as the “king of the Paeonians and Dacians”) viewed the Ottoman occupation of Bosnia as a direct threat to his own realm. During the winter following Mehmed’s departure, Matthias marched into Bosnia to seize back the province, especially Jajce. As Kritobulos writes, “it seemed to him a terrible thing, and a great ruin and manifest dissolution of his own kingdom and realm, that the emperor should hold this province” (Section 15, Book V).

Matthias succeeded in causing the greater part of the fortresses to revolt from the Ottomans, “partly drawing them to himself by fair words and agreement, partly by intimidation and threats” (Section 16, Book V). Jajce itself fell to the Hungarians when “those within had risen up, were killing each other, and voluntarily joined him” (Section 16). Matthias placed strong garrisons of armed and warlike men in Jajce and the recovered fortresses before returning to Hungary.

The Second Siege of Jajce (1464)

Mehmed II, upon learning of the Hungarian counterattack, took the matter ill and immediately prepared for a second campaign. During the winter he gathered as large an army as possible, along with cannons, mortars, iron, and copper. At the coming of spring 1464, he marched from Edirne back into Bosnia, determined first to retake Jajce.

Mehmed pitched camp before Jajce and first attempted negotiations, offering oaths in exchange for surrender. When the defenders refused, he laid waste to the surrounding area, dug a ditch around the fortress, set up his cannons, and began the siege. Within a few days his artillery “broke down and demolished the stone wall with the cannons” (Section 20, Book V).

First Assault and Repulse

Judging the breach sufficient, Mehmed prepared a full assault. He stationed himself at the breach with his household troops — “the best heavy infantry, archers, slingers, and especially musketeers” (Section 21, Book V). At the given signal, the soldiers rushed the breach with immense clamor. The Hungarian defenders met them with equal ferocity.

Kritobulos provides a vivid account of the struggle: “sometimes the heavy infantry prevailing gained the upper hand over the Hungarians and climbed up onto the wall; sometimes, being thrust back by the Hungarians, they were violently driven out; and not a few perished there, fighting even as they fell” (Section 22, Book V). The fighting continued for a considerable time, but the Hungarians gained the upper hand. Seeing his men suffering heavy losses, including many noble and brave men from his own bodyguard, Mehmed gave the signal to withdraw.

Second Assault and Repulse

After a few days of rest, Mehmed ordered a second assault. He himself went in the foremost ranks, proclaiming “very many and very great rewards, honors and considerable favors for those who should first scale the walls” (Section 24, Book V), and granted all the possessions of the townspeople to his soldiers for plunder.

The soldiers attacked with terrible shouts, using scaling ladders, rope ladders, spikes driven into the wall, and every possible means to get in. The emperor was present, watching each man’s assault. The Hungarians, however, “stood against them very valiantly, with great courage, fervor and speed” (Section 26, Book V). The struggle became so fierce that Kritobulos says the men “almost casting off human nature, they hacked and hewed each other mercilessly” (Section 26, Book V).

Many of the imperial bodyguard fell, fighting in the emperor’s presence. The Hungarians, fighting from above with ample missiles, prevailed everywhere. Seeing his men falling in great numbers and that the city could only be taken by prolonged blockade and hunger, Mehmed again withdrew his army.

Mahmud Pasha’s Defeat of King Matthias Corvinus

While Mehmed maintained the siege, news arrived that King Matthias Corvinus had gathered a great army and was marching to relieve Jajce. Matthias planned either to draw the emperor into open battle or to split the Ottoman forces. Mehmed decided to remain before Jajce and continue the siege, sending his grand vizier Mahmud Pasha (,,Mahmut Pasha”) with sufficient cavalry and infantry, including a considerable portion of his own bodyguard, to meet the Hungarian king.

Mahmud advanced swiftly and encamped near the Hungarian army, with the river Erigon (called ,,Brunosz” or Crna in the local tongue) separating the two forces. Mahmud wished to cross and engage, but Mehmed sent word forbidding it, saying: “There is no need at all for it, when you now hold the crossing of the river in your hands; it is sufficient if you remain there and keep an eye on what the enemy is doing” (Section 33, Book V).

When Matthias learned that Mehmed would not raise the siege and that Mahmud lay in ambush with a large army, he despaired of relieving Jajce. He secretly sent word to the city to hold out, claiming the emperor would soon withdraw. However, Matthias himself, setting fire to his camp by night, decamped and withdrew with his army.

Perceiving the retreat, Mahmud crossed the river hastily and pursued with great speed. Overtaking the Hungarian rearguard, he routed it and pursued it as far as the main army. The rearguard’s sudden flight threw the entire Hungarian camp into confusion, and “there ensued a great flight, everyone all at once fleeing in disorder, without any order or consideration” (Section 36, Book V). Mahmud pressed the pursuit, “slaying and killing them mercilessly,” capturing all their military equipment, wagons, weapons, and horses. “Very many of the enemy fell; barely two hundred were taken alive into captivity” (Section 37, Book V).

Aftermath

After Mahmud’s victory, Mehmed made every attempt on Jajce by every means, but could accomplish nothing. He abandoned the attempt to take it by assault, struck camp, and turned against the other fortresses of Bosnia. Within a few days he subdued them: some he placed strong garrisons in, others he demolished. The men were put to death; women and children were led away into slavery. He also overran no small part of Hungary itself, gathering immense booty. He returned to Constantinople toward the end of summer 1464 and disbanded the army.

Jajce remained in Hungarian hands for several more decades, but the core of the Bosnian kingdom was permanently annexed to the Ottoman Empire. The conquest eliminated the last independent Christian kingdom in the western Balkans and brought the Ottoman frontier directly adjacent to Hungary, setting the stage for centuries of Ottoman-Hungarian conflict.
